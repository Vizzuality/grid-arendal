As can be seen from this graphic presentation, the natural greenhouse effect is caused almost entirely by water vapour in the atmosphere. Only about 0.1% of the greenhouse effect comes from human activities. Any factor which alters the radiation received from the sun or lost to space, or which alter the redistribution of energy within the atmosphere, and between the atmosphere, land and ocean, can affect the climate. A change in the energy available to the global Earth/atmosphere system is termed as radiative forcing. Any increase in the concentrations of greenhouse gases will reduce the efficiency with which the Earth cools to space, as more of the outgoing terrestrial radiation from the surface is absorbed by the atmosphere. This results in a positive radiation forcing which tends to warm the lower atmosphere and surface. This is the enhanced greenhouse effect - an enhancement of an effect which has operated in the atmosphere for billions of years due to the naturally occurring greenhouse gases: water vapour, carbon dioxide, ozone, methane and nitrous oxide. The amount of warming depends on the size of the increase in concentration of each greenhouse gas, the radiative properties of the gases involved, and the concentrations of other greenhouse gases already present in the atmosphere.
