Freshwater Facts

Wetlands are a "transition" zone between deep water and dry land. They're soaked all year long and for parts of the year they're completely covered by shallow water. There are four basic types of wetlands marshes, swamps, fens, and bogs.

MARSH

If you see plants with stems above the water's surface and roots down below, you've probably discovered a marsh. But you won't see trees - it's too wet for them to take root.

SWAMP

A swamp is a wooded wetland. Trees take root because there's less water than a marsh. If you walked through a swamp, you'd hear a squashing sound under your feet.

FEN

You'll recognize a fen by the tangle of grasses, sedges, and willows stretched out over it. You'll find fens in bays of lakes or basins where little water flows in and out.

BOG

A bog is covered by a carpet of moss, and only the hardiest of plants grow here. The water is filled with peat, so it's acidic. Fish can't survive in this type of environment.

RIVER

Rivers are natural transport systems, where water flows in a channel to another body of water. They move 1.5 trillion swimming pools of water to oceans each year!

LAKE

A lake is a large body of water surrounded by land. Lakes get water and nutrients from rivers and rain. Lakes have lots of nutrients and make a great home for animals.

POND

Ponds are formed when water gets trapped in natural hollows or by beaver dams. Ponds have lots of underwater plants, but they're hard to see in the deep, dark water.
