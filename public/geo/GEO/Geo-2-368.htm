Designing a Report on the State of the Nation's Ecosystems
Freshwater Work Group
Summary of the Third Meeting - June 26-27, 2000
at The Heinz Center
The third meeting of the Freshwater Work Group was held at The Heinz Center on June
26 and 27, 2000. The meeting was chaired by Gail Mallard and all but two Work Group
members were present. A full list of meeting attendees is attached (see Appendix II).
The meeting was opened with a presentation by Robin O'Malley of the implications of
the recent Design Committee meeting (May, 2000). Mr. O'Malley discussed how the
general report structure has changed significantly, including: (a) there will be a short 8-
page national overview component to the report, which will be a stand-alone document
and will contain roughly ten measures reported across all of the systems; (b) the Design
Committee has charged the Work Groups with using only data sets having essentially
national coverage; because many measures will not have appropriate data sets, the 2001
Report will probably contain a larger fraction of "blank boxes" (i.e., no appropriate data
set available) than the 1999 Report; and (c) the total number of measures available to
each Work Group has been steeply reduced as compared to the 1999 Report, with
accompanying requirements for the distribution of the measures within subcategories
(e.g., System Dimensions, Human Uses, etc.).
The Work Group then proceeded to re-evaluate their list of potential measures developed
during the March, 2000 meeting. This process was greatly aided by the input of the
group's informal panel of outside reviewers, termed the "kitchen cabinet". Because the
Group had settled on a list of 35 measures prior to this review process and because the
Design Committee has required each Work Group to use no more than 18 measures,
considerable reduction of the Group's list was necessary. Over the course of the meeting
the Group identified several issues and requests:
*
It would be very helpful to be able to look at both the National-level measures
and the measures proposed by the other Work Groups-even if these are in
the early stages of revision.
*
The Freshwater Group is out-of-phase with several of the other Work Groups
and may well have decided on many of its measures before two important
steps in the overall Report process have been completed: (a) the
development of national measures, and (b) meetings of Work Group
members from all systems that will be convened to develop themes that cut
across al of the six ecosystem types. Freshwater Group members could be
put in an awkward position as these discussions proceed.
In addition, the Work Group identified several points that should be raised with the
Design Committee:
Page 2
*
"Biogeochemistry", which tentatively is used to describe the group of
system properties including productivity, chemical and physical conditions,
and nutrients, does not adequately capture the physical characteristics of
Freshwater Systems.
*
An allocation of 6 measures to the Human Use category was seen as
inappropriate for freshwater systems. The group proposes reducing this
allocation to five.
*
Release of the Data Gaps report, which will be of much interest, is important
and should, therefore, be at or near the same time as the other portions of the
report.
*
Geomorphology/Hydrology should be taken up as a National-level measure.
Near the conclusion of the meeting, the Group had decided upon a primary list of
eighteen measures, with two extra measures that they did not want to drop from
consideration. Work Group members were asked to volunteer to provide a detailed write-
up for each of the twenty proposed measures by August 17 (see the Schedule and
Volunteer List below). After these write-ups are completed, Work Group members will
be asked to write similar statements about potential data sets. Because the Group had
slightly more measures than will likely be permitted by the Design Committee, members
were asked to select fifteen measures that she or he deems most important. This
prioritization process, which will be conducted by mail, may be helpful if the Group
needs to further reduce the number of measures.

Indicator Description Format
(Maximum length: 2-3 pages)
1. Measure title (less than a sentence)
2. Describe the measure. (1-2 sentences)
Describe succinctly and clearly exactly what will be reported
3. Tell why the measure is important. (1-6 paragraphs)
For measures of ecosystem condition (biogeochemistry, biodiversity): (a) describe the
scientific basis for your recommendation, and (b) use a conceptual model to describe
the choice. Look at the NRC report (Ecological Indicators for the Nation) for
examples of the type of description the Design Committee had in mind. For other
indicators, describe why the specific measure is proposed.
4. Describe the units of measure, scale of reporting, and data requirements. (1-4
paragraphs)
Describe how the measure is to be calculated: national or regional means, variances,
ranges, annual frequency of events, or by using specific subsets that are selected
because, taken together, they reveal trends that are occurring on regional or national
scales, i.e. the subset is a representative sample of the national population.
Describe the data needed ­ what variables must be measured, what time and space
scales are needed to resolve trends, what precision is needed for the measurements.
Describe how measures would be aggregated to national or regional scale.
5. Identify appropriate reference points. (1-2 paragraphs)
Describe additional data that should be provided to allow readers to place the
indicator data in context. Where possible, time series (e.g. comparison to previous
periods) should be provided, with data going back to 1950 if possible. Consider also
whether long-term reference points exist and are useful. Consider regulatory
standards, advisory standards, and the like from states, the US, or other countries.
Data sources for reference points, including long-term historical trends, must be of
"high quality" ­ that is, they will be reviewed for their scientific validity according to
the same standards as all other data sets.
6. Give an overall assessment of the measure's status. (1-2 paragraphs)
Does this measure have a strong conceptual basis? Has it been tested in a sufficient
variety of circumstances to assure its reliability, reproducibility, interpretability, and
relevance? Should it be considered "established" or "experimental"? Rate the
measure according to the following scale and describe your rationale for doing so.
Page 10
a) Well established measure. Generally accepted by the relevant community. Tested in a
wide variety of settings. Not likely to require additional development or testing.
b) Conditional measure. Even though a fairly new measure, it has been applied or tested
across a relatively broad set of conditions. May require additional fine-tuning,
regional targeting, etc.
c) Experimental measure. Strong conceptual foundation, but relatively new concept or
approach, with testing or application in a limited number of settings. May be the
subject of continued debate and/or development.
7. Describe possible data sets that might be used, arraying them according to the scale
below.
a) National or large regional data set or sufficient sampling to capture variation in
relevant features, of known quality.
b) Existing, good quality data that needs to be assimilated into a national data set, OR
data available on a regional but not national basis.
c) Some data available, but unknown quality
d) Data not collected, anecdotal, or only from a specific site

Data Requirements Format
Once completed, this statement about each data set will be used directly in the
text accompanying graphics as well as in the technical notes section of the Report.
1. Raw Data Collection Methodology
a) List the agency or other entity that was directly responsible for the collection
of the raw data.
b) Describe the means by which the raw data were collected. Specifically, were
the raw data, for example, remotely sensed via satellite, or were they the
product of a nation-wide sampling program where individual water
samples were collected and analyzed? Be as specific as possible when
describing an analytical method (e.g., "water samples were analyzed for
nitrate by ion chromatography", instead of "water samples were analyzed
for nitrate").
c) Has the methodology remained constant over the entire chronology of the data
set? If not, comment on the impact of changing methodologies on the
overall data set.
2. Data Transformation Methodology
a) Describe the steps required to transform the raw data into what will be
presented as a graphic in the Report. For example, "...the SeaWiFS
protocol (Hooker et al. 1992) was used to convert the water-leaving
radiance into an estimate of the phytoplankton biomass (i.e., chlorophyll a
concentration)."
b) Has the agency listed in 1.a. remained in charge of the data from collection
through transformation?
3. Statistical Quality of Data
Given that few, if any, graphic presentations will include an indication of the
uncertainty of the data values (e.g., error bars) in order to achieve an easily assimilable
document, at a minimum the technical notes should provide a statement of this
uncertainty. Therefore, provide a description of the error analysis that has been
performed for the data set. For example, "...nitrate levels reported are

"...the best estimate of the uncertainty for a given SeaWiFS chlorophyll

4. Spatial Coverage
In order to be presented in the 2001 Report, a data set must convey a national, rather
than local, image of a measure. That is, data should be available on a national or large
regional basis (e.g., more than a few states), or they should be based on sampling
methods that are sufficient to capture the range of variation of the feature of interest.
Describe the extent of coverage for the data set.

5. Temporal Coverage
There are two aspects of temporal coverage that are important in the context of
this report. The first deals with the historical perspective that can be gained by using the
data set. The second has to do with the frequency at which the data set is updated. This
latter point is important because if the Report comes out every 5 years, yet the data set is
updated only every 10 years, then the utility of the Report will be lessened because
multiple editions of the Report will contain the same data.
a) What is the period over which data are available from this source?
Specifically, are they available from the period of 1950-present? (Note:
this is by no means a requirement for inclusion in the Report.)
b) How frequently is the data updated (e.g., annually, every 5 years, etc.)? If the
same update frequency has not been maintained over the history of the
data collection program, indicate how it has changed with time.
6. Data Quality (peer-review status)
One of the Report's underlying themes is that data must be of "high quality".
Therefore, describe the "pedigree" of the data set. That is, have the methods for
collecting and transforming the data been fully peer-reviewed (if not by external
reviewers, then at least by intra-agency review panels)?
7. Data Accessibility
Data must be readily available in order to contribute to a timely release of the 2001
Report.a) Is the data freely available on the web? If so, provide the URL.
b) If the data can only be accessed for a price, what is this (keeping in mind that
the data will be used not by an individual, but rather in a widely-
distributed report)?

accessing them? In what forms are the data available electronically?
