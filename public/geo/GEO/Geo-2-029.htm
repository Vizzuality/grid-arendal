Canada signs international agreement on acid rain and smog reductions

Ottawa – December 3, 1999 – Canada signed an international agreement with 25 countries today that will help address air quality and acid rain. The Protocol to Abate Acidification, Eutrophication and Ground-level Ozone (AEGLO) was signed in Gothenburg, Sweden, during a meeting of the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UN ECE) that also marked the 20th anniversary of the UN ECE Convention on Long Range Transboundary Air Pollution (LRTAP).

Canada has been strongly committed to the LRTAP process from its inception. Under the AEGLO Protocol, there are provisions for setting goals to reduce transboundary emissions of air pollutants that contribute to acid rain and ground-level ozone, two of Canada's priority air issues.

"The transboundary movement of air pollution poses a threat to the health and security of people, in Canada and around the world," said Foreign Affairs Minister Lloyd Axworthy. "International agreements, such as this Protocol, help address transboundary threats to human security, and benefit the health of Canadians. They also move us closer to sustainable development."

"Science has shown us quite clearly that air pollutants cross boundaries," said Environment Minister David Anderson. "For Canada, this Protocol constitutes one more step toward reductions in transborder flows across our border with the United States of the pollutants that cause acid rain and ground-level ozone - a major component of smog."

Acid rain continues to be a problem in eastern Canada and ground-level ozone, a major component of smog, is of particular concern in southeastern Canada and southern British Columbia. Canadian emissions are a major factor, but U.S. sources account for about 50 per cent of the acid rain problem in eastern Canada and between 35 per cent and 90 per cent of the ground-level ozone problem in southeastern Canada.

The Protocol covers Canada, the United States and the eastern and western regions of Europe. The unique aspect of the Protocol is that it separately treats the commitments from one region to another. It also allows Canada to bring emission commitments to the Protocol upon ratification, once domestic mechanisms are in place that are tailored to Canada's specific needs and that will ensure compliance with the Protocol obligations.

"The initiative is part of the Government of Canada's commitment to strengthen environmental protection, protect human health, and ensure that we maintain and enhance the quality of life for all Canadians as outlined in the 1999 Speech from the Throne," said Minister Anderson.

The Protocol was signed on behalf of Canada by Ambassador to Sweden, Christopher Westdal.
