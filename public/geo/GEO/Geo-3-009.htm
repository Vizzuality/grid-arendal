Four cheap methods to free water from arsenic 

Staff Reporter


A system to make drinking water Arsenic free. This system was shown at the International Workshop on Arsenic at BUET on Saturday:New Nation photo.

Eight crore people living in as many as 59, out of the country's 64 districts have been facing serious health risks due to drinking of arsenic-contaminated water.

This was disclosed by the experts at an international workshop on "Technologies for Arsenic Removal from Drinking Water" held at Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology (BUET) in the city on Saturday.

"Intake of arsenic associated with food is a common phenomenon, but this arsenic is the low-toxicity organic arsenic. But drinking water derived from underground source contains arsenic in inorganic form, which is of higher toxicity and a significant hazard for human health. Prolonged use of arsenic contaminated water for drinking purpose is unsafe and causes serious diseases like skin cancer and a number of internal cancers, among others. Cardiovascular and neurological diseases have also been found to be linked to arsenic contamination," the experts at the workshop said.

The experts also suggested four different approaches to remove or reduce arsenic from tubewell water, saying that the removal of arsenic from drinking water was possible by passing the water at controlled flow rate through wood-charcoal, treating the water with calcium oxide, by sedimentation method and by repeated removal of the top surface layer containing high level of arsenic.

"These four methods may be adopted as cheap and simple technologies for removal of arsenic from drinking water," they said.

These four different approaches were tested for removal or reduction of arsenic level from tubewell water at Goneshampur village in Mymensingh sadar upazila between November 2000 and March 2001. Arsenic concentrations were determined by an arsenic kit provided by NIPSOM.

Organised by the BUET in collaboration with the United Nations University of Tokyo, the three-day workshop was inaugurated by Deputy Minister for Local Government, LGRD and Cooperatives Saber Hossain Chowdhury while BUET Vice-Chancellor Professor Nooruddin Ahmed chaired the function.

Professor Dr M Feroze Ahmed of the Department of Civil Engineering, BUET, Dr Zafar Adeel of the Environment and Sustainable Development Programme of the UNU, Japan and Professor Dr M Mujibur Rahman of BUET also spoke on the occasion.

Researchers, academicians and entrepreneurs working on arsenic removal technologies, representatives from different international organisations and professionals with practical work experiences in arsenic contaminated areas in Bangladesh are participating in this specialised workshop to discuss the physical, physico-chemical and biological processes in removal of arsenic from the arsenic contaminated waters.

Some 21 papers will be presented in four technical sessions of the workshop, with nearly 125 local and foreign experts from the USA, the UK, Japan, Australia, the Netherlands, Canada, Nepal and India taking part in the workshop.

The experts noted that the number of people drinking arsenic contaminated water has increased over last 25 years due to well drilling and population growth in Bangladesh. And the number of affected persons may, therefore, increase further.

Many programmes on arsenic detection and creation of awareness have so far been completed and some are being implemented. "But very few efforts have been undertaken for the removal of arsenic from tubewell water except to abandon the arsenic affected tubewells", they said.

Speaking on the occasion, Saber Hossain Chowdhury described arsenic contamination of water as a major problem facing by the country.

The Minister called for a collective effort of the private sector, NGOs side by side the government to face the arsenic contamination of ground water.

He also suggested for raising awareness among the rural households about the arsenic problem.

Saber also urged the scientists and researchers to find out proper technological options for mitigating arsenic contamination in the country.

Vice-Chancellor Professor Dr Nooruddin Ahmed warned that arsenic in drinking water was not desirable at any concentration.

Dr Zafar Adeel said the arsenic contamination in Bangladesh had reached at a quite unprecedented scale.

Arsenic level in ground water in 47 dists crosses WHO limit, says study

 

A group of scientists in Jadavpur University in Kolkata have observed that arsenic level in groundwater in 47 districts in Bangladesh and nine district s in Indian West Bengal states have already crossed the World Health Organisation (WHO) maximum permissible limit of 50 micro-gram per litre, reports BSS.

The group, led by head of the School of Environmental Studies (SOES) of Jadavpur University, Dipankar Chakraborti, said the world's four biggest sites of groundwater contamination are Bangladesh, West Bengal of India, Inner Mongolia of the People's Republic of China and Taiwan. They said huge withdrawal of groundwater is taking place because of agricultural irrigation.

"We have made a green revolution at the cost of underground water," a study of the SOES observed.

Exploring the state of the diffusion of available techniques for removal of arsenic from groundwater, the SOES scientists noted that in the villages of India and Bangladesh even a highly successful technology may not succeed unless it fits in the rural context and is accepted by the rural people.

"Development of such technology is only possible when a combination is made between technocrats and villagers with proper village level participation," the scientists said.

They said appropriate arsenic removal technologies should be economically viable and socially acceptable.

The researchers said the processes used in the arsenic-affected areas of Chile and Taiwan at full scale treatment plant is coagulation. But the University of Connecticut (patent pending) has claimed a novel and cost effective Arsenic Remedy Technology (AsRT) for the immobilisation of inorganic arsenic such as arsenates and arsenites.

Quoting field survey reports, they said, in some arsenic-affected villages many families due to high iron content in their tubewell water cannot drink the water directly. To make it drinkable, they just keep the water after withdrawal from tubewell for some time, when a brown precipitate settles at the bottom and the users decant the upper clear water or use a common filter to arrest the flock.

"It is a burning question how and where to dispose of arsenic rich flock. Many thinks that arsenic rich flock if disposed of on soil, may contaminate the aquifer," the scientists said adding that in the laboratory (of SOES) we found that cow-dung can eliminate arsenic in volatile form.

Referring to some tests, the SOES scientists said arsenic removal system of Jadavpur University was proved to be 93 per cent to 98.5 per cent effective according to official test results of Government Industrial Toxicological Research, Lucknow, India, National Test House, India, CSIR institute at Nagpur India as well as independent research conducted by Asia Arsenic Network (AAN), Japan and Thailand bureau of the Asia Arsenic Network.

The AAN (Japan) has purchased 300 units of arsenic removal system from CSIR (Council of Scientific and industrial Research) New Delhi, India and installed them in the arsenic-affected areas across Bangladesh, experts in the SOES said.

The SOES researchers opined that the actual solution of groundwater arsenic contamination in Bangladesh and India would be achieved through proper watershed management utilising the available surface water.

Arsenic the silent killer

by Nasrine R. Karim

The 20th century saw many catastrophes among which Chernobyl and the chemical disaster in Bhobal are worth mentioning. But Arsenic poisoning in Bangladesh is overshadowing them all as over 75 million people are at potential risk and thousands have reached the point of no return.

Ironically, the drinking water crisis in Bangladesh is the result of safe water drinking programme spearheaded by UNICEF. The villagers were weaned away from the use of surface water for drinking and cooking as surface water because surface water was declared the source of deadly microbes and bacteria that can cause to life threatening pathogenic diseases. UNICEF assisted the Government to sink pipes into underground aquifers for clean groundwater. Presently, out of 5 million such tube-wells, 3 million may be contaminated with arsenic.

The region’s high level of naturally occurring arsenic went undiscovered until the 1990s. So far, action to address the problem has been very slow and arduous. World Bank and the Government have jointly set up a specialized unit at a cost of about $50 million under the Local Government and Rural Development Ministry - Bangladesh Arsenic Mitigation and Water Sanitation Project (BAMWSP). There is also, apparently, a government task force to oversee the developments in the sector.

With the donors insisting that assessment of technologies be made by a third party for transparency, WaterAid/DFID funded the rapid assessment of household Level arsenic removal technologies. Out of original twenty-one technologies, seven were found acceptable to be used for the masses with some reservations. Finally, only four were given certification by TAG/BAMWSP. TAG is the Technical Advisory Group who is in charge of vetting all technologies to be used by the public in Bangladesh.

Arsenic is a metal. Unlike iron, arsenic has no colour, taste or smell. This is a silent killer. One cannot get rid of it by boiling as we do in case of microbe-contaminated water. Only chemicals can effectively remove arsenic as other metals like iron or mercury. Arsenic also moves within the soil. So if one finds one tube-well free from arsenic today, it may not be so tomorrow.

Arsenic is absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract into the blood stream. It is still not understood what is the mechanism through which arsenic causes cancer. However, the NRC noted that data suggest that arsenic may cause chromosomal abnormalities that lead to cancer. Sensitivity to arsenic effects, including carcinogenic effects, seems to vary with individuals and appear to be influenced by factors such as nutrition and genetic susceptibility. However, in Bangladesh the problem is magnified dramatically due to thousands suffering from skin lesions, tumours, lung deficiencies and still more endured amputations from gangrene and other drastic procedures to ward off death.

Today over 20 to 30 million people are affected, tomorrow a further 75 million may be affected. What steps are being taken to combat this enormous catastrophe of the 21st century? BAMWSP, under the guidance of the ministry must develop a schedule, or a protocol or a sustainable strategy to bring effective relief to the helpless people of Bangladesh without any further delay. Funds are available with donors.

BAMWSP has access to millions of dollars, yet why has not the mitigation works started?

It is the democratic right of the people of Bangladesh to have access to safe drinking water. The authorities must take this up as a national priority. Poverty, fiscal and economic alleviation, education and future building are of no value, without clean water.

Source: The Independent,14 May,2001
