SERIOUS SALINITY WARNING MUST BE HEEDED - TUCKEY

Australia is facing massive economic, environmental and social challenges due to the widespread and growing phenomenon of dryland salinity, a National Science Briefing in Parliament House, Canberra was told today.

The National Science Briefing forum on salinity was chaired by the Federal Minister for Forestry and Conservation, Wilson Tuckey. The Minister introduced speakers, including Mr Alex Campbell, Chair of the National Dryland Salinity Program (NDSP), Dr Tom Hatton from CSIRO Land and Water Division and Mrs Doreen Wheelwright , Deputy Chair of the Lachlan Catchment Committee.

"With the WA wheatbelt in my electorate of O'Connor, I certainly appreciate the seriousness of salinity and the information provided at today's briefing is a further wake up call for all levels of government," Mr Tuckey said. "We need to work with farmers and rural communities to find a solution to the plague of salinity."

Dr Tom Hatton of CSIRO Land and Water said: "The costs associated with meeting these challenges are high, the costs of doing nothing incalculable.

"Australia currently has 25 million square kilometres of salt affected land and this is likely to increase six fold in the coming decades," Dr Hatton said. "Western Australia, one of the worst affected states, has 18 million square kilometres of salt affected land and this is increasing at a rate equal to one football field an hour.

"Lost agricultural production exceeds $130 million annually; costs to infrastructure are over $100 million a year; lost water resources are valued at around $100 million each in some local supply catchments and the impacts on biodiversity are huge and include wetlands of international significance - all attributable to salinity."

Currently of great concern is Western Australia’s Fitzgerald River area.

"The area is recognised internationally as ‘megadiverse’. Surveys under way indicate the region possesses an extraordinary number of plants and animals – most locally endemic to WA. Salinity now places many of these species in threat of extinction," Dr Hatton said.

Clean drinking water is also at stake.

"In South Australia, at least 20 per cent of surface water resources are sufficiently saline to be above desirable limits for human consumption. Next century, these limits will be pushed further and for longer periods," he said.

"To achieve significant improvement of the salinity problem, intervention on a massive scale is required.

"Research has shown that marginal intervention returns less than marginal salinity abatement. The large-scale salinisation processes we have set in train are insensitive to all but the most aggressive remediation.

"For example, tree planting is needed across most of the landscape, including 50-70 per cent of catchment areas, to achieve significant reductions in the ultimate extent of the salinity."

Dr Hatton emphasises; however, that the response times of salinity control will be long and it is unlikely these systems will be completely restored within normal human timescales.

Mr Alex Campbell, Chairman of the National Dryland Salinity Program, told the Briefing that more than 80 regional towns and cities have costs related to salinity – and even Sydney is being affected. The costs include damage to building foundations, bridges, pipelines and roads.

"Up to 30 per cent of regional roads are being affected with major highway reconstruction costing up to $1 million per kilometre," he said.

Mr Campbell stressed that unprecedented changes in management practices are needed.

"We also need to explore the broader options of saline aquaculture and growing various salt tolerant pastures, crops and woody perennials," he said. "With effective research and management, we could use salinised resources beneficially for both economic and environmental outcomes."

Mrs Doreen Wheelwright, Deputy Chair of the Lachlan Catchment Committee, told the Briefing that the processes involved in managing salinity are still insufficiently clear to allow definitive management options to be given in all cases.

"There is no single answer and a mix of management options is necessary," she said.

She called for increased funding to continue research into the cause and effect of salinity and to develop appropriate management tools.
