Preliminary1 Indicator: How Canadians Travel


In 1995, for every 100 kilometres individuals travelled, 81 kilometres were by automobile. Travel by automobile increased by 15% between 1990 and 1995, as did total travel.

Air travel was more than 90 times higher in 1995 than in 1950, reflecting a continuing displacement of rail travel and growth in international travel. Air travel increased 10% since 1994.




Total travel within, and originating from, Canada increased more than five times between 1950 and 1995, even though Canada’s population only doubled during that period. Automobile travel has grown steadily, except for a small but notable decline between 1980 and 1982, reflecting slow growth in the economy (indicated here by gross domestic product, or GDP).

Travel by automobile and plane is much greater and is growing faster than other modes. Automobiles and planes also use more fuel and emit more carbon dioxide per passenger for a given distance of travel than do trains and buses.2

Notes:
* A “passenger-kilometre” is a standard unit for measuring travel that takes into account both the number of people travelling and the distance travelled. For example, 200 passenger-kilometres of travel is equivalent to 10 people each travelling 20 kilometres or to 1 person travelling 200 kilometres.
** “Automobile” refers to cars and other private-use passenger vehicles, such as vans and small trucks.
*** “Bus” includes inter-city and urban bus plus other forms of urban transit (light rail, subway, etc.).

Sources:
Royal Commission on National Passenger Transportation. 1992. Directions: The Final Report of the Royal Commission on National Passenger Transportation. Volume 2, Chapter 2.
Canadian Urban Transit Association, Toronto, Canada.
Statistics Canada, Ottawa, Canada.
Natural Resources Canada, Ottawa, Canada.

1 Available transportation data, especially occupancy rates, are not sufficiently detailed to provide direct measures of on-road passenger transportation; thus, estimation methods were used, and the indicator has been designated “preliminary.” Please see SOE Technical Supplement No. 98-5 for details.
2 See SOE Technical Supplement No. 98-5 for estimation.
