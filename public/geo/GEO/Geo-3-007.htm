EXTENDED ABSTRACT

1 Introduction

Urban air fine particulates (PM2.5) are associated with excess mortality and morbidity (e.g. Dockery et al. 1993, Katsouyanni et al. 1997, Woodruff et al. 1997). The risk is transmitted via exposure to PM2.5, and risk reduction requires reducing this exposure. Because airborne PM2.5 is produced by many human activities and natural phenomena, chemical analyses of the PM2.5 and source apportionment of the exposures is essential for developing effective clean air strategies (Kaiser J 1997; WHO Guidelines 1997).

There are studies on elemental analyses of urban PM samples (e.g. Öblad & Selin 1986, Raunemaa 1989, Cahill et al. 1990, Clayton et al. 1993, and Cahill et al. 1996, Özkaynak et al. 1996), but European data are sparse, and the samples have not represented population exposures. In the EXPOLIS study the exposures to PM2.5 of a representative population sample were measured in Helsinki (and 5 other European cities). Population exposure distributions and life style, commuting and time activity related exposure determinants have been analyzed from the EXPOLIS data (Koistinen et al. 2000, Rotko et al. 2000), but from this data alone the exposures cannot be attributed to, only associated with sources. EAS-EXPOLIS addresses this question by analyzing the black smoke (BS) and elemental composition of the PM2.5 aerosol samples already collected in the EXPOLIS study and applying source apportionment techniques on the elemental composition data.

Objectives of EAS-EXPOLIS are to expand the EXPOLIS database with:
-  microenvironmental and personal exposure distributions of PM2.5 bound toxic and other elements, to be used for
- source apportionment of the microenvironmental PM2.5 concentrations and personal PM2.5 exposures, which enables
-  simulation/modelling of source specific PM2.5 exposures for urban populations in different exposure scenarios

2 Methods

2.1 Sampling
201 subjects participated in personal exposure monitoring and micro-environmental measurements (Jantunen et al. 1998 and 1999, Koistinen et al. 1999, Rotko et al. 2000). Personal exposures and micro-environmental concentrations of PM2.5, NO2, CO and 30 VOCs were sampled. This information was completed with questionnaire and time-microenvironment-activity-diary (TMAD) data.

In EAS-EXPOLIS, elemental analyses were done on PM2.5 samples from subjects with valid measurements from day- and nighttime personal exposures, and workplace, home indoor and outdoor microenvironmental concentrations.

2.2 Elemental Analyses
In 1999 the PM2.5 samples were analysed in the University of Basel, Geochemical Laboratory, by Prof. W.B. Stern with energy dispersive X-ray fluorescent spectrometry (ED-XRF). Quality assurance of the elemental analyses included repeated measures of the same filter, analyses of simultaneously collected particles (parallel measurements) and analyses of non-exposed blank filters. Half of the detection limit value (per element) were used for the samples below the detection limit.
2.3 Data Analyses
PM2.5 elemental data were combined into EXPOLIS database. SPSS for windows 9.0 was used for principal component analyses (PCA, Varimax rotation) of ln transformed concentration data. Elements detected in less than 20 % of the samples were not included and absolute coefficients below 0.40 were omitted from tables to facilitate identification of sources.

3 Results and Discussion

37 elements (Ag Al As Ba Br Ca Cd Cl Co Cr Cu Fe Ga Ge Hg I K Mg Mn Na Ni Pb Rb S Sb Sm Sr Ti Tl Tm V Zn and Zr) were analysed by ED-XRF. Co, Cr, Ge, Sb, Tl, Tm and Zr were also infrequently detected to be used in further analyses. There are also results, which are either interesting or erroneous, namely the high frequency of Ba, Cd, Hg I, and Mn in personal samples but virtual absence in microenvironmental samples, and the high frequencies of Fe, Ga and Sr in microenvironmental samples but virtual absence in personal samples. All these results must be viewed as preliminary.

Table 1. shows PCA of the elemental composition of the PM2.5 exposure samples from non-ETS exposed subjects. It identified 7 factors accounting for 70 % of the variance, the first factor accounting for 18.9 %.  The first factor comprising Al, Ca and Si is related to wind-blown soil particles, as these elements are characteristic tracers for the high silica soil of Helsinki.

Zn was associated with the second factor, accounting for 12 % of the variance, along with Cu, Mn and S. This factor probably represents long-range transport, as sulfate marks typically secondary particles generated in the atmosphere. There is a clear directional dependency of factor scores on winds that come predominantly from the east and southeast. One plausible source of these long-range transport pollutants is an oil shale-fired power plants in Narva, Estonia, southeast of Helsinki.

The third factor accounts for another 12 % of the variance, and is composed of Br, Pb, Rb and Se.  Br is a marker for traffic emission. Although leaded gasoline was already fairly rare in Helsinki in 1996-7, Pb was still correlated with (Ojanen et al. 1998) and was still present in automobile emissions in Finland (Ålander et al.1998). As other sources of Pb and Br in Helsinki are negligible this factor identifies the exposure to traffic emissions.

The fourth factor was composed of K, Na and Cl. This is street deicing salt. The remaining three factors probably result from other industrial emissions. Confirmation of these sources will require linkage to wind vectors, and analysis individual exposures.

Auto exhaust particles range 10-20 nm for gasoline and 60-70 nm for diesel (Ålander et al. 1998). Median diameters for secondary sulfate particles in Leeds were 1.1 µm (APEG 1999). Windblown dust particles are in the coarse fraction above 1 µm. Although long-range transport dominates in the outdoor environment followed by soil and then traffic, smaller particles dominate indoors, as they penetrate more readily into buildings.  Residential indoor environments are dominated by traffic followed by long-range transport, soil and road salt. Workplace environments are dominated by an ‘industrial’ factor accounting for (37%) variability. In personal exposures this is factor 4 accounting for 9% of the variance.

Personal exposure levels are higher than microenvironment levels for all elements. Personal exposures are not adequately represented by any one microenvironment, and that estimating exposures based on data collected in one microenvironment will give a distorted picture

4 Conclusions

For participants of EXPOLIS-Helsinki not exposed to tobacco smoke:
- Personal exposures of PM2.5 were higher than residential outdoor, residential indoor and workplace microenvironment.
- Traffic related emissions dominated PM2.5 in the indoor environment.
- Source contributions to PM2.5 personal exposure samples were not adequately represented by any one microenvironment, demonstrating the need for personal exposure sampling.
