The National Taskforce on the Prevention and Management of Marine Pest Incursions

  • Recommendations
  • Full Report

Recommendations

The National Taskforce on the Prevention and Management of Marine Pest Incursions (The Taskforce), submitted its report to the Australian and New Zealand Environment and Conservation Council (ANZECC) and the Ministerial Council on Forestry, Fisheries and Aquaculture (MCFFA) on 24 December 1999. The establishment of the Taskforce by the Councils was in part a response to the serious outbreak of the black striped mussel in Darwin during April 1999 and fulfilled a committment under Australia's Oceans Policy.

The Taskforce's report proposes a National System for the Prevention and Management of Introduced Marine Pests. The National System is being coordinated by a single body, the National Introduced Marine Pests Coordination Group (NIMPCG), that reports to all relevant Commonwealth and State Ministers. NIMPCG is chaired by Agriculture Forestry and Fisheries - Australia.

The Taskforce's report details recommendations for interim and longer-term action to establish the three main components of the National System.

First, for the prevention of new marine pest incursions and translocations. The report confirms the national leadership role in this area for the Australian Quarantine and Inspection Service (AQIS). The further development and implementation of AQIS's draft Action Plan for Minimising the Risks to Australia from the Introduction and Translocation of Marine Pests by Vessels has been identified by the Taskforce as an essential part of the proposed National System. All other vectors will also be addressed by the National System.

Second, for coordinating emergency response actions to new marine pest incursions and outbreaks. The report proposes a national agency leadership role for the National Office of Animal and Plant Health, at Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry - Australia (AFFA), in coordinating national responses to incursions and outbreaks. The report includes a detailed Australian Emergency Marine Pest Plan that provides practical guidance on conducting a staged response to an introduced marine pest emergency. The Taskforce's report also proposes an initial list of marine species that would automatically trigger a report by any jurisdiction to the national emergency response coordination body for advice and action.

Third, for undertaking longer term mitigation and control measures to combat established marine pests. The Taskforce's report proposes that the Commonwealth Government explore the option of developing statutory plans to reduce, eliminate or prevent the impacts of introduced marine species on the biodiversity of Australia using provisions of Environment Australia's Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999.

In addition to these three main components, the Taskforce's 57 recommendations include comprehensive proposals for action on a wide range of crosscutting issues essential to support marine pest prevention and management.
