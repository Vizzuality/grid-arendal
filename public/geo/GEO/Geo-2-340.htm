World Bank Board Approves $72 million for Kenya


NAIROBI, October 26, 2000—The World Bank has approved a US$72 million credit to assist Kenya in alleviating severe power shortages that have devastated its economy. The Kenya Emergency Power Supply Project will support contracts for supply of 105 MW of emergency power which is needed to bridge a prolonged shortfall in energy supply from conventional sources. The credit will be used to finance electric power purchases from three independent power producers who have been contracted by the Government of Kenya. It will also finance the fuel costs of these emergency plants and facilitate supply of incremental fuel to Kenya Electricity Generating Company's thermal power generation plants.

The Emergency Power Supply Credit is the third credit for Kenya to be approved by the World Bank during the current fiscal year. Other projects approved are the US$150 million Economic Public Sector Reform Credit to help improve economic growth and reduce poverty and the US$50 million credit for a Multi-sectoral HIV/AIDS Disaster Response Project.

The Emergency Power Supply Credit will benefit Kenyans by restoring economic activity, job levels and incomes, and also facilitate measures that will improve the overall efficiency of the energy sector to stop recurrence of an energy crisis similar to the one the country has experienced in recent years.

The credit was considered on an exceptional basis after the Government appealed to the World Bank, the IMF and other donors to assist Kenya in addressing the impact of prolonged drought on the economy. Kenya has experienced severe drought for over two years due to failure and poor distribution of rains in the main water catchment areas, which are also the source of water flows to hydro power generating facilities. This has necessitated severe power rationing to industrial and domestic consumers. An assessment by the Government and the donors estimates the economic losses arising from power rationing to US$2 million a day. It is also estimated that by the onset of long rains in April next year, the economic costs of unmet electricity demand will amount to US$400-630 million, equivalent to 3.8-6.5 percent of GDP. Restoring power supplies to near normal levels is therefore critical to reversing the decline in the economy, which has resulted in substantial job and income losses that have particularly hurt the poor sections of the population.

In approving the credit, the Board considered the effort the Government is making, under a difficult economic environment, towards reforms in key areas, including the energy sector. Last week, the Government issued a public statement on the restructuring of Kenya Power and Lighting Company (KPLC) as part of the continuing overall restructuring of the energy sector under the Bank-funded Energy Sector Reform and Power Development Project which started three years ago. The statement addressed concerns about the pace of reforms in the sector and commitments to increased efficiency that have been raised during the term of the project. The Government has established a broad based Emergency Electricity Coordination Committee (EECC) to initiate and oversee the restructuring process of improving the operations and financial management of KPLC.

In a remarkable managerial effort, the emergency power generating capacity to be financed by the credit has already been installed, and is making major contributions to the reduction of power shortages already.

The project will be financed by a US$72 million credit from the International Development Association (IDA), the World Bank's lending arm for the poorest countries. This IDA credit is on standard terms of 40 years maturity, including 10 years grace.
