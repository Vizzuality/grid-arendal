Producer Responsibility - An Overview

Introduction


At the end of 1994 member countries of the European Commission reached agreement on a Directive to minimise waste by reducing the amount of packaging used and entering the waste stream. The Directive, known as the European Directive on Packaging and Packaging Waste (PWD), set Europe-wide recovery and recycling targets to be met by the year 2001. The Directive covered all kinds of packaging materials, including aluminium, glass, paper, board, plastic and steel. Wood and other materials will be included from 2000.

The PWD’s definition of packaging included any material used for containment, protection, handling, delivery or presentation of goods, and was divided into three categories:

primary - packaging around individual items at point of sale;

secondary - packaging surrounding groups of products for storage or for display at point of sale;

tertiary - packaging for transport purposes.

The Directive decreed that between 50 - 65 per cent of packaging waste by weight must be recovered by the year 2001. Of this a minimum of 25% overall and 15% of each material must be recycled.

Each EU member country has been responsible for devising its own national strategy for meeting the targets required by the Directive. In Britain this has taken the form of the Producer Responsibility Obligations (Packaging Waste) Regulations.

 

Shared Producer Responsibility (SPR)


The UK Government preferred a purely voluntary approach to waste recovery and recycling, but industry was concerned that some people would avoid their obligations. It believed underpinning legislation would be necessary. The concept of Shared Producer Responsibility (SPR) evolved in 1995, and in 1996 the Government published its proposals requiring all businesses in the packaging chain to meet recovery and recycling targets for packaging waste.

 

Targets and those affected by the Regulations


Manufacturers of packaging raw material, converters, packers/fillers, retailers/distributors/sellers, are affected if they use/handle more than 50 tonnes of packaging a year, and have an annual turnover of more than £5m. (The turnover threshold was to be reduced to £1m in the year 2000.) It is estimated by the DETR that 5,935 companies are currently obligated under these Regulations. The government decided that by the year 2001 52% of all packaging should be recovered and 26% recycled with a 16% minimum target for all individual packaging materials. The balance of the recycling target can be met in any material.

What do businesses have to do?


Obligated businesses have 3 options:

  1. Comply with the Regulations on an individual basis;
  2. Join a collective compliance scheme;
  3. Start a compliance scheme.

Option 1 and 3 require businesses to register with either The Environmental Agency in England and Wales or the Scottish Environmental Protection Agency (SEPA) in Scotland as the Agencies responsible for enforcing the Regulations. The Agencies have published statutory guidance on how businesses will have to demonstrate compliance. If a company elects to join a collective compliance scheme, the scheme then becomes responsible for discharging the company’s obligations.

Whatever choice a company makes recovery and recycling obligations still have to be determined on an individual basis. The calculations are complex and, in part, depend on the activities undertaken by the company in relation to the packaging/packaging material it handles (Figure 1).

In the case of imported materials and/or packaging, the importer acquires the "rolled up" obligations (eg obligation for the packaging chain activities that have taken place outside the UK). For example, a packer/filler importing empty bottles will, in addition to the obligation he has by virtue of the activity he undertakes, also incur the raw material and converting obligations which were carried out before the bottles reached the UK. He also has an obligation on the transit packaging used while transferring the bottles to the UK.

Obligated parties are able to exclude any packaging and / or packaging materials which they export, or which are subsequently exported by their customers or others further down the chain, but they have to be able to provide evidence that such exports actually took place. Reusable packaging is counted only on the first occasion that it is used; packaging made of composite materials is counted as being of the material which provides the greatest proportion of its overall weight.

Failure to comply is a criminal offence and may result in a substantial fine.

The UK Paper Industry Compliance Scheme


Paperpak is a compliance scheme developed by the paper and board industry for producers whose obligations under the Regulations arise principally from their handling of paper packaging and paper packaging materials.

Paperpak Limited, a company limited by guarantee, which comprises a wide range of paper packaging industry associations, including:

  • The British Printing Industries Federation (BPIF) (incorporating the British Carton Association)
  • The British Recovered Paper Association (BRPA)
  • The Paper Agents Association (PAA)
  • The Paper Federation of Great Britain

operate the scheme.

Paperpak specifically aims to serve the needs of the paper packaging chain. It draws on the expertise of the various organisations within the industry to provide a cost-effective method of meeting the legally specified obligations of its members.

 

Packaging Recovery Notes (PRNs)


Obligated businesses and schemes have to show their obligations have been met by getting evidence of recovery and recycling, most usually in the form of a Packaging Waste Recovery Note (PRN). These are issued by accredited reprocessors*, in units of one tonne, for the packaging waste they receive (they cannot be issued for amounts less than 1 tonne). Each PRN has a unique number and must be kept for four years. They are used to support the Certificate of Compliance, which each scheme or obligated company must provide to the EA or SEPA annually to confirm that it has met its recovery and recycling targets.

As well as providing evidence of Compliance, the revenue received by reprocessors from the sale of PRNs is supposed to be used to improve the recovery and recycling infrastructure to enable the UK to meet its obligations under the Packaging Directive. In fact there was a lack of demand for them in 1998. This created hardship for some organisations and did not produce the expected improvement to the recycling system.

* a reprocessor is the actual user and not the merchant who may source the material

 

Developments


In 1998 the UK Government announced a "Fundamental Review" of the Regulations and the UK paper industry welcomed this. While the industry supported the philosophy of Shared Producer Responsibility it was concerned about the complexity of the system, its potential cost and the failure to prosecute the substantial number of free-riders that had failed to register.

The changes made were not as far reaching as had been expected:

  • Interim targets were increased for 1999 and 2000*

  • The turnover threshold from 2000 was increased from £1m to £2m.

  • The wholesaler obligation planned for 2000, whereby wholesalers were obliged to take on the selling obligation for the small businesses they supplied, was removed from the Regulations.

  • Obligated businesses whose main activity is selling are now required to inform consumers about the role they can play in increasing the recovery and recycling of packaging waste. A similar requirement will be based on compliance schemes.

  • Agencies will be required to publish their monitoring programmes and report biannually on numbers, size and sector of business monitored for registration, data and compliance with recovery and recycling obligations.

  • The registration deadline for businesses and schemes will be 7 April each year.

  • It was decided that separate targets for households would not be introduced at this juncture.

  • The decision regarding the adoption of proposed changes to the activity obligations on converters and packer/fillers was deferred pending further consultation.

 

Implications of the Regulations for the Paper Industry


Paper has the highest recovery and recycling rates of any packaging material in the UK. In 1998 we consumed over 4.2m tonnes of paper packaging; 2.6m tonnes were domestically produced. On the basis of evidence of Compliance provided to the EA and SEPA, 1.89 million tonnes were recycled. However statistics collated by The Paper Federation show that 1.69m tonnes of post consumer packaging waste were recycled. Significant disparities in packaging waste and recovery/recycling statistics provided by registered obligated parties and from industry assessments remain to be resolved.

Current paper packaging recycling rates are considerably in excess of the 2001 national target level of 15%. However, the EU Directive specifies that the overall recovery target can be met in any material. Given that most other materials will fail to reach this level, it is inevitable that, if the national recovery target is to be met, the recovery of paper packaging will have to exceed 50%, probably by a considerable margin. This is despite the expectation that, by 2001, paper packaging's share of the overall packaging waste stream will be no more than 48%. Some estimates suggest that paper packaging recovery (including Energy from Waste) will have to increase by more than 25% over the 1996 level. The industry is concerned that the increase in the financial turnover threshold will increase the number of "free-riders", placing even greater responsibility on obligated companies. It hopes that the increased targets will create a greater demand for PRNs, thus allowing them to fulfil their intended purpose.

 

Recent Developments


The European Commission is currently involved in discussions that could lead to a revision of the Packaging and Packaging Waste Directive for the period 2002-2006. There are signs that there will be greater emphasis on reuse, new definitions of packaging and recycling and separate targets for sales packaging. The timetable for the revision is to produce a draft for mid 1999 with possible adoption by the year end.

The Environment Agency and Scottish Environment Protection Agency published revised guidance on the interpretation of packaging early in 1999 to help companies determine what is packaging and what isn’t. Copies are available from Adrian Harding at adrian.harding@environmentagency.gov.uk.
