FOREWORD
Why we need good statistics
I
n today's globalizing world, where the
pace of information exchange is quickening
daily, statistics are becoming an ever-more
crucial tool in the work of government,
industry and public sector bodies. Not only
are they needed as a benchmark of how far
we have come, but they also help us to
analyze trends, to forecast future needs
and to direct our resources accordingly.
Without a doubt, the knowledgeable
decision-maker will identify and anticipate
opportunity long before it becomes evident
to others. But while the Internet and
telecommunications now permit us to
capture a broader array of data at its
source, it is easy to forget that in many
developing countries there is an enormous
mismatch between the demand for
information and these countries' ability to
supply and use it.
The need for a wide range of
timely and reliable statistics as a basis for
sound policy dialogue and decision making
resulted in the creation of UNESCO
Institute for Statistics in 1999. The
Institute sets out to systematically gather,
2
Page 3
record and analyze data that will be useful to policy makers
in all UNESCO Member States. In particular, it works towards
assisting these countries in strengthening their own data
collection and reporting capacities in the fields of education,
science and culture while building and improving
partnerships at the international level.
UNESCO's statistical capabilities are of central
importance to the quality, timeliness, and responsiveness of
our work. Collaboration between the UNESCO Institute for
Statistics and other international and regional agencies
(particularly the OECD) has resulted in agreement on
common definitions, the use of high standards of quality
control, and better data documentation. All of these are
improving the international comparability of the statistics
produced.
This publication provides a snapshot of the work we
are doing and contributes to the continuing effort to make
comparative data and information accessible and useful to
audiences worldwide. It is the first from the UNESCO Institute
for Statistics, so please feel free to send us any comments or
suggestions to help us improve future editions.
Denise Lievesley
Director, UNESCO Institute for Statistics
"Good statistics allied to appropriate government policies can
change things radically and for the better."
Clare Short,
Secretary of State for International Development (UK),
November 1999.

Part 1- Education
A
s countries worldwide restructure their economies and
societies in response to technological, demographic and
economic changes, governments are giving greater
weight to the knowledge and skills embodied in individuals.
But as education systems expand, one increasingly important
aspect for managing their growth is to ensure that all parts
of the population benefit. In many countries, certain
population groups have traditionally been at a disadvantage,
including children in impoverished rural and urban areas and
groups defined by factors such as income, ethnicity and
gender.
Even though population growth rates in many less
developed countries remain high, many countries will
experience a window of opportunity over the coming decades
to provide education for all. Given the falling numbers of
children, if in addition more resources are devoted to
education there is then the scope not only to increase access
but also to improve quality. Yet public money alone may still
not be sufficient. In the twenty-first century, new
partnerships are needed between the public and private
sectors to meet education needs.
The road from Jomtien to Dakar and beyond
In 1990, delegates from 155 countries gathered at
the World Conference on Education for All (EFA) in Jomtien,
Thailand, to set the future global agenda on education and
literacy. Since then, UNESCO, has been working with a wide
range of institutional, private sector and civil society partners
to monitor progress by countries and organizations towards
the goals of Education for All and particularly to ensure that
basic education remains on the development agenda.
Two important conferences took place during the
1990s. As a result of the first in Delhi in 1993, the nine most
7
Page 8
populous developing countries -- between them representing
half the population of the world and two-thirds of adult
illiterates -- formed the E-9 initiative (Bangladesh, Brazil,
China, Egypt, India, Indonesia, Mexico, Nigeria and Pakistan).
Following the Mid-Decade Review of Education for
All, which took place in Amman, Jordan in 1996, a data
gathering process, culminating in the EFA 2000 Assessment
(in which more than 180 countries participated), was
initiated. The UNESCO Institute for Statistics has played a key
role in this exercise in compiling country data and analyzing
trends. It also enabled us to identify those countries for
which statistical capacity-building should be a priority.
The results of the EFA 2000 Assessment were
reviewed during the World Education Forum in Dakar,
Senegal, in April 2000, where Ministers of Education, other
policy-makers, representatives of civil society and others with
an interest in education came together to review progress
and to agree on a Framework for Action, which defined
education goals and concrete targets.
Caveats and qualifications
The following data have been taken from a number
of key sources and are subject to caveats and qualifications
concerning comparability. Every effort has been made to
compile the most up-to-date and reliable information, mainly
through questionnaires sent to each of the UNESCO Member
States as part of the EFA 2000 Assessment exercise. Sources
for each sub-section are provided in the footnote
accompanying the title, as well as individually where
appropriate.
It should be borne in mind that all data are subject
to differences within and among countries concerning
definitions, accounting practices and recording methods. At
the present time, the Institute for Statistics is seeking to
improve the harmonization of data gathering techniques in
the field of education.

W
hile more children than ever before are benefiting
from their right to an education, the quality of
primary education has not always kept abreast of
the expansion in enrolment. Progression in formal schooling
should not be considered smooth if more than a small
proportion of students have to repeat one or more years of
schooling, if they dropout or if their participation is
interrupted for a period of time. Dropout at the primary level
is virtually non-existent in industrialized countries because
compulsory education laws are enforced. In the less
developed regions, however, many obstacles remain to
providing universal access to schooling and to reducing
school wastage. Child labour is one of these obstacles. In
these countries grade repetition and early dropout are
indicators than the education system is not efficient in its
use of resources or has inadequate resources.

ore people are now entering secondary education

need to be continued throughout adulthood. Rising skill
requirements of labour markets, increases in unemployment

.
Enrolment in secondary education worldwide has expanded

nearly fourteen-fold from 6.5 million in 1950 to 88.2
million in 1997.
.
Today, an average of four out of ten young people in OECD
countries are likely, during the course of their lives, to enter
tertiary programmes which lead to the equivalent of a Bachelor's
degree or above.
.
But while a 17 year-old in OECD countries can expect to
receive 2.3 years of tertiary education on average over his
or her lifetime, the expectancy of tertiary education is
below one year in Brazil, Indonesia, Malaysia and Thailand.
.
In Malaysia, half of 25-34 year-olds have completed upper

1.6 million higher education students pursue their studies

Adult literacy rates have risen relatively faster for women
than for men: from 55% in 1970 to 74% today, compared
to a rise from 71% to 85% for men during the same period.
.
More women throughout the world are now entering
tertiary education. Only 32% of all students in tertiary
education were women in 1950. This percentage now
stands at 48%, with, since 1985 more female than male
students in Europe and Northern America.
.
In the world women represent 94% of pre-primary school
teachers, 58% of primary school teachers and 48% of
secondary school teachers.

T
he gender gap is the indicator that Education for All is
not being achieved. This is despite the fact that it is
clear that improvements in the access of women to
education and ensuring their broader participation in society
provides one of the keys to the eradication of poverty and has
a positive impact on demographic trends as well as on social
and economic development.
.
Regional differences are evident. Whereas in Europe and
North America only about 2% of women are illiterate
(and only 1% men), in Southern Asia nearly 3 in 5
women and 1 in 3 men are illiterate.
.
The greatest improvements in literacy rates for women since
1990 have occurred in sub-Saharan Africa (where literacy
rates have risen from 41% in 1990 to an estimated 54%
in the year 2000) and the Arab States (where rates have
risen from 37% to 50%). Despite these recent improvements,
however, in the Arab States the difference between the
sexes is greater than any other world region apart from
Southern Asia.
.
Gender disparities for literacy rates are less marked amongst
young adults. There is now little or no gender difference
in the literacy rates of 15-24 year olds in several regions
of the world including Europe & North America, Latin
America & the Caribbean and Eastern Asia & Oceania.

L
ike any investment, education involves both costs and
returns and while many countries share ambitious
targets for educating their citizens, they are constrained
by availability of resources and the efficiency of the systems
that exist to allocate them.
Public expenditure on education
.
An estimated 63% of education costs world wide are met
by governments. Countries such as Bangladesh, Brazil and
Egypt allocate close to 6 % of their gross national product
(GNP) to education.
.
But although education remains predominantly publicly
funded, the private sector* is becoming increasingly more
important and accounts on average for 35% of education
funding. The last 2% comes from overseas aid programmes.
*
The private sector is taken here to mean a combination of pupils, parents, employers, non-
governmental organizations and private business.
.
Average levels of spending on education worldwide have
hovered around 5% of GNP for more developed countries
and 4% for less developed countries in the period between
1980 and 1997.
.
However, in 1998, half of the less developed countries that
provided data to the EFA 2000 Assessment exercise reported
public spending on primary education of less than 1.7%
of Gross National Product (GNP). One-tenth of the countries
reported spending of less than 0.7% and another one-tenth
over 3.6%.
.
Including all levels of education, in 1997 the more developed
countries spent 28 times as much per pupil as that spent
by the less developed countries.

UNESCO's study on the measured flows in the value of
cultural goods is based on data provided by the Commodity
Trade Statistics (COMTRADE) and covers printed matter and
literature, music, visual arts, cinema and photography, radio
and television, games and sporting goods. It provides an
indication of consumer patterns throughout the world and
suggests that there been a structural change in the relative
importance of the components of cultural trade since 1980.
In part, this reflects the rapid and breathtaking expansion of
fast moving consumer goods (FMCG), including music,
photographic goods, games and sporting goods and the
convergence of consumer tastes.
.
World imports of cultural goods rose from $48 billion in
1980 to $214 billion in 1998 (from $12 per capita in 1980
to $45 in 1997).

and European Union (EU) accounted for 79% of all imports
of cultural commodities and 85% of exports. By 1998, these
two trading blocks accounted for 91% of imports and 94%
of exports by value.

The United States, the United Kingdom, Germany and France
comprise the Big Four Consumers, importing more than
half the international exchange in cultural goods.

periodicals, phonographic equipment, musical instruments,

Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Article 26) *
*
Article 26 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, proclaimed by the United Nations on
10 December 1948
"1. Everyone has the right to education. Education shall be
free, at least in the elementary and fundamental stages.
Elementary education shall be compulsory. Technical and
professional education shall be made generally available and
higher education shall be equally accessible to all on the
basis of merit.
2. Education shall be directed to the full development of the
human personality and to the strengthening of respect for
human rights and fundamental freedoms. It shall promote
understanding, tolerance and friendship among all nations,
racial or religious groups, and shall further the activities of
the United Nations for the maintenance of peace.
3. Parents have a prior right to choose the kind of education
that shall be given to their children."
