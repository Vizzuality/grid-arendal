[ Summary ]

The Role of Major U.S. Foundations in the Implementation of Agenda 21:
The Five-Year Follow-up to the Earth Summit

by Norman Myers and Noel Brown
Report to the Earth Council, sponsored by: Ford Foundation, The Rockefeller Foundation, W. K. Kellogg Foundation, The John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation

Abstract

This study addresses the question: "What has been the role of major U.S. foundations in supporting the implementation of Agenda 21 and in financing key sustainable development projects in the United States and around the world?"

The study is cast within a five-year time frame to coincide with a global assessment of achievements since the historic Earth Summit held at Rio in 1992. It is set against the background of major trends and developments in the post-Rio world particularly as they affect natural resource systems and/or undermine the world's development of its human capital.

In determining the efficacy of foundation giving programs in addressing the sustainable development agenda, this study reviews major implementation efforts by intergovernmental organizations, national governments, the business community, and non-governmental organizations, to not only gauge the scope of the implementation process and the momentum that has been generated and maintained since Rio, but also the major constraints and challenges to effective implementation.

The study examines considerable data on foundation giving, its scope, consistency, shifts, and emphasis, and the way it has addressed issues of sustainable development. A number of key findings resulted and a set of indicators were produced and applied to select giving programs to determine the kind of impact foundations have had and could have on the implementation process. Finally, the study proposes a set of recommendations with a view to placing foundations in a more strategic position to exploit the potentialities and influence more profoundly the forces that shape the future and exert leadership on our transition to sustainable development.

The study supports the fact that although philanthropy dollars are small when compared to those of government and business, "when these dollars have been targeted independently and creatively, the leadership value has been tremendous."1

I. Introduction

The central objective of the Earth Summit, the United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED) held in Rio in 1992, was to move the joint environment and development issues into the center of economic policy and decision-making and to lay the foundation for the transition to a sustainable way of life on our planet.

To this end, UNCED, the largest international conference ever held, adopted a series of concrete measures, including two historic documents: a declaration of basic principles for the conduct of nations and peoples to ensure the future viability and integrity of the Earth as a hospitable home for humans and other forms of life; and Agenda 21, a plan of action establishing the agreed work program of the international community for the period beyond 1992 and into the 21st century in respect of the issues addressed by the Conference.2

Although Agenda 21 was adopted by consensus, there was no real consensus on the critical question of implementation and the financial arrangements through which this was to be effected. The proposed budget of $625 billion - never intended to be definitive - proved to be elusive and $125 billion of that sum, to be provided largely by increases in Official Development Assistance (ODA), remains as yet an unfulfilled expectation with equally diminishing prospects. In any event, the developing countries remain firm in their insistence that "new and additional" financial resources be provided by the developed countries who are largely responsible for the degradation of the planet and who should bear the main responsibility for its restoration and rehabilitation, and lead the process of sustainability.

As the United Nations prepares for the fifth anniversary of UNCED, the question of financing, especially in the light of declining levels of ODA during this period, is likely to dominate the discussions with the need for new and additional resources assuming a new urgency.

It could provide new opportunities for serious exploration of new and creative ways of financing sustainable development. It is here that the non-profit and philanthropic sector, with their long-standing experience in "giving" and supporting a wide variety of human and environmental concerns, could assume a new leadership, the more so as the citizens' sector begins to take on ever-widening responsibilities for action at the grassroots level in addressing environmental problems where the impacts are the most real and most immediately felt.

If the present implementation and financial dilemmas are to be resolved, then other resource bases will have to be tapped and new partnerships with the strategically relevant sectors forged. This would be very much in keeping with the spirit of Rio whose essential characteristic was "partnership and cooperation". Agenda 21 was essentially a framework for new and unprecedented levels of global cooperation. Moreover, it was quite explicit in calling for a fuller engagement of major groups including youth, indigenous peoples, municipal leaders, and business and industry, among others in the sustainable development revolution. Any serious assessment of the implementation process, therefore, must examine the state of such cooperation. Foundations have a long-standing and demonstrated record of support for sustainable development and hopefully, the forthcoming global assessment will not only acknowledge and encourage such support but also establish the necessary mechanisms for new and creative partnerships with the philanthropic community. This study will examine such a prospect and make recommendations accordingly.

The terms of reference are:

I. Define and develop a set of indicators to evaluate the response of the U.S. philanthropic sector to the UNCED meeting in general and Agenda 21 in particular.

II. Measure the impact (in terms of character and quality) of initiatives taken by U.S. foundations to the provisions of Agenda 21 with particular reference to the following sectors namely; biodiversity, climate change, energy, human capital, as well as the implications of changes in overseas development assistance.

III. Suggest options, challenges, constraints, and strategies for improving effectiveness of the initiative of the U.S. foundations in relation to the UNCED mandate.

II. The Post-Rio World

A. Trends and Pressures

Since the end of the Cold War, and particularly in the five years since Rio, the world's economy has grown explosively. We have seen growth in the standard of living, incomes and Gross Domestic Product (GDP) in a majority of the world's developing countries. The world's gross output of goods and services is expected to grow by about 2.5 percent in 1996 for a third year in a row. Global trade has expanded dramatically; the value of world exports has increased by an average of 10.5 per cent annually. The net value of worldwide global trade was $4.3 trillion in 1995. Growth in global GDP in developing countries grew by 5.3 per cent per year between 1992 - 1996, up from 3.1 per cent in the 1980s thereby outperforming the developed countries.

Many factors have contributed to this dramatic growth in the world's economy, including the globalization and liberalization of trade, as well as the explosive growth in communications and information technologies. These advances in the movement of people, goods, images and ideas, as well as the deregulation of international trade barriers, have lead to the unprecedented growth in trade and global economic activity.

But while these economic trends have been good for many of the world's people, the benefits have yet to reach many more or to be shared equitably. Nearly 1.5 billion people, especially in "least-developed" countries, experienced declining or stagnant per capita incomes and GDP throughout the 1990s.

Also, this expansion has caused various social and ecological externalities which threaten ongoing improvements in the standard of living worldwide. The world is steadily urbanizing; people are abandoning rural areas and flooding into cities. While nearly half of the world's population now lives in cities, a majority of the world's population -- over five billion people -- are expected to make this move by 2015.

Growing energy demands have characterized this economic expansion. By 1993, world energy consumption was almost 50 per cent greater than in 1973. Most of this demand was satisfied by the burning of fossil fuels, posing serious risks of global climate change, acute local and regional air pollution, and disruption of communities and agriculture. Transport is the fastest growing end-use of energy in developing countries. In 1995, more new cars were sold in developing Asia than in Western Europe and North America combined.

Improvements in agricultural technologies since the onset of the Green Revolution have brought increased global harvests, food surpluses in industrialized countries, and falling prices, as well as a sense that the world's ability to feed itself was inexhaustible. More recently however, a number of factors have arisen to raise doubts about food security into the next century. The growth rate in world food harvests has slowed markedly, marine fish stocks are depleting, soil erosion has accelerated, and population growth remains steady. The 1995 world grain harvests dropped 4 per cent from 1994 and soybean harvests fell 8 per cent. At the same time, meat production grew 4 per cent and the worldwide fish catch climbed 5.8 per cent between 1993 and 1994. While globally there is still abundant food, nearly 840 million people remain hungry worldwide. Solutions to the threats the world's food supply faces are in addressing the environmental and social consequences of intensified farming techniques, including land degradation, water pollution, pesticide use, and more rational dietary and consumption patterns.

Water presents an equally problematic picture and the most likely leading constraint on development in many countries.3

These are but a few of the more manifest social, economic and environmental problems that the world increasingly will be obliged to confront as we move into a new and perhaps unprecedented era of global economic expansion. These problems should give new meaning and an added sense of urgency to the provisions of Agenda 21 and the principles of sustainability that it offers as well the opportunities for more effective participation by key elements of civil society in reversing non-sustainable patterns of production, consumption and lifestyles, and in shaping the new order.

B. Agenda 21 : The Implementation Process

Implementation Initiatives and Strategies

Global Conference Processes

Since 1992, the United Nations has convened a number of global conferences designed to set in motion a systematic process for implementing key provisions of Agenda 21, strengthen the commitments of governments and civil society, and maintain the momentum generated by the UNCED:

  • Global Conference on Small Island Developing States (Barbados, 1994) which focused on the susceptibility of island states to environmental degradation, their vulnerability and their requirements for sustainability.
  • The International Conference on Population and Development (Cairo, 1994) formulated programs for expanded education, health care, family planning and economic opportunities, especially for women, in reducing desired family size and thereby reducing population growth.
  • The World Summit for Social Development (Copenhagen, 1995) emphasized that eradication of poverty, full employment and social integration are vital to social development.
  • The Fourth World Conference on Women (Beijing, 1995) noted that many women disproportionately bore the burdens of environmental degradation but remain largely absent from the process of policy formulation and decision- making although their activities are crucial to sustainable development .
  • UNCTAD IX (Midrand, 1996), while recognizing the potential benefits of globalization and trade liberalization to developing countries, emphasized the risks of marginalization of poorer countries unable to capitalize on new opportunities.
  • HABITAT II (Istanbul, 1996) gave new meaning to the promise of partnership between governments and civil society and the urgent need to address the importance of human settlements in sustainable development.
  • Highly Migratory Species and Straddling Fish Stocks (New York, 1996) secured broad agreement on the need to protect species that move in and out of the territorial jurisdictions and exclusive zones of coastal states.
  • World Food Summit (Rome, 1996) refocused the problem of hunger as a central global concern and the need to ensure both food security and food availability, especially in the coral regions of the world.
All of these conferences have adopted plans of action in support of Agenda 21 and provided new impetus for international cooperation in specific sectors.

National and Local Initiatives

In addition to these international initiatives, the United Nations also reports encouraging progress at the national level. It notes for example that over 150 countries have established national level commissions or coordinating mechanisms designed to develop an integrated approach to sustainable development and to include a wide range of civil society sectors in the process of agenda setting and strategy building. More than 90 per cent of them have been established in response to Rio, the majority in developing countries. In some countries, the national councils of sustainable development have been more political than substantive in nature.

Progress had also been reported among major groups such as women, indigenous peoples, and NGOs that are increasingly beginning to interact directly with national governments and international organizations and are promoting effective partnerships among members of the wider community in advancing the cause of sustainable development. In this connection, indigenous peoples have become especially active participants at the intergovernmental level in encouraging the implementation of the Convention on Biodiversity and the need to protect genetic resources.

But perhaps the most promising developments are occurring at the local and municipal levels where local Agenda 21 initiatives are being generated at a remarkable rate.

According to the United Nations, these have been grass roots expressions of concern and involvement rather than top-down planning exercises. In many cases local authorities have been reluctant to link their efforts to national action plans for fear that the agenda will then be imposed on them from above rather than flowing from local needs. A wide variety of successful cases have been reported in the last four years on these initiatives. It has also been concluded that local level strategies and plans have proven far more successful than those at the national level in terms of making a direct impact.

On the other hand, whereas NGOs are very active in developing and implementing actions for sustainable development at the local and national level and serving as implementing partners with national governments, bilateral and multilateral aid organizations, they do not always receive the necessary financial support from national institutions and do not have adequate access to international bodies. If their full potential in helping to achieve sustainability is to be realized, however, then their role needs to be greatly enhanced.

The Business Community The Earth Summit made a direct appeal to the private sector to become effective partners with the United Nations and governments in shaping a sustainable future. So far, business leaders across a wide spectrum of the industrial sector are beginning to launch initiatives with sustainability as their stated objective and "with notable progress in the areas of joint industry/government partnerships and the development of innovative policy instruments, environmentally efficient technologies and products, and broader sustainability concerns regarding the relation of business and the wider community."

In its latest survey on post-Rio developments affecting the business community, the United Nations reports that considerations of cost-efficiency and effectiveness are encouraging governments to supplement traditional regulatory approaches with a broader policy package including economic instruments and other strategies based on "partnerships" between the private and public sectors. These instruments, which include environmental taxes and charges, environmental subsidies, environmental funds, negotiable emissions instruments, environmental performance bonds and voluntary agreements, are in use at some level in all regions of the globe by both developed and developing country governments.

Business and industry organizations are responding with the development and implementation of an increasing number of voluntary codes of conduct and environmental management systems which help firms to meet environmental performance standards without the need for detailed regulation.5

A number have also moved beyond "end-of-pipe" pollution control to a more integrated cleaner production and life-cycle approach aimed at reducing the environmental impacts of the goods and services they provide.6

Small and medium-sized industries are also addressing the problems of sustainability with varying degrees of success. But as the United Nations suggests, in both developed and developing countries, they are in need of support if they are to manage the growing sustainability challenge posed by national and international environmental regulations, standards and voluntary codes that are developed by large companies.

Impediments, Constraints and Emerging Challenges

Despite a significant number of gains in the effort to advance the provisions of Agenda 21, there are a number of influences which, while indirect, could significantly modify - if not nullify - the gains anticipated in the implementation process. These include perverse subsidies, institutional roadblocks, environment/development linkages, environmental discontinuities, concealed costs of inaction, and consumption.

Perverse Subsidies

Perverse subsidies are those that exert adverse effects of both environmental and economic sorts over the long run. The principal sectors in question are agriculture, fossil fuels/nuclear energy, water, fisheries and forestry. All in all, these perverse subsidies, being funds going into unsustainable development, total some $600 billion per year worldwide, hence they exert a significant and often distortive impact on the global economy of $26 trillion, as well as serving to encourage abuse of environmental resources (Myers, 1997). Subsidies for agriculture can foster over-loading of croplands, leading to erosion and compaction of topsoil, pollution from synthetic fertilizers and pesticides, denitrification of soils, and release of greenhouse gases, among other adverse effects. Subsidies for fossil fuels aggravate pollution effects such as acid rain, urban smog and global warming.

Subsidies for water encourage mis-use and over-use of water supplies that are increasingly scarce in many lands. Subsidies for fisheries foster over-harvesting, while subsidies for forestry encourage over- logging. Consider marine fisheries: in 1994 the fish harvest cost $124 billion and was sold for $70 billion, the difference being made up largely by government subsidies which thus stimulated over-fishing to meet short-term consumer demand and prompted more fishermen to chase after fewer fish.

This is not to say that subsidies cannot serve many useful purposes. The key question is: which subsidies, of what sorts, of what scope and with what impacts, can be viewed as "perverse", i.e. inimical to society's long-term interests? Recall that the proposed budget for Agenda 21 was also $600 billion per year. Of course, it would make a superb investment to outlay a mere 2.3 percent of global GNP in order to ensure that development becomes sustainable. But for all manner of powerful even if unacceptable reasons, most governments have emphatically declined the opportunity. A notable exception is New Zealand which has phased out virtually all agricultural subsidies--remarkable in a country where agriculture is unusually prominent.

Clearly this is a question of major import. Yet it has scarcely been identified as a salient issue of our times, let alone documented and analysed. Fortunately the MacArthur Foundation has recently funded a small scale research project to investigate the question, and as such it is a prime example of what foundations can supply: investments that are innovative, catalytic and facilitating, rather than merely supportive and reinforcing. As such, they offer much scope for policy leverage and multiplier effects.

Institutional Roadblocks

Institutional roadblocks are profound and pervasive in many sectors of government. This applies especially to environmental concerns, stemming as they often do from lack of integration -- whether economic, political or otherwise--among our principal institutions of government (plus other forms of governance, notably corporations). In essence, we are blighted by a historic division of government into rigidly defined sectors, a division that is antithetical to the kinds of changes required to integrate environment into policy making throughout government systems.

To wit: the fiefdoms of e.g. energy, transport, agriculture and industry all have their own interests and agendas, which frequently induce them to subvert and otherwise oppose each other's activities.

Energy policy usually amounts to a fragmented approach to energy supply, instead of offering a coherent framework with emphasis on reductions in both supply and demand for fossil fuels plus provision of non-polluting and renewable fuels. Transport policy encourages the car culture to the detriment of alternative forms of transportation. Agriculture policy promotes unsustainable forms of farming based on high-energy inputs, plus over-intensive use of fertilizers and pesticides. Equally to the point, transportation policy is often pursued with indifference to energy policy; agriculture policy with indifference to land conservation policy; and both sets with indifference to climate constraints.

Key question: Why are environmental concerns not better translated into public policy in light of their strong intellectual rationale? Who are the main movers and shakers, and how can one best influence them? Where are the prime pressure points in the nerve ganglia of bureaucracies? We need an in-depth assessment of the entire issue of institutional roadblocks in the environmental arena: how they arise, why they become entrenched, and what can be done to side-step or eliminate them.

For illustration, consider two salient types of environmental roadblocks. The first lies with lack of integration between government sectors, as per the examples cited. In addition, national policies are often formulated with scant concern for international repercussions. Of course, it is one thing to formulate policy interventions to promote economic/environmental integration, it is another thing to establish them as a working reality in governmental systems (plus many other systems, from business enterprises to households). What is needed is reform of governmental mechanisms so that throughout economic-planning activities, environmental values are taken into account both systematically and systemically. Various responses have been attempted, such as joint programming and integrative planning.

A second type of roadblock lies with institutional inertia. Officialdom's attitudes and perceptions are deeply entrenched in myriad ways, inducing institutional inertia in virtually every sphere of government. This inertia is an exceptionally powerful force, and changes will demand an even more powerful countervailing force. At Rio, political leaders engaged in rousing rhetoric in support of the environment, while studiously avoiding commitments to actual action in the form of specific policy measures backed by concrete programmes and time targets. As a result, there have been few solid initiatives to stem deforestation and species loss in much of the world, to cut back GHG emissions everywhere (even to expand energy efficiency anywhere), and to counter wasteful and excessive consumption in affluent societies.

Since Rio, moreover, there has been all too little mention of the environmental cause in the councils of power, in the media or in most other sectors of the public arena. If the road to Rio was difficult, how much more so has been the road from Rio in face of gross indifference and an implicit readiness to maintain institutional inertia. The longer we delay with measures to counter the inertia that infects the body politic, the more deeply the present patterns of departmentalized government will become institutionalized.

Fortunately, there can be productive inertia triggered by a turn-around in our institutional systems, generating multiple benefits of progressive size and inducing a constructive form of momentum. The more a government engages in policy measures to promote the environmental cause, the more the benefits will become plain, percolating through every sector of government and segment of society. When once the change in direction is achieved, traditionalist resistance can quickly give way to organic support.

This assessment of institutional roadblocks offers scope for U.S. foundations to assemble a group of leading policy makers, also political scientists and institutional experts, to confront the challenge head on. In other words, to gather together a number of individuals with extensive experience of the fundamental problem, and to ask for their views on how to resolve it. Or, to express it another way, assemble individuals who understand both the flow of energy through ecosystems and the flow of influence through corridors of power.

Salient issues to be addressed could include:

How to overcome the super-preoccupation of governments with the short term ("A week in politics is a long time")? Related topic: how to institutionalize the rights of future generations?

How to ensure that government operates as an organic whole, with policies that are consistent throughout the entire system? Translation: how to persuade individual sectors and departments to coordinate rather than compete? How to counter turf rivalries and empire building?

How to tackle the tendency of governments to react rather than act (the squeaking door syndrome of special interest groups and potent lobbyists)? With particular reference to environmental issues, shall we need a catastrophe before it becomes clear to governments they cannot continue with business as usual--even though that option is increasingly foreclosed by force of circumstance (becoming ever-more forceful)?

How to persuade governments that to do nothing is to do a great deal in a fast changing world? And how to educate governments on the new nature of change, which often arrives in non-linear form, i.e. in jumps rather than curves?

What is the role of public opinion and the media? Who are the main moulders of established (or establishment) thinking?

Environment/Development Linkages

Environment/development linkages--whether linkages between sectors, issues, institutions or stakeholders--have become as vital as they are widespread. Despite their super significance, however, these linkages tend to be ignored because our view of the world is traditionally grounded in a practice of splitting it up into "manageable" components. In a world growing ever- more interdependent by virtue of proliferant relationships--whether environmental, economic, political or social relationships--we can no longer afford the luxury of supposing that linkages are an incidental factor that is too complex to be reflected ("operationalized") through institutional responses. Yet we still try to direct our daily affairs with institutional systems that reflect a bygone and far less integrative era. These institutional systems are singularly unsystematic in that they disregard what is now a predominant phenomenon of the planetary ecosystem and the world order.

Bottom-line concern: we will respond to linkages either by responses of sufficient scope and character, or by salvage measures in a world impoverished by our disregard for linkages.

For illustration, consider the question of the oceans. This obviously includes sectors such as fisheries, biodiversity, pollution (both sea- and land-derived), technology, climate and energy. Less obviously though inevitably insofar as "the oceans" extend to coastal zones, the thematic sphere includes human settlements (and hence population: 2.5 billion people live within 100 kilometers of coastlines, also agriculture and industry (both major sources of pollution). The sector can even include forestry insofar as deforestation of inland watersheds leads to siltation of port facilities. Deforestation also leads to a "smothering impact" from soil, silt and other debris washed off watersheds onto in-shore fisheries.

Yet our analytic response to oceans issues is typically scientific and technical for the most part, whereas the economic and political dimensions should receive at least as much weight. Equally to the point, the policy approach to the oceans is generally specialized and categorized rather than integrative and holistic, even though the latter is surely required for an entity with a continuum of human activities that recognize no boundaries. Even more significant, the institutions deployed to tackle ocean questions usually enjoy scant mandate to address questions in systemic as well as systematic manner, even though oceans problems tend to be systemic in both scope and scale. Among institutions grappling with oceans problems are the International Maritime organization, the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission, the U.N. Law of the Sea, UNEP's Regional Seas Programme, and the Food and Agriculture Organization's Fisheries Department. Yet because they are constitutionally little concerned with questions of linkages, these specialized bodies, mostly under the United Nations, are inclined to respond in competitive if not contradictory fashion--quite the opposite of the collaborative response postulated by the phenomenon of linkages. Indeed the entire U.N. system is constitutionally mal-adapted to manage linkages. The specialized agencies (FAO, WHO, UNESCO, etc.) have been set up to tackle single-sector issues for the most part (food, health, science, etc.). Plainly there should be methodical collaboration on environment and development between the two agencies principally designated, UNEP and UNDP; but equally plainly, there is little. So too, UNFPA and UNICEF do not always work in close accord on child welfare. To this extent, the U.N. system hardly functions as a system.

The Secretary-General and the central secretariat have inadequate authority to ensure that the specialized agencies coordinate their activities.

It is precisely because of this disregard for linkages that many ocean problems are increasing in size and number. When land-use planners decide to site industries in coastal zones, they unwittingly foster use of the marine realm as a costless sewer, whereupon pollution depletes ecosystem health in the oceans with adverse impacts on fish stocks and biodiversity generally. When fisheries experts develop plans for sustained-yield harvests in developing-nation waters, they tend to limit their analyses to scientific and technical factors, overlooking the often impoverished status of fisheries' communities that fosters over- harvesting. When human-settlement agencies encourage the buildup of ever-larger numbers of people in coastal zones, they act with indifference to climatologists' warnings of sea-level rise which will surely inundate some of the most densely settled areas of Earth's surface. Most of these instances of "linkages myopia" apply in developing and developed countries alike. The problems arise because planners do not take automatic cognizance of the entire gamut of consequences stemming from their activities.

What can be done to operationalize the question of linkages? How can we ensure that environment/development interdependencies in particular become a working reality in economic, political and institutional terms? The challenge is to modify policy makers' outlook so profoundly that they no longer look upon environment and development as two separate arenas of activity, viewing the environment as a constraint on development, to be articulated as little more than an add-on factor--instead of the built-in dimension that it has demonstrably become. This is no longer an option, it is an imperative--a solidly entrenched fact of life. Yet despite its detailed documentation on many occasions since UNCED, there has been all too little attempt on the part of governments and international agencies, let alone business enterprises and other parties, to adapt their policy strategies accordingly. This should not be so surprising, since the challenge is no less than a seismic shift in the mind-set of policy makers, and a fundamental departure in the day-to-day workings of governments, agencies and corporations.

To break the mould, a sound start could be made through the introduction of natural resource accounting, entraining a radical revision of the way in which governments assess the state of their economies. A related measure would be to adjust those pricing policies that send out false messages about sustainable use of environmental resources because they do not reflect all costs of production (a measure known technically as "full social cost pricing"). Further measures could include the Polluter Pays Principle and the Precautionary Principle. These measures will require a radical reorientation to shift policy makers' outlook from their erstwhile mode of linear thinking, i.e. non-linkages thinking.

To further illustrate the scope and character of the challenge, consider the pivotal role played by fuelwood in the rural economies of many developing countries. Due to fuelwood deficits, rural households divert animal manure and crop residues from farm fields to house hearths, causing a decline in soil fertility and cropland productivity.

In developing Asia and Africa alone, at least 400 million tonnes of dung are burned each year; and each tonne means a loss of at least 50 kgs. of potential grain grown, or a total of 20 million tonnes of grain per year, being enough to feed 100 million people for one year (some analysts calculate the total at twice as much). To purchase the grain on world markets could cost $4 billion. In much of Ethiopia, between 60 and 90 percent of livestock manure is used as fuel, which translates into agricultural output foregone worth $600 million (mid-1980s figure), or roughly 30 percent of the country's value added in its entire agriculture sector (Pearce and Warford, 1992). In addition, fuelwood shortages cause people to spend large amounts of time daily in trekking to find supplies. If we allow just 25 U.S. cents per hour as the value of the time opportunity costs involved, the aggregate cost in Asia and Africa alone amounts to at least $50 billion per year (Myers and Simon, 1994). This figure is equivalent to one-third of the collective GNP of the region with the worst fuelwood shortages, Sub-Saharan Africa. It is also to be compared with the cost of a comprehensive fuelwood-plantation programme, $1 billion per year for ten years--a figure often regarded as quite unjustified and unavailable.

A more localized illustration lies with traffic congestion. In Bangkok, traffic jams are so severe that the amount of passengers' time lost on city streets, plus the amount of extra gasoline consumed, are reckoned to cost at least $1 billion per year. A further $1 billion is lost through medical bills and worker absenteeism due to pollution-related ailments, also through pollutants' damage to buildings and other infrastructure. In developing-world cities generally, more than one billion people are believed to be living in conditions so polluted that the air is not fit to breathe.

These are just two of the more important intersectoral linkages. The numbers and significance of these and other salient linkages suggest that collectively they can sometimes be as important as the sectors themselves for overall processes of development. Yet almost all the sectors cited are handled by separate bodies (whether within governments or international agencies), often with institutional interests that are far from congruent with each other.

Environmental Discontinuities

Here we shall look at a strongly emergent challenge for the environmental outlook, viz. environmental discontinuities together with the associated issue of synergisms. Both will surely become front-rank problems for the environmental cause -- a proposition grounded in the notion that the future is not going to be a simple extension of the past. We should anticipate that environmental discontinuities, or non-linearities, will become a prominent phenomenon worldwide, hence they will carry profound repercussions for the environment. Many discontinuities are likely to arise from synergistic interactions between two or more environmental problems.

Both discontinuities and synergisms constitute "surprise" outcomes in the sense that their effects diverge from the expected extensions of established trends. They have the capacity (1) be to be profoundly disruptive of environments, and (2) to catch us unawares by overwhelming our anticipatory and preventative capacities. Indeed, the worst environmental problems ahead will often be the ones we have scarcely thought of as yet. These two sets of surprise phenomena deserve priority attention from environmental scientists. Yet the literature has hardly touched on either of them. A library computer check reveals few substantive efforts to broach them even in exploratory terms. They are largely black holes of research and analysis, even though both promise to become ever-more frequent phenomena in light of the expanding niche of humankind and its multifarious activities.

A classic illustration of a discontinuity is when liquid water changes to ice or steam. In the present context, environmental discontinuities, sometimes designated by ecologists as "jump effects", occur when ecosystems absorb stress over long periods without much outward sign of damage, but eventually are pushed to the limits of their resilience. They reach a disruption level at which the cumulative consequences of stress finally reveal themselves through systemic change of critical scale (Costanza and Cornwell, 1992; Faber et al., 1992; Schneider and Turner, 1995). A familiar instance is acid rain. Another is the deforestation-induced drop-off in rainfall recorded in a dozen areas of the humid tropics (Myers, 1988). Still another example is the depletion of the ozone layer to the point where "holes" appear, allowing penetration of unusual amounts of UV-B radiation to the Earth's surface.

Yet another example is the buildup of greenhouse gases, which, after accumulating for decades with little effect, seem set to trigger a pronounced disruption of climate systems worldwide (Schneider, 1995; Woodwell and MacKenzie, 1995). Yet in each of these cases, our most advanced models tend to discount, thanks to their very structure, the possibility of discontinuities.

To illustrate the scope of potential impacts, human activities can readily deplete the environmental underpinnings (soil, water, climate) of agriculture, leading to a downturn in the capacity of agriculture to sustain human communities at their erstwhile level (Brown et al., 1997; Ehrlich et al., 1995). As a result, established farmlands may no longer be able to do their job of feeding humankind with its burgeoning numbers. A further factor lies with landlessness: in Philippines, Costa Rica and a number of other developing countries, it has recently transpired that no more lands remain readily available for opening up to agriculture. There has occurred a "break point" in conventional patterns of settlement on the part of smallscale farmers. As a result, there has ensued a greatly and suddenly increased pressure on forests and savannahs among other environmental resources (Jazairy et al., 1992). We can expect there will be many steeply stepped-up tendencies for land-hungry peasants to encroach onto hitherto little disturbed environments.

While we are well aware of the main factors disrupting environmental resources, they tend to be studied in isolation from each other. We know much less and understand less still about the dynamic interplay between discrete factors. Yet synergisms -- literally, the uniting of energies -- are unusually significant. For instance, a biota's tolerance of one stress tends to be lower when other stresses are at work. A plant that experiences depleted sunlight and hence reduced photosynthesis is unduly prone to the adverse effects of cold weather, and it thereby suffers much more in the cold than would a plant enjoying normal growth and full vigour. A similarly amplified effect operates the other way around as well. In certain circumstances, a synergisms-induced outcome can be a whole order of magnitude greater than the simple sum of the component mechanisms. While ecologists agree that synergisms rank among the most important phenomena in nature, most leading textbooks do not even cite the issue in their indexes. But when we consider the likely outcome of several factors operating together, we can surmise that many of their effects serve to amplify one another through mutually reinforcing interactions. Synergisms in the biodiversity sphere, for instance, working collectively and with compounding impact, will surely lead to an extinction episode of greater scale than usually envisaged. They may also cause the episode to be telescoped in time, meaning that the full biotic crisis could arrive sooner than anticipated. To the extent that we can discern some possible synergistic interactions, the better we shall start to understand the potential patterns and processes as grand scale environmental problems works themselves out--and the better we shall be able to anticipate and even prevent some of them.

An example of compounded impact consists of likely interactions stemming from global warming. There will be a need for wild plant communities to migrate away from the equator and toward the poles, which will be difficult enough for the many plant communities that will have to attempt their migrations across "development deserts" of agriculture, cities and other obstacles of human-dominated landscapes. In addition, there will be problems for agriculture due to population losses among many crop plant species. The changed temperatures and rainfall regimes expected in a greenhouse-affected world will not prove appropriate for those many crops that are finely attuned to current climatic patterns.

There will be a premium on expanding the genetic underpinnings of crops in order to increase their resistance to too much or too little rainfall, or new diseases and pathogens, among other problems arising from global warming. Yet the gene reservoirs of many crop plants are being more rapidly depleted than ever due to virtual wipe-out of wild relatives of crop plants (Food and Agriculture Organization, 1993).

In summary, we can anticipate that it will increasingly be the case for several factors to interact with multiplicative impacts, these factors including e.g. deforestation, biodepletion, desertification, soil erosion, acid rain, freak weather phenomena, global warming, ozone-layer depletion, and immunotoxin pollution among other types of environmental disruption. When we consider all environmental problems together, there is potentially a multitude of synergistic interactions that could generate pronouncedly adverse repercussions.

In face of this challenge, let us bear in mind that we have recently witnessed some constructive discontinuities of political sort: the fall of the Berlin Wall, leading to the end of the Soviet Union, communism and the Cold War; and the peace breakthrough in South Africa, plus potential breakthroughs in the Middle East, Bosnia and Northern Ireland. We are certainly able to undertake policy discontinuities of the most positive sort, provided we can identify the critical openings for intervention and leverage.

Herein lies an admirable opportunity for U.S. foundations: (a) to mount a research effort that will investigate the scope and scale of a phenomenon that may well prove to be the single most disruptive meta- factor for environments in the world ahead; and (b) to identify the most productive points for policy intervention.

Concealed Costs of Inaction

It is generally easy to calculate the costs of a specific action. It is less easy, though no less pertinent to calculate the concealed costs of inaction. Herewith a few examples with reference to just a single sector, forestry (we have already noted the case of fuelwood shortages).

a) Watershed functions - Some 40 percent of developing world farmers depend upon regular flow of rivers and streams from forested watersheds to irrigate their croplands. In Rwanda, the montane forest of the Volcanoes Part (home to one of the last populations of the mountain gorilla) covers only one percent of the country, but acts as the sponge that absorbs and metes out about 10 percent of agricultural water for that severely overpopulated nation. In Java, deforestation-deprived siltation of reservoirs, irrigation systems and harbours levied damage costs worth $58 million in 1987 (Magrath and Arens, 1989). The on-site soil conservation benefits of tree cover within India's forests are worth between $5 billion and $12 billion per year, or $100-240 per hectare, while the nation-wide value of forest services in regulating river flows and containing floods are roughly assessed at $72 billion a year (Panayotou and Ashton, 1992). Perhaps most pertinent of all: what price for watershed services in the year 2025 when a full three billion people in developing countries may well be suffering water shortages?

b) Fisheries - In Bacuit Bay on Palawan island in Philippines, logging on steep slopes has increased soil erosion 235 times above that for undisturbed forest, with a "silt smother" effect for the Bay's coral reef and its fisheries that reduced commercial revenues by almost half in the mid-1980s (Hodgson and Dixon, 1988). At the Korup Park in Cameroon, forest-derived protection of fisheries (plus food prevention and soil conservation) have a net present value of $85 per hectare (ruitenbeek, 1989). Old-growth forests in the Pacific Northwest of the North America protect habitats for 112 fish stocks, just the salmon industry is worth $1 billion per year (U.S. Forest Service, 1993).

c) Genetic resources - Asia's rice crop was hit in the early 1970s by a "grassy stunt" virus that threatened to devastate 300,000 square kilometers of rice fields. Fortunately, a single gene from a wild rice in an Indian forest offered resistance against the virus. Then in 1976 another virus, known as "ragged stunt" disease, emerged; and again, the most potent source of resistance proved to be a wild forest rice. In India alone, the introduction of wild rice strains (plus primitive cultivars) has increased yields by $75 million a year (Evanson, 1992). Many other crops -- ranging from food crops such as avocado, banana, cashew, coconut, grapefruit and lemon to beverages and spices such as cacao, cinnamon, coffee, paprika and vanilla, and to industrial crops such as oil palm and rubber -- depend for part of their productivity, disease resistance, etc., on genetic infusions from wild relatives in tropical forests. Just as the exports of these crops are worth well over $20 billion a year (Food and Agriculture Organization, 1996). Medicinals from tropical forest plants have a commercial value of $25 billion a year and an economic value several times larger (Principe, 1996). Potential future drugs awaiting discovery in tropical forests could have a theoretical value to society of $147 billion (Mendelsohn and Balick, 1995).

The problem triad of environment, population and adverse technology is now being joined by consumption. This could prove the least tractable of the four interlinked problems, since consumption patterns and expectations are deeply entrenched in most societies and cultures. Industrialized-world citizens overall generate three-quarters of other wide- ranging pollutants, also toxic chemicals and hazardous wastes. Much of the same applies to depletion of many of the world's non-renewable natural resources (Goodwin et al., 1997). Hence the consumption problem lies primarily with affluent communities (especially the U.S.), and they bear a responsibility to pioneer a path towards sustainable consumption. This is all the more pertinent insofar as sizeable communities in Eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union, also in China, India, Southeast Asia and Latin America, are entering the consumption classes. Already, they total 750 million people, or as many as the long established consumers of rich nations. In 1995, more new cars were sold in developing Asia than in western Europe and North America combined. While poor communities certainly need to increase their consumption, they might consider less wasteful and polluting forms of consumption than those that characterize rich nations.

With less than five percent of the world's population, the U.S. utilizes nearly 30 percent of the Earth's resources (Dower et al, 1996). Of course, there is nothing intrinsically wrong with rich nations consuming a large percentage of natural resources if those resources remain plentiful and can be recycled, as in the case of iron and steel (85 percent of which are consumed by the top 20 percent of people, who do not thereby limit the consumption of poor nations). Indeed, the rich nations' conversion of natural resources into human capital often enhances human welfare all round. It is of scant consequence that the average American consumes 115 times as much paper as the average Indian, provided the American recycles most of the paper (at present, only 41 percent). Much more significant is that the average American consumes 227 times as much gasoline as the average Indian (Parikh, 1991). The key question is whether consumption uses resources or uses them up.

The main responsibility for consumption problems lies with the rich, who have much more opportunity to modify their lifestyles than people struggling to lift themselves out of poverty. Not that the poor can ever aspire to matching the rich's lifestyles a long as these are based on e.g. artificially cheap fossil fuels. Were individuals worldwide to suddenly engage in fossil-fuel consumption to match that of Americans (five times the global average), they would inject so much carbon dioxide into the global atmosphere, and prompt so much rapid global warming, that many human communities and natural biotas would find it all but impossible to tolerate such a disruptive change in such a short time (Woodwell, 1995).

The fossil-fuel problem is epitomized by cars, which account for almost 20 percent of all carbon dioxide emissions (25 percent in the US). In 1950, 2.5 billion people owned 50 million cars, but today, with more than twice as many people, there are ten times as many cars. Within another 25 years and with 40 percent more people, the car population may well double again to top one billion. Rich nations, with 13 percent of the world's population, own 81 percent of all cars (the U.S., 35 percent), but in 1996 as many new cars were sold in developing Asia as in Western Europe and North America combined (Tunali, 1996).

In sum, exploitation pressures on natural resources are already unsustainable in several respects apart from global warming. Humans co-opt at least 40 percent of plants' net annual growth on land, leaving 60 percent for millions of other species. What when human numbers double and individuals demand still more products from plants? We use over half of available freshwater runoff, an amount projected to rise to three-quarters by 2025 through population growth alone. The number of water-short people today, 55 million, could well soar to three billion by 2025. Much of the same prospect -- reflecting fast-growing consumption in relation to expanding population and environmentally adverse technology -- applies to other strategic resource stocks such as topsoil, forests, grasslands, fisheries, biodiversity, climate and the atmosphere (Harvey and Pimentel, 1996). True, technology can help relieve environmental pressures, but to date it is far from matching the burgeoning scope and scale of the problem.

There are various policy options to address the consumption problem, certain of which could well be promoted by U.S. foundations. The principal goal is to move towards "sustainable consumption" definable as the use of goods and services to meet basic needs and foster quality of life, while minimizing the use of natural resources and reducing waste and pollutants to environmentally acceptable levels, and without undermining the rights of future generations (Merck Family Fund, 1995; Consumers International, 1995).

There is a lengthy list of policy options available, which could well be promoted by U.S. foundations (among interested bodies to date are the MacArthur Foundation, the Merck Family Fund, and the Pew Charitable Trusts). For instance, we need to correct marketplace failures so that prices reflect all environmental costs. A prime source of externalities is our use of gasoline. If we calculate the "true social cost" of burning gasoline by internalizing the more immediate externalities such as air pollution (which alone are worth $300 billion), the U.S. price would rise by the equivalent of a $2 tax per gallon. If Americans were to cover all costs of their car culture, including noise pollution (though still omitting global warming), they would pay at least $8 per gallon. The car culture would then lose many of its attractions: there are few better educators than the wallet. Moreover, the car increasingly fails as a convenience when traffic jams cause Americans to lose time worth $80 billion a year.

A further approach to the externalities question is to examine the "ecological footprint", being the foreign resources required to support consumption. These include agricultural lands, forests, fisheries, and energy supplies, also waste- absorption services. Rich nations' demands for food, energy and other resources, plus waste absorption, oblige them to depend increasingly upon outside sources. This would be acceptable if trading relationships were equitable, i.e. if consumer nations were to pay prices reflecting all externalities, including depletion costs. Often, however, rich nations compensate for their depletive use of their resource stocks by effectively appropriating those of poor and otherwise disadvantaged nations (Korten, 1995). In addition, there is often no market for environmental benefits such as atmospheric waste-absorption services, hence they cannot be traded whether fairly or not; and their general lack of property rights means that they are open-access resources. To reiterate, global warming from rich nations' consumption of fossil fuels will affect countries and people right around the world.

On average, a rich-nation consumer currently needs four to six hectares of productive land (croplands, pastures, forests, etc.) to sustain his or her lifestyle, yet the amount of such land worldwide is only 1.7 hectares per person. If everyone were to consume in the manner of rich nations today, we would require an extra three planets like the Earth (Durning, 1996). To this extent, a rich-world person living beyond his or her environmental means may be depriving people in poorer nations of the wherewithal to meet their basic needs -- people who are often up against the "environmental wall" anyway. An American's footprint is 5.1 hectares, an Indian's 0.38 of a hectare (though because of India's huge population, its national footprint is 361 million hectares whereas its productive land totals only 250 million hectares) (Wackernagel and Rees, 1996). An American with an energy-efficient can reduce his or her transport footprint by a factor of three, riding the bus by a factor of five, and using a bicycle by a factor or twelve. Consuming less meat and more fresh food decreases the food footprint by a factor of three (Wackernage and Rees, 1996).

In addition, we can increase resource-use efficiency through fewer materials inputs, longer product lifetimes and waste prevention. We could accomplish much by utilizing technologies already available. With the right incentives (which foundations could expertly identify), there could even be a four-fold increase in resource productivity (von Weisacker et al, 1996). Efficiency enables sufficiency, which means scant need for belt tightening or hair-shirt sacrifices. On the contrary, there is sizeable profit in it. During the past two decades, the 3M Corporation has saved $780 million through recycling and waste management.

Similar savings can be gained by reducing excess packaging. Packaging with repeat emphasis on brand-name image is costly to consumers and makes little if any contribution to human welfare. Add in advertising and other inducements to commit customers to ever-greater consumption, and the annual total amounts to some $600 billion worldwide (Barnet and Cavanagh, 1994). Moreover, throw- away packaging in the US, as in several other industrialized nations, comprises nearly half of all municipal solid waste. Tomatoes and green peppers that last a week are sold in foam containers and plastic trays that last a century. Seattle recycles 42 percent of its solid waste, by contrast with a nation-wide average of 17 percent: again, much scope to take up the slack.

In addition, governments could be persuaded to lead the change towards sustainable consumption through their procurement and administrative procedures. To cite a simple example: were governments to equip all their buildings with long- lasting and low-power light bulbs (as has been done at the White House), this would greatly expand the market for these products and enable manufacturers to engage in production economies of scale. The same applies, only more significantly for purposes both environmental and economic, to high mileage vehicles.

Finally, there is much scope for mobilizing the media to promote sustainable consumption. Each day, an average American is the target for 300 messages urging him or her to consume more. How about using the media to encourage people to consume more discriminatingly and sparingly? The same American spends an aggregate of one year watching television -- the main mode for advertising. There are now television sets in one billion households comprising almost half of humankind, a total that could well double by 2010. Again, this could prove a singularly fertile field for foundation research and intervention.

In the U.S., just the 100 largest corporations pay for 75 percent of commercial television time -- making them an admirable limited target for foundation activity. These corporations' advertising outlays have multiplied nearly seven-fold since 1950, with the growth rate fastest since 1980.

At the same time, there may well be need to restrain some of advertising's practices. How about reducing the sheer volume of advertising, now worth $650 per American per year and often aiming to promote products that no one seemed to want a few years ago but that everyone will shortly "need"? Advertising has gone so far to stimulate consumption as an end in itself that it now views it as a perpetual mandate. To the extent, moreover, that consumption is increasingly pursued as a means to enhance social status rather than to foster personal fulfillment, a reduction in advertising, shared by everyone, may prove readily acceptable to public opinion.

Let us bear in mind the ultimate imperative. It is not that consumption levels should alter among rich communities, rather they will alter if only because the Earth cannot indefinitely support the present consumption loading, let alone future loading. the realistic choice is whether present consumption is changed through greater efficiency, smarter technologies, more equitable sharing, and a start on moderation in consumption -- or whether it changes anyway as ecological and social systems are undermined until they fail, whereupon people will find themselves obliged to modify their consumption behavior, even to "do more with less". The choice will be made by design or by default. In many respects, it is already being made, albeit by default for the most part. Public opinion polls reveal that more than half of all Americans, and an ever greater proportion in several other rich nations, recognize that if environmental problems are to be surmounted, "changes in lifestyle" and even "reduced consumption" must be accepted as inevitable (Merck Family Fund, 1995; Korten, 1995).

A shift in consumption patterns for the rich will mark one of the greatest revolutions in human behaviour. Daunting as is the prospect of generating such a revolution in face of long-established inertia, recall that some surprising shifts have recently proved feasible: during the 1980s, 55 million Americans gave up smoking -- a social earthquake, virtually overnight (For an overall assessment of behavior shifts in relation to environment, see Gardner and Stern, 1996). In any case, a shift in consumption patterns means there will be scope for people to engage in the sorts of lifestyles that can ultimately prove more rewarding, with emphasis on non-material satisfactions. Should the second car be foregone and the savings be spent on personal computers, CDs, photography and sports, none of which pollutes much or eats up large amounts of raw materials? The basis of certain of these activities, electronics' technology, offers much potential for enriched lifestyles and environmentally friendly products. Whereas materials account for 40 percent of the value of a car, they make up 0.3 percent of the value of a micro-chip -- and all the microchips in the world comprise so little volume they would fit inside a jumbo-jet.

III. Actions by Major U.S. Foundations to promote Environmental and Human Sustainability

A. Giving Community, USA

As was indicated previously, foundation giving is a well established American tradition with programs covering almost the entire spectrum of human concern and whose value to the United States and the world is assuming increasing significance if not urgency. Perhaps Warren Weaver, in his book "U.S. Philanthropic Foundations: Their History, Structure, Management and Record" best captured the essence of the foundations, their scope of giving, and their contributions to society over the years, when he said almost 20 years ago:

"...foundations have freed large parts of the world from the curse of diseases such as malaria and yellow fever; have brought enjoyment of the arts to millions of people; have created and helped supported universities and research institutes; have clarified and otherwise serve the law; have in many practical ways promoted international understanding and have encouraged the cause of peace; have shown how population can be controlled and people fed; have helped developed broadly trained leadership for business and government; have significantly aided the emerging nations; have importantly contributed to our growing knowledge of physical and living nature; have been alert in aiding new fields of activity; have helped to clarify the goals of present-day humanistic scholarship; have made possible the development of important new scientific instruments for studying the atom, the cell, and the star; have in language and area studies, anticipated and provided for some of the pressing needs of our country in its new worldwide responsibility; have created multi- million dollar free funds for basic research; have recently developed and supported several projects to extend the opportunities of higher education to qualified Negroes; and have liberated thousands of gifted individuals from the limitations of inadequate education, thus freeing them for greater service to society."

This pattern of giving has continued throughout out the years adjusting to new circumstances and the imperatives of explosive global change, democratization and accelerating environmental degradation. Looked at in terms of Agenda 21 and the challenge of environmental sustainability, it might be said with confidence that foundation programs have antedated these developments by several decades. Although it is tempting to consider the UNCED, which gave rise to Agenda 21, as a convenient point of departure, it should not be forgotten that the Conference was part of a dynamic continuum of human efforts to come to terms with our changing relationship with the natural world, especially as we shift progressively from being mere ecosystems creatures to ecosystems shapers, a virtual force with nature and a source of dramatic earth change.

Agenda 21 could thus be said to give added legitimacy and authority to the concept of sustainability, making it a central global concern in the closing days of the 20th Century. During this process, however, the role of foundations can hardly be overestimated in view of their contributions supporting the mobilization of scientific energy and expertise to address more fully the state of the environment and to enable the non-governmental community to become effective participants in the shaping of effective solutions. Sometimes such support has resulted in significant scientific breakthroughs, as was the case of the Green Revolution. At other times foundation activities have served to keep issues in public focus by providing consistent support for such critical environmental issues as forestry conservation, biodiversity, as well as rural poverty. Moreover, as civil society begins to assert itself and to empower citizens to become more effective participants in informed decision making, foundation contributions have likewise begun to expand in these areas.

A clear demonstration of foundation support for Agenda 21 may be found in their assistance to non-governmental organizations enabling a number of them to become effective participants in the UNCED preparatory process. Not only did the NGOs make their presence felt in actively participating in discussions, providing new and often fresh perspectives on major issues, they also submitted their own documents which often were reflected in the final texts. NGOs were also a powerful presence at Rio and their influence on both the Biodiversity and Climate Change conventions were both responsible and effective. These examples are clearly suggestive of possibilities that are open to foundations given the considerable resources over which they preside. Moreover, foundation giving continues to grow as their numbers and assets increase.

According to most recent estimates, private contributions from all sources -- foundations, individuals, corporations and bequests -- total some $129 billion in 1994, representing around 2% of GDP. Preliminary estimates for 1995 place this figure at $143.85 billion, an increase of about 9%. In 1994, US foundations numbered 38,807 with combined assets of about $195 billion, and gave a total of $11.3 billion, a slight increase over 1993.

The larger foundations are responsible for a substantial portion of total grant dollars and account for a considerable share of total assets. They therefore hold the potential of being effective leaders in the sustainable development revolution, and as such, would seem to warrant closer scrutiny. For example, the 50 largest foundations ranked by total grants in 1994 accounted for $2.7 billion, or 24% of the $11.3 billion total grants in 1994 (Renz, Qureshi & Mandler, 1996). The 50 largest foundations in total assets accounted for $70.9 billion, or 36% of total foundation assets of $195 billion in 1994 (Renz, Qureshi & Mandler, 1996) .

During this period, their major area of focus appeared to be: education, which received the greatest portion of grant dollars, 29.2%; followed by human services, 15.4%; and health, 15.2% (Renz, Qureshi & Mandler, 1996). Although environment and animals appear to be somewhat on the lower end of giving, 5.1%, this level has remained constant during the last 5 years. Recent foundation grants were reviewed particularly in the critical issue areas of biodiversity, forests, climate change, energy, human capital development, agriculture and oceans. While these have not been definitive, they are indicative of how consistently foundations have responded to issues of environmental degradation. In a number of instances, although the grants may have been small, the results have been exceptionally promising and could serve as models for future targeted grants that hold the promise of strong multiplier effects. These are the Rockefeller Foundation's Energy Programme, the Climate Institute, Smart Wood, and Sustainable Northwest.

B. Select Sectors and the Challenge of Implementation Biodiversity

The biodiversity issue loomed large at the Rio Earth Summit, to the extent that the main outcome was one of the principal products of the entire Earth Summit, the Biodiversity Convention. The Convention has now been signed by 167 governments, indicating that it is a salient concern for the community of nations. Despite some action, bio-diversity continues to decline more rapidly than ever. This is due primarily to habitat loss through population pressures, poverty, and a host of exploitation activities (notably over-logging of the most species-rich environments, tropical forests) that are not directly aimed at extirpation of wildlife and species (Wilson, 1992).

Biodiversity--or rather, biodepletion--is one environmental problem that is intrinsically irreversible. When a species is gone, it is gone for good. In terms of our economic welfare, that will often be for bad, as witness the many drugs and pharmaceuticals, new and improved foods that depend on wild plants and animals (Pearce and Moran, 1994). For instance, plant-based drugs and pharmaceuticals were worth $40 billion per year in just the developed world as far back as the mid- 1980's, and probably as much in the developing world as well; plant-derived anticancer drugs in the U.S. save 30,000 lives per year (Principe, 1996).

Were the present species extinction spasm allowed to persist unchecked, the number of people obliged to live in a severely impoverished biosphere for the next 5 million years (possibly several times longer before evolution could generate replacement species) could be in the order of 500 trillion, or 100 times more people than have ever existed (Myers, 1995).

The main mode of safeguarding biodiversity is through protected areas. The amount spent by governments domestically on protected areas in 1994 (the latest year with data) was $4.1 billion. In addition, $800 million was assigned for biodiversity during the period 1994-96 by the Global Environment Facility (GEF) for an average of $267 million per year. On top of this, international NGOs spent in the order of $1 billion in 1995 (World Wildlife Fund, $290 million). The amount estimated for worldwide biodiversity conservation under Agenda 21 for the period 1993-2000 is $3.5 billion per year while the Global Biodiversity Strategy estimated conservation needs worldwide at $17 billion per year (International Union for Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources(1996; Johnston and Green, 1995).

Since UNCED, annual ODA funding for biodiversity has been lower than in the pre-GEF period of 1987 - 1991. Indeed, non-GEF biodiversity aid during 1987-1994 was only $446 million, hence massively short of what is needed for conservation worldwide (International Union for Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources, 1997). AID's funding quadrupled during 1989-92, but then dropped for the next two years.

Forests

Closely associated with biodiversity is the forests issue insofar as the great bulk of species occur in the world's forests. In fact, at least half and possibly a still higher proportion of all species occur in tropical forests alone. Fourteen of 18 "hotspots" are located in tropical forests, and measures to safeguard these "hotspots" are also measures to safeguard key forest tracts. Climate change is also tied in with forests insofar as forests contain three-fifths of all carbon on land; at the same time, deforestation is the source of perhaps one-fifth of all carbon dioxide emissions into the global atmosphere, a proportion that is rising rapidly. All the more regrettable, then, is that the Rio Earth Summit could not come up with a Forestry Convention to link up with the Biodiversity and Climate Conventions. True, Rio produced a set of 15 Forest Principles for the sustainable management of forests as the basis for further negotiations on an international forests agreement. These Principles have been much discussed in the interim, though without much substantive progress.

There have been two major institutional initiatives since Rio: the World Commission on Forests and Sustainable Development, and the Intergovernmental Panel on Forests. They are still underway, and it remains to be seen how far they will achieve. To-date, however, it seems they will largely be following the old- time religion that addresses problems arising within forests, such as careless logging, lack of protected areas, and inadequate ecological understanding of forests ecosystems. Yet many if not most depletive processes arise outside forests in the form of shifted cultivators (displaced landless peasants) in the case of tropical forests, and acid rain and global warming in the cases of temperate and boreal forests. Hence there is a premium on tackling sources rather than symptoms of forest decline. We urgently need to gain a closer grasp of the status of forests within economies whether national, regional, or global, and of those forces -- economic, political, social, etc.-- that often underlie the progressive depletion of forests. It is indicative of how far this challenge is overlooked that the conference agenda of the 1997 triennial meeting of the International Union Forestry Research Organizations gives all too little attention to these crucial factors that lie outside the realm of traditional forestry.

The challenge also seems to be sidestepped by the Intergovernmental Panel on Forests (IPF). While prompting governments to set up an International Negotiating Committee in February 1997 for a global Forest Convention, the IPF is showing itself to be deficient in two respects.

First, it does not acknowledge the underlying causes of forest depletion in whatever part of the world, calling instead for further research on issues that are already understood well enough to warrant urgent initiatives. Secondly, it does not address the question of why bodies such as FAO, ITTO, UNEP, the World Bank and many governments, have not yet got to pragmatic and productive grips with forest problems that have been well-known for at least two decades.

In light of the above, it is scarcely surprising that the Forest Principles amount to a non-binding and rather vague set of propositions. Worse, they are opposed by a good number of leading tropical-forest countries notably India and Malaysia -- and the G-77 countries as a whole have argued that if they were to accept the Forest Principles entirely, they would effectively sign away part of their sovereignty over these natural resource stocks. Although a few action initiatives have been agreed, there is no timetable established for them (unlike the case of the Climate Change and Biodiversity Conventions), and they have no legal teeth.

Climate Change

The Rio Earth Summit not only produced the Climate Convention but it established the Conference of Parties (COP) as the body responsible for implementing the framework convention. The COP goals are to "keep under regular review the implementation of the convention" by examining the "obligations of the parties, the exchange of information, and the implementation and evolution of scientific and technical knowledge".

The most recent COP meeting (COP-2) was in Geneva in July 1996 with some 140 countries represented. The meeting was marked by a dramatic shift in the former U.S. stance when Under Secretary of State Tim Wirth called for a legally binding agreement to reduce post-2000 industrialized-country emissions of CO2 and other greenhouse gases, with specific timetables and targets.

While pressure builds up for action on climate change and especially global warming, the divide between various industry groupings is growing wider. The oil and coal industry are becoming ever-more resistant to initiatives to reduce fossil fuel use, while other industry groupings are looking for stronger climate targets, and especially for market-oriented climate policies. At the Geneva meeting, the insurance industry became notably vocal. Some 60 insurers signed a statement calling on governments to reduce emissions of greenhouse gases. Insurance overall amounts to $1.4 trillion a year worldwide, or five percent of the global economy and half as much again as the oil industry. Insurers have encountered unprecedented costs in recent years through a steady increase in extreme weather phenomena. During 1990-95, the industry paid out $57 billion for weather-related losses of the type to be encountered in a greenhouse world (floods, droughts, hurricane -- ostensible portents of global warming), compared with a total of $14 billion for the entire 1980s.

The insurers' fears are well-founded. A 3 to 4 degree Centigrade rise in sea temperatures as projected by atmospheric models could increase the destructive potential of hurricanes by 50 percent. It could also lengthen the hurricane season in North America by two months or more, allowing storms to move north as far as New York City. Were a hurricane similar to Hurricane Andrew to strike Miami or New York, it could cause $50 billion in claims, or nearly one-third of the capital base of the American insurance industry. Already, insurance rates in Florida have been driven up by one fourth, and reinsurance premiums by four to five times, while reinsurers' liability has been capped. The time could soon arrive when people in high risk zones could not obtain any insurance at all (Flavin and Tunali, 1996).

Despite the fact that the insurance industry has $160 billion in surplus, just two freak weather events could eliminate $80 billion of that surplus, leaving the industry in danger of "global collapse", according to Frank Nutter, the head of the Reinsurance Association of America. Parallel expressions of concern have stemmed from the Gerling international trade. For one thing, it magnifies the environmental effects of production by expanding the market for commodities beyond national boundaries. More than one-quarter of world merchandized trade involves goods derived directly from the natural resource base that ultimately underpins all economic activities. This is notably the case with primary products such as minerals. Second, it allows countries that have depleted their own resource bases (or have passed laws protecting them) to reach beyond their borders for products, effectively shifting the environmental impacts onto other countries and communities. Third, certain national environmental laws and even some international environmental treaties are coming to be viewed as "nontariff barriers to trade", threatening efforts to enhance environmental quality within countries and to safeguard the global commons. If the potential environmental benefits of free trade are to be realized, then trading rules such as those developed by GATT must recognize that widespread environmental externalities are in effect environmental subsidies.

This all means that national governments acting alone are unable to control the environmental effects of trade, making international cooperation essential. As a result, environmental issues are now featuring strongly in ongoing trade negotiations such as those of the Uruguay Round, NAFTA, and the European Union's Maastricht Treaty. Thus trade can become a prime tool for shaping a world that is environmentally sustainable.

The trade boom has been reinforced by the revolution in information/communications technology. Daily trading on the New York Stock Exchange frequently exceeds 300 million shares, 200 times the average in the 1950s. Thanks to electronics, over $1 trillion moves around the world each day in search of investment opportunities. This is a sizable segment of the global economy's $26 trillion. While many of the funds return next day, the sum offers huge scope to affect the environment. this is notably the case when, for instance, the stock of a timber corporation is suddenly boosted by an inflow of funds, enabling it to purchase another forest holding or concession in Amazonia, Borneo or Siberia. Funds of this scale can be exceptionally influential in land-use planning and other activities affecting environments in all parts of the world.

There have been proposals to exploit the daily $1 trillion by levying a tax of e.g. one percent of turnover. The $100 billion annually garnered from what is in effect a single worldwide stock exchange could be utilized for various international purposes, including safeguards for global commons such as the atmosphere and climate, the oceans, or the planetary spectrum of species (the anticipated revenues would be twice as much as all foreign aid). It would also help to damp down the more speculative and disruptive forms of currency trading.

Energy

Closely allied to the climate sector is energy, especially with respect to new, renewable and non-polluting sources of energy as alternatives to fossil fuels. Many developed-world governments have asserted that they intend in principle to counter global warming by limiting year-2000 CO2 emissions to 1990 levels, but only half of them are likely to meet their targets without substantive policy measures to translate principle into practice. At the same time, they continue to subsidize fossil fuels ten times more than non-polluting energy sources (energy subsidies worldwide, mostly in favour of polluting fossil fuels amount to over $200 billion pe year). By contrast, India now allows a full tax deduction for renewable energy investments.

Of the various energy alternatives, a leading candidate is solar energy. Recent initiatives in technology development have resulted in dramatic improvements in efficiency and costs. But the solar prospect hinges upon critical factors of start-up economics. If the high capital costs could be temporarily offset by financial mechanisms, it would provide momentum for solar energy to become an economically competitive response to CO2 emissions and rising energy demands. As it is, the consumer's cost had fallen from more than $70 per watt in the 1970 (199 dollars) to $4 per watt today, and it is expected to drop further.

Two billion people in developing countries mostly in rural areas and far from a central power supply, lack access to electricity or other modern energy sources. Photovoltaics can provide them with services such as lighting, heating and refrigeration at cheaper cost than adding power lines. During the past ten years, silicon cells that convert sunlight into electricity have been installed in or adjacent to 250,000 homes, mostly in remote areas of countries such as China, Sri Lanka and Mexico. In Kenya in 1993, more homes were powered with solar cells than by extending the national electricity grid. In Brazil, utility companies are starting to support solar electrification in Amazonia and other areas where it is impractical to extend power lines. In South Africa, the government has initiated a major effort to supply millions of rural people with solar energy, and in Vietnam, where less than one-fifth of the country's 77 million people have electricity, the Women's Union has launched a solar electrification program (Flavin, 1996).

As a measure of solar power as a burgeoning energy source, Japan now features over 100 million solar-powered consumer electronic devices. In Norway, 50,000 homes are powered by photovoltaics. In Israel, more than 900,000 solar units heat 83 percent of the country's domestic water (Roodman and Lenssen, 1995). In Colombia, there are 30,000 solar water heaters. In Jordan, over one quarter of households use solar hot-water systems (Flavin, 1996).

Other non-polluting sources of energy are available, primarily wind, geothermal and hydro-power. While none of these enjoys the flexibility, adaptability, and portability of solar energy, they all deserve further development. Wind power has become the world's fastest growing energy market even though it currently provides less than 0.1 percent of the world's electricity. There were 23,000 turbines operating by late 1995, and output expanded from 2000 MW in 1990 to 4500 MW in 1996. The cost has fallen by more than two-thirds during the past ten years and is expected to drop to only four cents per kilowatt hour, making it the least expensive power source to date which can be developed on a large scale worldwide. In many regions, the cost is no lower than that for energy from new coal plants. India is the first developing country with a significant market for wind power, and it is second to Germany in the number of annual installations. Germany's wind power now exceeds 1000 MW, making it the world's most vigorous wind power market. India possesses the second fastest wind-power industry with 500 MW installed, while China plans on 1300 MW by the year 2000. In California, there were 1700 turbines generating enough electricity to supply all of San Francisco's residents (Flavin, 1996).

This account of energy alternatives offers much opportunity for foundations to spur the development of solar and wind power among other renewables. In addition, there is still extensive scope to meet energy needs through efficiency and conservation. Aggressive public education and advertising, promoted in part by foundations, could encourage people to reduce wasteful consumption of energy. There are lots of examples of the exceptional savings available. According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory in Golden, Colorado, a new bank headquarters in Amsterdam uses 90 percent less energy per square meter than its predecessor. It costs an extra $0.7 million to build but saves $2.4 million a year. Climate-sensitive design of buildings in the United States, using available technologies, could cut heating and cooling energy use in residential buildings by up to 70 percent, and total energy use in commercial buildings by 60 percent. Simply planting trees around some buildings can provide enough shade to cut cooling needs by as much as 30 percent (Roodman and Lenssen, 1995).

Human Capital Development

Principle 1 of the Rio Declaration on Environment and Development was emphatic in its assertion that "human beings are the center of concern to sustainable development" and as such they were entitled to a healthy and productive life in harmony with nature. This position was recently reaffirmed in the Human Development Report 1996 which reminded the world that "human development is the end and economic growth is the means" and that the purpose of growth should be to enrich peoples' lives. In too many instances, unfortunately, this was not the case. In its most recent findings, the report noted that growth has been failing about one-third of the world's peoples in about a hundred countries for much of the last 15 years, and that lopsided development likewise had been failing to preserve the link between growth and human development with good growth and little or no human development in some instances and good human development but little or no growth in others. For economic growth to be successful, however, and to genuinely advance the human cause, much more attention will have to be given to the structure and quality of growth in order to ensure that it is directed to supporting human development, reducing poverty, protecting the environment and ensuring the quality of life.

Agriculture

In its projection of humanity's agricultural future, Agenda 21 estimates that, viewed against the background of a global population of 8.5 billion people by the year 2025, 83% of whom will be living in developing countries, "the capacity of available resources and technology to satisfy the demands of this growing population for food and other agricultural commodities remain uncertain". To meet the challenge, agriculture will be obliged to utilize land already in use and avoid further encroachment of land that is only marginally suitable for cultivation. To this end, major adjustments in agricultural policies are needed to create the conditions necessary for sustainable agricultural and rural development (SARD). Such policies will be successful, however, only to the degree that they, "involve education initiatives, utilization of economic incentives, and the development of appropriate new technologies thus ensuring stable supplies of nutritionally adequate food, access to those supplies by vulnerable groups and the production for market, employment and income generation to alleviate poverty and natural resource management and environmental protection"7. Seven hundred million people are chronically malnourished. This number and other figures have led to projections that food production may in fact have to triple to provide adequate nutrition for all. Compounding troubling population and environmental trends is a growing acknowledgment that the Green Revolution of the 1960s to the 1980s -- which provided reassuring evidence of the power of agricultural science -- was stalling.

Oceans

In the development of a comprehensive program for the protection of the seas and oceans of our planet, Earth Summit leaders were unanimous in their affirmation that the "marine environment formed an integral whole that is an essential component of the global life support system and a positive asset that presents opportunities for sustainable development". This reality was stated even more bluntly by the Brundtland Commission in a report to the United Nations five years earlier which argued that "...in the earth's wheel of life, the oceans provide the balance". And that balance is now being threatened in almost all seas and oceans and must be quickly reset if the vital ecological and economic services they perform are to be maintained.

In perhaps the clearest popular account of just how far and how fast that balance is being upset, Michael Specter8 noted that "The last decade has not been easy on the world's oceans, the largest and seemingly least vulnerable resource on earth".

Among the most discouraging trends are giant tumors on turtles, PCBs in the flesh of fish, amnesic shellfish poisoning of humans by algae blooms - many of these were unknown 30 years ago. And while there is no significantly certain way to prove a pattern, or to assert that human beings are to blame, the deterioration has escalated notably in the last decade. Autopsies on beluga whales -- sometimes called the most polluted animal on earth - regularly find ulcers, cancers, immune deficiencies and lung infections that may be caused by the mercury, lead, PCBs and other toxins that quietly accumulate in their blubber every time they swallow a fish from the rivers of Europe or America.

Specter then noted that even the essential chemical balance at many river mouths has changed as nitrogen-rich fertilizers and acid rain have increased while fresh water is siphoned off by dams and development. As the water's composition changes, so does the sea's intricate food chain -- starting with plankton, and ending with people.

Other dimensions of the crisis are becoming even more manifest as coral reefs, one of the planet's most productive ecosystems, are dying at an alarming rate. Numerous factors are to blame for the coral reef's die-off, including "ocean pollution from coastal run-off" and gradual warming of the world's oceans due to the greenhouse effect. But recent reports suggest that fish collectors, especially in the South Pacific and Southeast Asia are among those causing the most damage to the reefs. These fish hunters often use sodium cyanide which is dumped in the water to stun the fish which are then shipped alive to restaurants and collectors throughout Asia. Sodium cyanide, which is toxic, destroys the reefs' micro- organisms as well as cause numerous health problems among fishermen. It is now estimated that as much as 80% of the Philippines' coral reefs have been damaged by sedimentation, explosive and toxic pollution.

At another level, the scope of the crisis which has even more immediate implications for human society was further elaborated by the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) which noted that as much as one-third of 200 fisheries which it monitors world-wide are being depleted or being over-fished. Moreover, throughout the world, fishcatch is in a steady state of decline; 1988 being the last year when the catch topped 100 million tons. And whereas a variety of factors have contributed to the current situation, a key element may be the surplus of fishing boats, plus more efficient fishery techniques and such advanced technologies as sonar, and the use of factory ships as well as substantial subsidies.

According to a recent Business Week report, since 1989 "...too many boats chasing too few fish have kept the world's fishing fleets operating at a loss despite $54 billion in annual subsidies". Moreover, the subsidies have accelerated the expansion of fishing vessels which now total some 1.2 million boats, a 30% increase over 1975 levels. The implications of this dramatic expansion may best be observed in Alaska where it now takes the 5,000 or so boats that fish the halibut just 30 hours to catch their limit versus 120 days in the mid-70s.9

Clearly, such practices are unsustainable and pose serious ecological, economic and social problems for many nations especially in regions that are dependent upon fishery, and could very well become a source of tension, if not conflict, among states.

Even before the United Nations Conference on Environment and Development in Rio de Janeiro several U.S. foundations had assumed a catalytic role in promoting global environmental protection.

Smart Wood Program

Background: Initiated in 1989, Smart Wood, a program supported by grants from Ford Foundation, MacArthur Foundation and Sequoia Foundation, is the first and largest forestry certification program in existence. Smart Wood is a program of the Rainforest Alliance, an international nonprofit environmental group based in New York City. Though the program initially focused on tropical forests, today Smart Wood works in all forest types worldwide, Smart Wood is managed by a headquarters staff of experienced forestry specialists based at Rainforest Alliance offices in New York and Vermont, in collaboration with the growing number of cooperating nonprofit organizations in the Smart Wood Network (see below).

The purpose of Smart Wood is to provide independent, objective evaluation of forest management practices, forest products, timber sources and companies, enabling the public to identify products and practices that do not destroy forests. Through certification, and use of the Smart Wood label, the program provides a commercial incentive for forest managers to adopt sustainable forest practices. Smart Wood certifies forest products that come from "sustainable" or well-managed" forests ("sources"). Candidate sources may include a natural forest, a plantation, a large commercial operation or a small- scale community project. Smart Wood also certifies companies that process, manufacture or sell products made from certified wood, through "chain of custody" certification.

Forest Monitoring for Carbon Offset, Environmental Protection, Financial Investments, and Other Purposes: Smart Wood also evaluates forestry operations for other purposes, such as determining a project's compliance with government or donor environmental standards or assessing the suitability of a project for investments that are conditioned upon the use of sustainable forest management practices. Smart Wood is also monitoring "forestry carbon offset" programs. In forestry carbon offsets, investments in the conservation and management of forest are made by companies such as electric utilities in recognition of the forest's value as a carbon sink for offsetting carbon dioxide emissions. Since November 1992 Smart Wood has participated in designing and implementing a field monitoring program for the first natural forest management carbon offset project in the world in Sabah, Malaysia, as a service to an electric utility company (New England Energy Services, Inc.) The project is analyzing the benefits of such initiatives and refining techniques for field monitoring and verification of benefits. The Smart Wood program is also engaged in dialogue with governmental bodies (e.g., White House, United Nations), companies and other organizations concerned with developing policies that encourage balanced and credible carbon offset efforts in both the energy and forestry sectors.

Smart Wood Network: To facilitate credible and low-cost evaluation, monitoring and certification in all forest types, Smart Wood has developed the Smart Wood Network, composed of collaborating nonprofit organizations throughout the world. This constantly expanding network has staff representatives in Central and South America (Bolivia, Brazil, Costa Rica, Guatemala, Honduras, Mexico), six regions of the United States (California, Florida, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Vermont), and expanding coverage in Europe, Southeast Asia and Africa. A complete list of current Smart Wood representatives can be obtained through Smart Wood headquarters. Network representatives offer the complete range of Smart Wood services, including forest and chain of custody certification and access to the internationally recognized Smart Wood label. The advantages of certification through the non-profit Smart Wood Network include:

  • high credibility and acceptance with local environmental groups, scientists, government and forest industry; thus reducing environmental or corporate risk and fostering positive local relationships;
  • low cost certification and monitoring services;
  • immediate, local access to multilingual representatives of the Smart Wood program;
  • access to, and understanding of, certified forest products markets worldwide; and
  • international coverage and representation.
Sustainable Northwest:
Human Capital, Community Development and Sustainable Resource Management

The mission of Sustainable Northwest, a small, non-profit NGO, is to strengthen local capacity to promote environmentally sound economic development in communities of the Pacific Northwest (in the U.S.). To fulfil this mission, Sustainable Northwest has devised a three-part strategy:

  1. Partnerships for Community Sustainability -- Engage rural communities in economically viable and environmentally sustainable projects that strengthen their self-reliance, long-term vitality and commitment to place and people.
  2. Information and Tools for Sustainable Communities -- Share ideas, examples, expertise and technologies that help build local, regional and national understanding of the linkages between economy, ecosystem, and community.
  3. Regional and Systemic Change -- Facilitate communication, learning and development of cooperative solutions between communities, agencies, interest groups, and the non-profit and private sectors.

Sustainable Northwest is currently focusing its efforts in the Interior Columbia River Basin, a vast and diverse region where soil, water, forest, wildlife and mineral-resources drew settlers and fuelled extractive industry development. More recent arrivals however, have increasingly influenced resource utilization policies and challenged traditional lifestyle values. The resulting tensions can either create endless conflict or catalyze new collaborative efforts that build on the energy, determination, self-reliance and creativity of a diverse citizen.

The operating premise of Snw is that local communities can and should become effective stewards of the land, in partnership with agencies and national interest groups. But too often rural communities are singled-out as the cause of poor land and ecosystem conditions and treated as the problem rather than as potential conservation allies. Engaging the constructive energies of rural residents, SNw assists them to identify locally-grounded solutions to both environmental and economic challenges by offering direct support to economic development projects that stress responsible stewardship of natural resources.

Three encouraging examples SNW projects include:

  1. Wallowa County, Oregon -- In this its first project, SNw explored environmentally sound economic development opportunities in the forestry sector. With the aim of promoting forest and watershed health, a task force was formed to prepare landowners to participate in sustainable forest management and watershed restoration activities, build on the example of exemplary land stewards in the area, develop local sustainable forestry standards, and encourage cooperative approaches to land management. Other job opportunities exist in land restoration activities and in developing tourism options for the region. The final objective of this effort is to help build community understanding through stakeholder dialogues and the development of interest groups.
  2. Okanogan County, Washington -- A group of local organic farmers are exploring the potential of developing a shared processing kitchen facility. With local partners, SNw developed a needs assessment to gauge interest in using such a facility. In addition, stakeholder meetings have been convened by SNw to help residents debate alternative uses of the resources of the Okanogan National Forest.
  3. Council, Idaho -- A small, one industry town on the border of the State, Council was devastated when a large wood processing mill was closed. SNw was able to facilitate the transfer of the mill to the town. The original owner has agreed to finance the clean-up efforts and the town will use the developed site to attract new business, creating up to an estimated 30 jobs.

IV. Conclusions

In evaluating the response of major U.S. foundations to the UNCED meeting in general and Agenda 21 in particular, it is important to emphasize that many foundations had been responding to environment/development issues years before UNCED and these pioneering initiatives continue to contribute to the implementation of Agenda 21. Although the concept of sustainable development received wide currency since the Brundtland Commission commended it in 1987, the term itself had been used before in the World Conservation Strategy prepared by IUCN, UNEP, and WWF in 1980.

The Consultative Group on Biological Diversity (CGBD), consisting of forty foundations and the U.S. Agency for International Development, has been in existence since 1987. This consortium supports foundation activity in the field of biodiversity conservation, and serves as the nexus for its members. Its 1996 working group program areas were: forests, conservation of marine biodiversity, systemic contaminants, and conservation & development.

Similar consortia could be established to support foundation activity in other Agenda 21 sectors, i.e. climate change, population, etc. The goals of consortia like CGBD could be to: create partnerships among funders; improve the quality of grant-making; increase the quantity of grant-making; identify gaps in current funding patterns; target priority subjects and geographic regions, and broaden the public constituency.

In 1988, The Rockefeller Foundation pointed out that it was important to integrate concern for the global environment with the international and technical dimensions of their Science-Based Development programs in agricultural, health, and population sciences with an emphasis on developing nations, and to insist that the issues of development and the environment are structured as part of one overall framework. This set the stage for their Global Environmental Program that was launched in 1989. In 1991 two major initiatives were established: (1) Leadership for Environment and Development (LEAD) and (2) the International Energy Initiative (IEI), both focused on developing countries.

The MacArthur Foundation initiated the World Environment and Resources Program in 1989 to underscore the Foundation's commitment to sustainable development in regions where large numbers of people depend on local natural resources for survival.

Again -- prior to UNCED -- the Ford Foundation through Rural Poverty and Resources and other programs supported research on the links between economic and ecological concerns as well as activities to strengthen public and private institutions working to advance environmentally sound economic development. The Foundation also sought to encourage ties among local, regional and national environmental organizations. It supported the design and replication of innovations leading to more equitable allocation of such resources as land and water, and funded projects intended to advance knowledge of the global dimensions of sustainable development.

The Kellogg Foundation supported agriculture, education, health, leadership and youth (all important sectors of Agenda 21) before UNCED.

Another pre-Rio initiative to encourage a more integrated approach was the establishment of The Energy Foundation by the Rockefeller, MacArthur, and Pew Foundations, to assist in the transition to a sustainable energy future by promoting energy efficiency and renewable energy in the U.S. Similar collaborative initiatives could respond to other Agenda 21 sectors in order to help operationalize sustainable development at the national and local level and support the requirement that global sustainable development policies and practices be based on sustainability policies and practices at the national and local level not the other way around.

In 1991, the Andrew Mellon Foundation was supporting programs in Conservation and the Environment and Population. Also pre-Rio, the Jessie Smith Noyes Foundation was focused on tropical ecology, water and toxics, sustainable agriculture, and population and reproductive rights. It is significant that these foundations recognized the interrelatedness of areas like environment and population and the need to protect natural systems and ensure a sustainable society before UNCED.

It is also important to underscore that U.S. foundations contributed to the UNCED meeting in a variety of ways.

In 1991, the Ford Foundation supported projects designed to facilitate the participation of women, indigenous peoples, non-governmental organizations and developing countries in the discussions leading up to UNCED. The Foundation also supported a study of the challenges to the international system posed by global environment problems, the preparation of the Brundtland Commission for UNCED and efforts to raise the press coverage of the Conference.

The MacArthur Foundation in 1991 supported roundtable meetings and the publication of policy papers with the goal of securing agreement among participating nations for the signing of the U.N.'s 1982 Law of the Sea Convention.

A. Criteria or Indicators for Evaluation

Two categories of criteria or indicators are recommended to evaluate the response of the U.S. philanthropic sector to the UNCED meeting in general and Agenda 21 in particular: (1) process and (2) content.

Process Criteria or Indicators and Selected Examples

Some examples of how foundations have successfully applied the recommended criteria or indicators follow.

Simplify the processes and principles involved with making sustainable development work, i.e. contribute to the development of a core knowledge base and a standardized language

Kendall Foundation. Scientists and economists representing five continents focused on where agricultural research and technology development should be focused to find solutions to two key questions: How can we feed a world population that is expected to double before leveling off? and How can we produce sufficient food without substantial environmental harm?

Help operationalize sustainable development, particularly at the local and national levels

The Ford Foundation's Conservation-Based Community Development Initiative supports community economic development and resource conservation by helping to create jobs that sustain the ecosystem.

A broad base of external support, including Ford Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, and Sequoia Foundation, underlies the training efforts of the Rainforest Alliance and the Mexican Council for Sustainable Silviculture in providing the world's first formal training course in environmental forestry certification -- starting with twenty professionals from eight Latin American countries -- to meet the need for local certification auditors.

Develop collaborative initiatives on sustainable development that involve two or more organizations tackling the same problem, but from different perspectives, to create new approaches

"Defining Sustainable Communities", a project of the Tides Foundation with support from 16 other foundations, is committed to creating a new collaborative model based on the belief that environment, community development, social justice and health are integrally linked.

Develop collaborative sustainable development initiatives inhouse with program staff from different areas working together to learn from each other

Peace and International Cooperation, Population, and World Environment and Resources -- three programs of the MacArthur Foundation -- developed Collaborative Studies on Human Societies and Environmental Change.

Designate a special program area for cross-cutting initiatives that embody the sustainability category The Rockefeller Foundation's Global Environment Division continues two major initiatives: (1) Leadership for Environment and Development (LEAD) -- to build human capital for leadership that will help preserve the environment, promote fair and sustainable development, and forge international agreements to accomplish these ends -- and (2) E&Co., a nonprofit international energy service to facilitate the transition to a new, sustainable-energy paradigm by promoting energy enterprises in developing countries.

The John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation's World Environment and Resource Program supports conservation, public education, policy studies, and sustainable development projects relating to key environmental issues in the tropics. The program seeks to increase understanding of the delicate relationship between the health of the biosphere and the welfare of human communities. With its focus on biodiversity the program links environmental concerns and economic needs, conservation and commerce.

The Rockefeller Brothers Fund's One World: Sustainable Resource Use Program fosters environmental stewardship which is ecologically based, economically sound, culturally appropriate, and sensitive to questions of intergenerational equity. Strategies on climate change focus on utility-based energy efficiency, renewable energy, transportation, and green taxes; strategies on biodiversity preservation focus on an ecosystem approach with an emphasis on temperate rainforests and fishery and coastal zone management.

Additional process criteria/indicators include: (1) promote considerable cooperation among different stakeholders to achieve sustainability; (2) draw previously marginalized people into policy debates, leading to the formulation and implementation of more responsible environmental and economic policies; (3) utilize up-to-date, reliable non-technical information that incorporates different perspectives on achieving sustainability; (4) facilitate the ability to make choices based on an understanding of the trade-offs, alternatives, and resulting consequences of achieving sustainability; (5) create a separate long-term funding program for sustainability projects to increase the time horizon for determining the success of a project; (6) develop a separate "risk" fund for innovative projects that attempt innovative approaches; and (7) reconsider blanket prohibitions on conferences or meetings on sustainable development.

Content Criteria/Indicators and Selected Examples
Focus on conservation of biodiversity

In light of the declining prospect for biodiversity, there is all the greater need for supplementary funds, especially those that can make incisive contributions. This means there is a unique challenge for U.S. foundations. In 1995 foundations that generated most funding for biodiversity worldwide were MacArthur, followed by--on a far smaller scale -- W. Alton Jones and Rockefeller Brothers Fund, with a total of a little over $20 million, of which the great bulk was for biodiversity in developing countries.

One way of tackling priorities is to focus on "hotspots". These are areas that (a) feature exceptional numbers of species with exceptional levels of endemism, and (b) face exceptional threat of habitat destruction. As many as 20 percent of Earth's plant species and a still higher proportion of animal species are confined to 18 hot spots comprising 0.5 percent of Earth's land surface (Myers, 1990). This finding allows conservation planners to concentrate on areas where investment will yield the highest payoff.

The hotspots opportunity has been addressed by a remarkable initiative on the part of the MacArthur Foundation. In 1989 its World Environment and Resources Program embarked on a major effort to safeguard hotspots. Thus far the foundation has funded conservation activities in virtually all the 18 hotspots cited (plus a few others), working mostly through on-ground NGOs. Total funds disbursed from 1989 through 1996 amounted to some $150 million, for a rough average of almost $19 million per year. This should surely rank as one of the most substantive and closely targeted of all biodiversity investments since the start-up of conservation efforts forty years ago.

Support forest-related issues

The World Environment and Resources program of The John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation supported the Tropical Forest Foundation's efforts to promote sustained yield forest management projects that demonstrate low-impact logging techniques in Brazil's Amazon basin with an $180,000 grant in 1995.

The World Wildlife Fund received a $1.1 million grant in 1995 from The Pew Charitable Trusts to help protect and conserve British Columbia's threatened ancient forests and wilderness areas.

There are other forms of forestry support that lend themselves to foundation input: for example, the need for grassroots involvement. There is an important role for U.S. foundations to support smaller-sized projects which otherwise cannot obtain funds from large donors and especially official sources.

Another way for foundations to make an incisive intervention is to support research that could identify key factors such as "deforestation fronts" in tropical forests (plus possibly Siberia). Through a marginal amount of research funding, the Joint Research Centre under the European Commission has established that almost half of all tropical deforestation occurs in just 14 fronts comprising less than one quarter of all tropical forests (Myers, 1993). As with biodiversity hotspots, this opens the way for responses on the part of forestry agencies of the European Union in conjunction with the World Bank, the Food and Agriculture Organization, NGOs, etc. The research also demonstrates that deforestation is primarily due to nine factors (population pressures, poverty, rural infrastructure, etc.), that collectively allow development of "early warning" criteria for future foci of deforestation--thus enabling policy makers, land-use planners and forestry experts to engage in preventative measures. This is precisely the sort of imaginative and pioneering research that could represent an unusually productive contribution by U.S. foundations.

Support climate change issues

Several foundations have already been quite active in this sphere and their contributions had profound impact and far-reaching implications ranging from cabinet-level briefings, pioneering studies on regional and national impacts and outlining the principles on which the Convention is based. One area where urgent and imaginative work remains to be done is consensus-building for national climate policies. So far, few governments can claim anything approaching a national consensus supportive of the Climate Convention, and even fewer seem likely to achieve the goal of reducing greenhouse emissions to 1990 levels by the year 2000.

In the United Kingdom, sizable shifting from natural coal to gas, a more economic as well as greenhouse benign energy source, has made achieving these greenhouse targets possible. In Germany, significant reductions seem possible due to the closing of many energy inefficient industrial facilities in the former East Germany and the initiation of aggressive efficiency measures on a country wide basis. Most other industrial countries are likely by 2000 to have emissions significantly exceeding year 1990 levels with U.S. emissions mirroring this trend. Emissions are growing much more rapidly in developing countries.

It would be timely for foundations to support meetings of key stakeholders in such areas as energy, transportation and agriculture to work on the development of national climate policies. For instance, The Rockefeller Foundation together with the Moriah Fund has put substantial sums into work of the Climate Institute, including an assessment of the scope for alternatives to fossil-fuel energy such as biofuels, plus a series of minister-level briefings in developing countries on climate change.

The Third Convention of the Parties (COP3) of the Climate Change Convention to be held at Kyoto, Japan in December 1997 would seem to provide a unique and significant opportunity for foundations to contribute meaningfully to the implementation process and perhaps place the U.S. in a more strategic position to exercise leadership and exert influence based on a solid foundation of public support. On 17 January 1997, the U.S. government submitted to the conference secretariat a proposal for an emissions protocol that would set up binding emissions levels for industrialized countries that choose to ratify the protocol. Patterned after the mid-1980s EPA rule-making on lead in gasoline and the 1990 U.S.

Clean Air Amendments permitting CO2 trading, the U.S. proposal would seek to reduce emissions mitigation costs by permitting trading among parties to the protocol, allowing them to bank excess emissions reductions and to borrow from future years. To increase flexibility, the proposal would permit the binding targets to be yearly averages over periods as long as a decade. Despite the intellectual elegance of the recent U.S. proposal, it may have three drawbacks.

The most fundamental is that there seems little likelihood that such a protocol, if approved at COP3, would receive the necessary two-thirds majority in the U.S. Senate required for U.S. ratification. In the judgement of most observers including key Administration officials, a binding emissions protocol might capture a bare majority of votes in the U.S. but would, for the next several years, have little possibility of achieving the two-thirds vote required for U.S. ratification. Refusal of the U.S., the world's largest emitter of greenhouse emissions, to ratify an emissions protocol might convert it to nullify much as the U.S. refusal to participate in the League of Nations led to the failure of that body. Secondly, the long time period before parties would be clearly out of compliance if they exceeded negotiated target levels, about 20 to 30 years from now, is so well into the future that it may do little to affect corporate strategy energy investment decisions. Third, the U.S. proposal does relatively little to encourage developing countries, the likeliest source of most emissions growth, to limit greenhouse emissions.

To minimize the possibility of a breakdown in climate negotiations, it is important to work on several fronts. An essential precondition for U.S. action to support further emissions reductions is a wide scale public recognition of the potential implications of climate change for the U.S. and of the role of U.S. energy consumption in contributing to global climate change.

Success of a global greenhouse emissions limitation strategy will also depend heavily on an active commitment to energy efficiency and expanded reliance on greenhouse benign energy by key developing countries. To this end, it is helpful that planners and decision makers in developing countries are aware of the implications of climate change for their countries and of the potential of cost effective energy efficiency and renewable measures to limit their greenhouse emissions.

To achieve these changed perceptions in the U.S. and in developing countries, the philanthropic community should undertake some highly focused initiatives prior to COP3 including the development and placement on the Internet of a guide to policymakers on climate change implications.

B. Recommendations

Additional suggestions for improving the effectiveness of the initiative of the U.S. foundations in relation to the UNCED mandate follow.

Provision of auspices for exploring new and creative ways of financing the sustainable development agenda.

One of the most urgent issues to be addressed is the question of financing. This was the subject of a recently held U.N. expert meeting in Santiago, Chile that drew some very sobering conclusions, particularly in connection with the status of the Overseas Development Assistance (ODA) Programme that shows very significant signs of decline and may even be reduced for lack of political will and changing global interests and priorities of the major donors. Some have even suggested that ODA may have served the interests of the Cold War and not development and with the Cold War now over, the reason for its being may likewise have disappeared. This is obviously the pessimistic view. Still, looking at the progressive decline of ODA, the U.N. is now beginning to wonder whether alternative modes of financing should not now be aggressively pursued.

In this connection U.S. foundations have a new and even more significant role to play. Because of their respected status in society, the composition of their boards, with strong linkages to money and power and to new as well as old wealth generators, they are in a unique position to provide auspices for the convening of high-level meetings of financial and industrial leaders to explore the question, "how to generate the resources necessary for financing Agenda 21?"

Although the question of creating new ways of financing various U.N. agendas and action plans have occurred with almost tedious frequency, most of the discussions have been held under governmental auspices to which select experts have been invited. As the question moves again to center stage, foundations may find it timely to join the argument, not with technical experts as such but with the real money- makers and the people who make wealth happen -- bankers, brokers and business leaders. Such meetings convened under foundations' auspices could look at Agenda 21 not only as another government-driven perspective but also as a set of challenges to their own futures and the future of their enterprises as well as their own livelihood.

Promoting business leadership in sustainable development

As a result of diverse activities and changes, there are rapidly evolving opportunities for foundations to leverage innovation. For example, given that we are on the verge of a period of profound experimentation, there is a potential role for longitudinal research involving the monitoring -- and in some cases, the helping -- of companies and even entire industry sectors as they begin to make the sustainability transition. The goal should be to watch for barriers or constraints that are interrupting the progress of a significant number of companies, and to identify ways in which business in conjunction with governments and other actors can address these problems.

Also, foundations could help evaluate the emerging area of industrial ecology. This applies particularly to industrial symbioses when several companies develop characteristics of an industrial ecosystem, exchanging effluents and energy emissions that might otherwise go to waste.

In addition, foundations can complement their grantmaking activities through their investment and fiduciary activities. This applies with respect to three notable areas: screened portfolios, venture-related investment, and active shareholder involvement. The Jessie Smith Noyes Foundation, for instance, has made small, direct investments in companies that make enzyme-based cleaners, leak detection systems, and shoes from recycled materials, as well as in an energy services company and a person-to-person marketer of non-toxic cleaning and personal care products (Viederman and Tasch, 1995)

Mobilizing and energizing civil society

Clearly, as the 20th Century draws to a close, two features would seem to be manifest. One, the globalization of the democratic spirit; and two, the rise of the citizen's sector of the civil society. Increasingly, citizens are beginning to take action in defining their future and determining their destiny and well-being. They have a very critical role to play in securing the natural resources on which their community rests and in developing effective programs of action for sustainability. It is recommended that foundations look very closely at how they might support civil society and empower them to participate in the work of sustainable development and the implementation of Agenda 21.

Mobilizing the religious and faith communities in a new mission for the earth

All the world's main religions have powerful teachings on how we should care for the world, albeit coming from very different understandings and leading to different goals. There is no other major social issue upon which all faiths so readily agree nor upon which they have all undertaken such major plans.

In the growing worldwide movement of religious-environmental alliances, the Global Forum of Spiritual and Parliamentary Leaders on Human Survival is the largest and most multi-representative, in addition to being the most celebrated because of its orientation, its distinguished membership, and its major global conferences. The International Coordinating Committee on Religion and the Earth (ICCRE) is a unique consultative body of representatives from the world's major spiritual traditions and environmental groups established "to foster the relationship between religion and ecology and to address the spiritual dimension of the ecological crisis imperiling the earth. Its Earth Charter, inspired the only NGO document written for UNCED to reach negotiation stage. This document came so close to being adopted that ICCRE was encouraged to continue its international work on the Earth Charter toward possible adoption by the U.N. as a "declaration of principals and bill of rights for all life on earth."

As a result of the interfaith collaborations, four major faith groups and denominations have established an unprecedented partnership, the National Religious Partnership for the Environment, to better reach and educate a membership of over 100 million Americans on environmental justice, individual and corporate responsibility, and deeper moral commitment to help change environmental values.

Perhaps the time has come now for all religions to consider a new mission - that is a mission of restoration and renewal for an ailing earth. It should not be too difficult for religious leaders to grasp since most faith considers Earth to be a manifestation of the work of God, and that as creatures we have a responsibility to ensure that this work is not degraded as a result of our careless and irresponsible stewardship. One popular philosopher perhaps best summed it up when he said, "The Earth is God's gift to Man; how we treat the Earth is Man's gift to God". This was later paraphrased by a student as "The Earth is God's gift to Man; how we treat the Earth and each other is Man's gift to God." Perhaps religious institutions may indeed wish to look anew at their mission to see how far it would include stewardship of the Earth as part of its mission. Foundations might serve as an effective catalyst in encouraging the development of such mission.

Convening "implementation summits"

One of the most consistent features of the United Nations is its power as convenor. For nearly two decades, it has convened a number of conferences which have yielded action plans, and Agenda 21 is one such plan of action. Unfortunately, the question of implementation as a systematic global effort has remained elusive. Perhaps the time has indeed arrived for us to reconsider "implementation summits" whereby governments would take time to review some of these plans systematically and creatively with a view to determining what are or would be required to ensure effective implementation. Foundations could contribute significantly to launching such a process by commissioning a systematic examination of all action plans from Stockholm to Rio with a view to establishing their place on the sustainable agenda and the prospects for implementation through collective collaboration and effective strategies. It seems ironic that after nearly 20 years of conferencing, few of us have a clear idea of the status of implementation of all these plans of action. The Earth Summit may therefore provide a catalyst for undertaking this important analysis.

Marketing of Agenda 21

Agenda 21 suffers from the fact that whereas it has become a popular label, it is virtually unknown even among senior government officials in many nations. Part of the difficulty here is that there is yet to be published a truly readable text and one that engages the mind as well as the attention. Perhaps this is where the foundations might make a significant contribution by supporting efforts to effectively market Agenda 21.

Possibilities include more effective utilization of the arts, entertainment and advertising. Foundations have traditionally and consistently supported the arts.10 Many are committed to making arts and culture an active part of the daily lives of people and community, and have invested in a number of programmes to that end. Given the fact, however, that art is a universal medium, an effective medium for transmitting messages, it is deemed unfortunate that it is not more widely utilized in the sustainable development revolution.11 This is perhaps an area where foundation giving might consider an additional focus using the powerful medium of art to help develop a new sense of caring for the earth and the values for a sustainable lifestyle.

Markets are more than just centers for economic transactions for goods and services. Values are also in transaction - and what better place to transmit environmental values than in the marketplace where the consumer can indeed exert influence if not exercise power. An environmentally responsible consumer may yet turn out to be our best guarantor for environmental sustainability, and advertising can do much to influence attitudes and change behavior. This was explicitly recognized in Chapter 36 of Agenda 21 which urged countries and the United Nations system to mobilize their experience in shaping public behavior and consumption patterns and making wide use of their methods. Although a number of foundations have already supported a wide variety of media and public education campaigns, the advertising community have not yet been sufficiently mobilized to have the necessary impact in the consumers' sphere. Perhaps under foundations' sponsoring, "advertising for sustainability" campaigns could be developed.

Education for sustainable development

Education, public awareness, and training are linked to virtually all areas in Agenda 21, especially the ones on meeting basic needs, capacity-building, data and information, science, and the role of major groups. As we move into the twenty-first century, the need for a basic understanding of how the natural and cultural environments are totally interrelated is paramount to our ability to maintain a sustainable environment.

The telecommunications revolution and the "information highway" that has resulted have provided unprecedented opportunities. The ability of computers to model complex systems, combined with the power of interactive multimedia, can bring users a wealth of interrelated information in a format that is at once understandable, exciting and engaging. The integration of text, graphics, video, audio, still images, and simulations provide a unique and powerful educational methodology for interrelating disciplines.

What is needed is a World Wide Web site that addresses the range and complexity of the interrelated nature of all human activities and the environment for both policymakers and the general public. This site requires an accessible, structured database that can be maintained, updated, and expanded. Such a database exists based on twenty-five key topics organized to illustrate how the natural and cultural environments are totally interrelated in a sustainable world. The Encyclopedia of the Environment published by Houghton Mifflin Company for The Rene Dubos Center for Human Environments contains 550 nontechnical, alphabetically arranged, concise entries, covering critical concepts, basic terms, problems, and issues written by over 300 eminent scientists, scholars, researchers, policy analysts, and economists for the general reader. The entries include succinct definitions, interpretive essays, selected biographies, graphs, figures, tables, and maps. In this way the site can utilize up-to-date, reliable, nontechnical information that incorporates different perspectives on achieving sustainability.

The prototype for such a website has been initiated with support from the National Fish and Wildlife Foundation, Exxon Corporation, United Technologies Corporation and others. Entitled "EarthLine" it responds to the need for balanced perspectives and authoritative information to help individuals contribute to the development of positive, effective solutions to environmental problems and issues involved with sustainable development. "E2Zone" is a corresponding site for young people on EarthLine. There is a wealth of specialized information to be found on the World Wide Web. By hyperlinking to relevant sites, EarthLine can integrate appropriate materials into interactive learning experiences to meet the needs of stakeholders worldwide. Establishing and maintaining EarthLine would be an ideal collaborative initiative for the Environmental Grantmakers Association (EGA).

Indigenous people: the case for special attention

A number of foundations have already began to address and support indigenous peoples, particularly those who are living in critical enviroo attract private funds to share the cost of projects.

There are a number of key areas to which foundation support might give a new vitality to this provision. For example, it has been proposed that this should be made the Decade of the Indigenous Peoples and yet the world is none the wiser. How can foundations assist in promoting the Decade, its objectives, and perhaps educating national societies on what their responsibilities might be? It has also been proposed that a special unit might be created at United Nations for indigenous peoples. This too remains to be addressed.

More importantly, there are a number of outstanding issues which might be looked at immediately. One initiative was undertaken by UNEP and the Indigenous Development Institute (INDI) at Cambridge University as a model. At the initiative of UNEP and under the auspices of Chapter 26 of Agenda 21, the Haudenosaunee people were encouraged to review for themselves the critical environmental issues that affected their reservations and territories with the view to defining a Restoration Strategy for Native Lands. This has produced a unique report on the state of the Haudenosaunee environment, the issues which call for much more energetic action by the governments and states in which these territories are located, and greater recognition and support by the international community. A budget for implementing the Haudenosaunee Plan of Action has also been produced. Because of the amount of work that has been put in so far, foundations could review this program and similar such programs to see how they could redirect resources or facilitate the engendering of new resources for this purpose.

Risk-taking, innovations and flexibility

Foundations should develop a separate "risk" fund for innovative projects that attempt innovative approaches. Many may fail, but the few that succeed will provide powerful models for broader implementation.

Activating relevant constituencies

It is being suggested here that far from looking at Agenda 21 abstractly and amorphously, it may be useful to take a look at key provisions and identify the strategic constituencies that may be most be suitable for propelling the implementation process.

For example, the medical community and health-givers would seem most relevant to Chapter 6 (Health and environmental issues). Likewise, for Chapter 28, youth organizations might be encouraged to join the process of implementation. We can cite, for instance, an outstanding example where, at the initiative of UNEP and with the support of a number of U.N. agencies, a small NGO called Peace Child International was invited to re-write Agenda 21 in an idiom, language and style that is familiar to youth. The result, "Rescue Mission: Planet Earth", remains to-date the most popular and perhaps the most readable version of Agenda 21.

Consensus-building at national and global levels

Only in very few countries is there any broad-based consensus in support of Agenda 21. Many governments have made commitments and even assumed obligations, as for example in the case of climate change and the biodiversity conventions, but have been unable to develop implementing policies due to lack of public support. Only in a few countries have there been serious national debates on Agenda 21. This would be an area where foundations could make a contribution by hosting workshops, meetings, or seminars on Agenda 21 with a view to educating the public on its meaning and message, and what they could do to engage the policy process in terms of implementation.

Cabinet-level briefings

In the past, there have been some successful attempts to hold cabinet level briefings, and similar attempts may be undertaken more systematically under the auspices of say, the Commission on Sustainable Development, with the support of the foundations to host periodic meetings of cabinet members where top experts may be invited to address issues of sustainability and Agenda 21. For example, in 1990 a Rockefeller Foundation grant of $100,000 provided the seed money for heads of states and ministerial briefings which were held in 22 countries in 1991 and 1992 and helped significantly to solidify developing countries' support for the Climate Change Convention. But the foundation's grant contributed more than just financial support, it also provided a kind of cachet which enabled the Climate Institute to exert some leverage in lining up other resources for this ambitious undertaking.

Toward a new philanthropic diplomacy

Foundations are now embarking on new areas of promoting bi-national cooperation within ecologically designed frameworks where governments will otherwise seem to be reluctant. This could prove especially critical in areas where neighbors are "enemies" but share a common economic resource whose joint exploitation could be mutually beneficial but whose deterioration would deny both parties significant benefits. Such initiatives may engender a new "Neighbors to Partners" strategy for "environmental sustainability" and could reduce tensions in such flashpoint areas as the Nile, the Jordan River and even at more moderate levels of tension between Haiti and the Dominican Republic. The MacArthur Foundation, for example, has developed a number of such initiatives and hopefully these could be broadened and encouraged.

New partnerships with the United Nations

It is time to think anew about relationships with the world organization, and as we examine the processes of implementing Agenda 21, it is clear that governmental efforts alone will never be able to meet the challenges involved and that new partnerships are called for. It is being proposed here that the Commission on Sustainable Development work out a new relationship with the foundation community with a view to jointly defining and approaching specific problems.

APPENDIX

The Future of Giving: Hopes, Concerns, and Fantasies

The record of growth in the assets of the foundation community has been impressive but there is no assurance that this pattern will obtain in the future. Already some observers have been flashing alerts, if not alarms, of a somewhat dismal prospect. These include, among other things:

The changing status of the family and religion

Traditionally, the family and religion have been the forces that have shaped giving in the U.S. These two institutions are undergoing an important transition, however, and there is no guarantee that the values that have infused the habit of giving will emerge at the other end of the transition. This in turn could be a subject of serious examination in order to determine how giving might survive this very worrying developments.

The new billionaires

An interesting trend in the U.S. is the rise of the new billionaires many of whom have no historic family ties or deeply-rooted relations to society. Few has yet formed a coherent system of environmental values or program for systematic giving. This may be a promising area for exploration by foundations - how to engage the interest and attention of the new billionaires and the new wealth and how to help create a value system which would continue a historic tradition of philanthropy.

The second- and third- generation philanthropists

Whether or not the children as successors of the founders of philanthropic institutions and great givers of the past will continue the tradition is an area which might be addressed by foundations as we look to the future of giving in the U.S.

"What-ifs" and "why-nots"

The end of the Cold War and the forces of globalization, economic liberalization, democratization and the explosive electronic communications and information revolution as well as the emergence of "virtual communities" have created what some have termed as open in history. Now humanity has an unprecedented opportunity to re-invent itself - establishing that a future that is equitable, prosperous and sustainable. It is a time which calls for bold visions, creative thinking, experimentation and innovation, a time to liberate humanity's considerable "fantasy resources". It is a time to ask "what if?" and "why not?" along the lines advocated by the French students in their revolution of the '60s when they urged us to be "realistic and ask the impossible". What if foundations could mobilize their collective resources for say five years and commit these to the service of the Earth in a clear and coherent strategy for the implementation of Agenda 21? Such a question posed in fantasy might yet surprise the world with a plain and simple answer, "why not?".

Rekindling the Spirit of Rio

Historians may yet conclude that one of the most significant achievements of the United Nations during its fifty years of existence was conceiving and convening the Earth Summit. Historians may also characterize it as a defining moment for humanity and the planet. Without question, this was a conference of great expectations and strong sense of purpose. It was also perhaps a special moment for the earth establishing principles for a new covenant for the earth based on the realities of the human impact on the natural world and the dangers posed for the human future by unsustainable patterns of production, consumption and lifestyles.

Perhaps the former United Nations Secretary-General, Dr. Boutros Boutros Ghali, captured the spirit best and the requirements for new forms of good citizenship when he said:

"After loving his neighbor as the bible required him to, post-Rio man must also love the world, including the flowers, birds and trees - every part of that natural world that we are constantly destroying.

Over and above the moral contract with God, over and above the social contract concluded with men, we must now conclude an ethical and political contract with nature, with this earth to which we owe our very existence and which gives us life."

He then continued:

"Our road is now illuminated by a new light which I, along with many others, have called the spirit of Rio.

Perhaps, the fifth anniversary of the Earth Summit will provide a unique opportunity to rekindle that spirit. And perhaps, just perhaps the U.S. foundations might provide the spark.
