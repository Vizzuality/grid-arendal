Chernobyl Shelter Fund Report

EU & Eastern Europe/Central Asia

 

  

The issue

  • Chernobyl Shelter Fund- EC makes 40m Euro payment for 2001 21/12/01

In April 1986 Unit 4 of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant exploded and radioactive fallout spread across much of Europe. This disaster heightened public concerns about the risks caused by poorly designed and ageing nuclear power plants in central Europe and the then Soviet Union. It prompted governments across Europe and the rest of the world to try and join forces in an effort to prevent a repeat of ‘Chernobyl’.

Issues to be considered are:

  • a significant number of civilian reactors are involved. Current data are: 66 Soviet-designed reactors in operation, of which 21 are in central Europe and 45 are in the NIS, and of which the G7 identifies 35 to be fully upgradable: 13 in central Europe and 22 in the NIS.

  • the costs involved in upgrading or decommissioning a particular reactor are immense.

  • the international community has only limited resources available compared to the huge scale of the problem. For example, the Commission has had available under its Phare and Tacis nuclear safety programmes a total of e 838 million (of which e 181 million is under the EU’s Phare Programme and e 657 million is under the Tacis Programme) for the period 1991-98.

  • nuclear power is particularly important for the electricity supply of certain countries in central Europe and the NIS. For example, in Lithuania, the share in the electricity production is 85%, in Ukraine it is 44%; and in Bulgaria it is 40%. In some countries, part of this electricity production is exported. A considerable amount of time and resources is needed to replace this energy where necessary.

  • little detailed information about the state of the reactors and regulatory environments in central Europe and the NIS was available. Therefore, in the initial phase of international assistance, many studies were carried out.

  • there were considerable differences of opinion among experts in the G7, central Europe and the NIS, for example on the quality of the technology used in Soviet-designed reactors and on safety. The gap has narrowed over the past eight years as more information has become available, although on a number of issues different opinions continue to exist.

  • the nuclear power plants that need to be decommissioned or upgraded are located in and belong to independent sovereign countries. The governments of these countries have their own policies regarding the management of their economy, their energy sector and, specifically, the upgrading or closure of their reactors.

In this context, a nuclear safety strategy was adopted by the international community at the G7 summit in Munich 1992. Reactors of Soviet design were classified according to one of two categories: those that could be upgraded and those that could not and should therefore be decommissioned. In addition to the G7 strategy, the IAEA classified the problems associated with the design and operation of all types of Soviet-designed reactors in four categories according to their seriousness.

The role of the EU

As nuclear safety in Europe is one of EU’s primary concerns, the EU decided to take a prominent role in international efforts to help the NIS and countries of central and eastern Europe to ensure the safety of their nuclear reactors. The Member States charged the European Commission with this responsibility.

The Phare nuclear safety programme has focused on the following main priorities:

  • On-site assistance and operational safety

  • Design safety

  • Regulatory authorities and their technical support organisations

  • Waste management

  • Off-site emergency preparedness.

In the case of the NIS, the Tacis nuclear safety programme has focused on the following main areas:

  • On-site assistance and operational safety

  • Design safety

  • Regulatory authorities and their technical support organisations

  • Waste management

  • Off-site emergency preparedness

  • Control of nuclear materials

  • Conversion of nuclear military scientists

  • Chernobyl closure and sarcophagus.

Candidate countries of Central Europe – Phare

In the context of enlargement, the situation in central Europe has taken on a particular importance for the EU. Nuclear safety is a major part of the enlargement process. The candidate countries are fully aware of the importance that the EU attaches to this subject and have demonstrated their readiness to tackle the international community’s concerns.

New Independent States - Tacis

The EU’s strategy with regard to the NIS differs from its strategy for central Europe for the following reasons:

  • the NIS are not candidates for EU membership and therefore the EU has less political leverage

  • the NIS and their utility companies are facing considerable economic difficulties.

In addition, the EU’s strategy for the NIS reflects the differences among the NIS themselves.

Implementation of the Phare and Tacis nuclear safety programmes

To implement the EU’s strategy for improving nuclear safety in Central Europe and the NIS, the Commission developed a ‘Master Plan’ – a method of evaluation in 12 key practical safety areas, in line with the IAEA risk classification. Notwithstanding the major impact of the PHARE and TACIS Nuclear Safety Programmes in the improvement of nuclear safety in Central and Eastern Europe, still remaining difficulties with their implementation have to be mentioned.

Tender Opportunities

The implementation of all phases of assistance projects undertaken under the PHARE and TACIS nuclear safety programmes is the responsibility of the EuropeAid Co-operation Office: http://europa.eu.int/comm/europeaid/tender/index_en.htm

The way ahead

With respect to the immediate future, it is considered that actions could be strengthened in a number of areas in order to improve their effectiveness, taking into account the achievements and difficulties of the nuclear safety programmes to date.
