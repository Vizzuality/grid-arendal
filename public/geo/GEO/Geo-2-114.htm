Introduction

To satisfy the requirements of the European Environment Agency (EEA), its Member Countries involved in the European Environmental Information and Observation Network (EIONET), the European Commission, Non-Governmental Organisations and the general public it is necessary to provide access to objective, reliable and comparable information at a European level enabling them to take the requisite measures to protect the environment, to assess the results of such measures and to ensure that the public is properly informed about the state of the environment.

This will involve the construction and maintenance of a number of databases regarding the water environment which are known collectively as WATERBASE.

WATERBASE is therefore, an information system offering facilities needed to collect, validate, evaluate, store and visualise water environment data and statistics and (meta) information on water quality and quantity monitoring networks and stations and making this data available at different aggregated levels, for different information users and through different distribution channels.
 

Information Needs

Information is therefore needed on the status of Europe’s water resources in order to detect changes over time (trends), and how the status and trends are related to the pressures on the environment and to policies directed at the pressures or the societal forces creating those pressures.

This information will only be credible if its component data is comparable and gives a representative assessment of water types within a Member State and across Europe.

Information Sources

Member Countries design networks and monitor water resources according to their national requirements (legal and operational) and international obligations (e.g. EU Directives and International Conventions). Both types of monitoring network are potentially a source of data for the EEA’s needs.

The EIONET and EURO-WATERNET

The EIONET is the information network of people, organisations, hardware and software that has been developed as the process whereby data can be gathered from within Member Countries for use by the EEA and/or its Topic Centres in order to achieve the objectives of the political mandate. National EIONET systems are being set up in all Member Countries and some are currently operational.

EURO-WATERNET is the process or system, based upon the EIONET, for the EEA (or the ETC/IW on its behalf) to obtain the information it requires on water resources to fulfil its legal tasks. It provides information on the state and pressures on the environment to allow the Commission and Member Countries to evaluate the effectiveness of policy. Policy targets are often expressed as a percentage reduction of a pollutant over a number of years. WATERNET is designed to “sample” existing monitoring networks to produce aggregated data of known statistical power and confidence. This need to compare like with like is achieved through a statistically stratified design with each identified and specified stratum containing similar water bodies. The use of the same criteria for selecting the strata and water types across Member Countries ensures that valid status comparisons will be obtained at the required levels of statistical power and confidence.

WATERNET will permit comparison of the size/numbers/types of water body across Member Countries on a like with like basis (e.g. large rivers, alpine streams, large lakes) and assessment of the human pressures (e.g. population density, land use, point discharges). The stratification criteria e.g. river size, altitude, slope, land use are currently being tested. It is certain that different objectives will require different stratification criteria and the selection of different monitoring stations from those available in the national “pool”. For example, the assessment of the extent of surface water acidification will require quite a different approach to the assessment of the effectiveness of the Nitrates Directive.

WATERNET will draw upon the existing national and international monitoring networks. The pilot studies have shown that by and large most countries have a national monitoring network for rivers that is more than adequate for the EEA’s needs. This is less true with regard to groundwaters and even less true for lakes/reservoirs although there are some exceptions. The pilot studies have also shown that the information supplied to the Commission in compliance with directives is of little to no value to the EEA although there may be exceptions.

Users of WATERBASE

WATERBASE will be made available to a number of different target users:

  • EU - Directorate General XI
  •  European Environment Agency staff and National Experts at Copenhagen
  •  European Topic Centre Co-ordinating Group and partners
  • Other European Topic Centres
  • EEA member countries through National Focal Points and National Reference Centres (the EIONET “family”)
  • EEA collaborating organisations such as Eurostat, Joint Research Centre (DG XII), Word Health Organisation, United Nations Environment Programme, OECD, World Meteorological Organisation, UN FAO, UNESCO
  • EEA’s other potential information users: other EC Directorate Generals, Council of Ministers, European Parliament, national environmental authorities, non-governmental organisations, representatives and organisations of industry, commerce and agriculture, academic organisations, research centres, media and the general public.


Such a varied target group requires that the system should provide information that matches the needs of each individual group. This has important consequences for how and where the system may be accessed, for user-interfaces and for data presentation and visualisation applications.
 

Process

 Countries provide data in many different formats (e.g. excel files, access databases, word and text file). The data and information are also expressed in different ways (e.g. river names in national languages, determinands with several names and with different units, measurement). The process of identifying river stations and determinands, and the means of transmitting that information to the ETC/IW and the EEA is known as EUROWATERNET.
All the data is stored in a working version of Waterbase where the data is checked and processed (e.g. correct determinand names in the same units, validated determinands values, correct station identity, and rivers names).
Once the data is processed, it is imported into the consolidated, validated version of WATERBASE (running several queries, linking tables from one database to the other one).
This is situation at present, the process will hopefully be developed and improved within the TERESA project.
