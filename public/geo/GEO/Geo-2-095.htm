Seu pedido

 
Since its discovery, Brazil has caught the world´s eye and caused envy because of its fauna and flora. Its rich and precious biodiversity has always been the target of those who get here. Until today the country is represented by a flag that enhances the green of its forests and by an anthem that says: “our woods have more life and our fields have more flowers”. Each year, however, data shows us that a less romantic destiny lays ahead our patriotic symbols. There aren´t so many forests anymore, and the green is getting “quieter”.

The cultural development of the Brazilian people was unique, allowing conquerors and people who had a close relationship with nature and the environment to meet. Nowadays, we can see signs of this miscegenation if we take a close look into the big cities, or if we look at some far-off places in the countryside, and can find several wild animals living peacefully with men, in a relationship of both admiration and control.
The habit of having wild animals as pets has existed since the time of colonization. When the Portuguese came ashore, they started doing the same thing as the native indians who had monkeys and tropical birds as pets did, besides using the colorful feathers of Brazilian birds on hats and on clothes.

According to the Brazilian journalist, Eduardo Bueno, during the first thirty years after the discovery of Brazil, the Portuguese ships that left Brazil used to take in their basements approximately 3,000 jaguar hides (Panthera onca) and 600 parrots (Amazona sp.), in average. These “goods” would adorn dresses and palaces in the old world, as soon as they reached the shores in Europe. Using hats that were adorned with colorful feathers from tropical birds was considered to be of extreme good taste, and it was often a luxury reserved for those in the upper classes. This covetous foreign look exists until today, it carries more than simple curiosity , though. It shows us that we certainly have the greatest biodiversity reserve on the planet, and that in it are several answers not yet known to mankind and to science. According to data from UNPE – The United Nations Program for the Environment (UNPE -profile/1992), about 100 species disappear every day on the planet, and the illegal trade of wild animals is one of the principal reasons for this tragedy.

In less than 500 years, Brazil has lost about 94% (“Veja” magazine, special edition/1997) of the original Atlantic Forest, one of the most important ecosystems in the country. More and more often poachers go into tropical forests searching for animals to promote national and international traffic, and keeping wild animals in cages is still a Brazilian cultural habit. Whether the rich who show their private collections of wild animals off as trophies due to their vanity; whether the poor who go into the woods hunting these animals which will be sold to ease their hunger, or even foreign scientists who look for, in the Brazilian fauna and flora, a possibility to make good money in developing new drugs, the naked truth is that: fauna resources in Brazil are endangered by illegal trade.
