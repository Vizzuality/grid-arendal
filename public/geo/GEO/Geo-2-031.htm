Canada and the United States Reach a Draft Agreement to Reduce Transboundary Smog

Statement by the Hon. David Anderson, Minister of the Environment

OTTAWA – October 13, 2000 – I want to congratulate the Canadian and American negotiating teams for finalizing the draft text of the Ozone Annex to the Canada-U.S. Air Quality Agreement. They have laid the foundation for significant reductions in smog causing pollutants from both sides of the border.

The draft Annex, once approved by both the Canadian and American governments, will bring cleaner air to more than 16 million Canadians in southern Ontario, southern Quebec and Atlantic Canada as emissions of Nitrogen Oxide (NOx) and Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) from 18 northern and north eastern states are reduced starting in 2004.

The United States will reduce NOx emissions by 35% by 2007. This represents a 70% reduction in U.S. emissions from power plants and major industrial sources during the summer months when smog creates the greatest health risk. Transboundary smog accounts for 30 -70 percent of dirty air in eastern Canada.

In Canada, the final draft puts an annual cap of 39 kilotonnes on Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2) emissions from fossil fuel power plants in southern Ontario and a five kilotonne cap on NO2 emissions from southern Quebec by 2007. In the case of Ontario, this represents a 50 percent reduction in emissions of NO2 from the current average of 78 kilotonnes. Canada will also align emissions standards for cars, light duty trucks and SUVs.

Both countries recognize that the Ozone Annex is not the end to transboundary cooperation to reduce the emissions from air pollution. For that reason, the final draft Ozone Annex notes that in 2004, both countries will assess progress in implementing the obligations in the Annex and discuss the possibility of amending the Annex to designate additional emission management areas and/or to determine if the emission commitment for NOx and VOCs need to be revised.

The final draft also commits both countries to look at further options to reduce emissions from significant sources such as transportation, manufacturing and electricity. The goal is to implement cost-effective emission reductions through energy efficiency, renewable energy, cleaner fuel and alternative technology and approaches.

The Ozone Annex demonstrates the Government of Canada's leadership through the Clean Air Agenda. The reductions specified in the final draft combined with domestic actions under Canada Wide Standards to cut emissions of particulate matter and ozone by 2010 or earlier will reduce premature deaths caused by dirty air and result in fewer hospital admissions for Canadians with respiratory problems. Air pollution currently accounts for 5,000 premature deaths in Canada annually.

I am committed to bringing the draft Annex forward for approval as quickly as possible and intend to sign a final agreement by the end of this year.
