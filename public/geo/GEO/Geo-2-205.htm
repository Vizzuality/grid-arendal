    2. Freshwater Caucus 

  1. 
 

      (a) Improve equity and efficiency in the use of water resources with a view to maintaining water for nature and ecosystems (including wildlife and wildlife habitat) and preserving or restoring ecological integrity in fragile environments and initiate programs to protect water resources against domestic, agricultural and industrial pollution; in doing this, one should repeat the main objectives as defined in Agenda 21, Chapter 18, Paragraph 18.2: "to make certain that adequate supplies of water of good quality are maintained for the entire population of this planet, while preserving the hydrological, biological and chemical functions of ecosystems, adapting human activities with the capacity limits of nature ...". Furthermore one should emphasize what Chapter 18 says about the priority between need and users (paragraph 18.8): "In developing and using water resources, priority should be given to the satisfaction of basic needs and the safeguarding of ecosystems. BEYOND these requirements, however, water users should be charged appropriately."  

      (b) Provide international support to help developing countries, in particular least developed countries and small island developing states, to develop their own solutions and models, including integrated river basin and watershed management strategies, plans and programmes; developed and developing countries should ensure that their water resource policies and management plans comply with international agreements and take into consideration processes such as those concerned with climate change, desertification, biodiversity, wetlands and dams as well as sustainable development, poverty eradication, food security, and sustainable agriculture.  

      (d) Promote public information and participation in decision making as prerequisite conditions to the success of small and large water projects, and decentralize decision making, implementation of projects and operation of services to the lowest level appropriate, with a watershed as the appropriate reference unit for integrated water resource management; in doing this, promote the adoption of WCD recommendations in national water policies.  

      (e) Assist developing countries in monitoring and assessing the quantity and quality of water resources, including the development of water resources databases, in particular remote-sensing and satellite data, and linked data collection and mapping efforts, including the development and application of relevant indicators; in addition, global water assessment initiatives, such as World Water Assessment Program and GIWA and other assessments, should be gender disaggregated wherever appropriate and made publicly available and broadly disseminated. 

      (f) Promote programmes for the sharing internationally, nationally and locally of technology and capacity-building in the area of sustainable and eco-friendly water management using low cost technologies aimed at conserving freshwater including alternative waste treatment technologies, recycling technologies, water purification systems that use solar and wind energy power, and using traditional practices such as rain-water harvesting and drip irrigation.  
 

We request that the Chairman add a special section under Section IV, following 6(g) that includes the following recommendations:

Actions are required to:

 
  • Establish an international framework on freshwater that recognizes the following: the interconnectedness of the lack of water security and poverty; water as a fundamental human right; and that water is kept outside the purview of the World Trade Organization.
 
  • Strengthen the international and national implementation of the international treaties addressing freshwater resources and ecosystem health and the right to water, including the Convention on the Rights of the Child of 1989 (Article 24), the Convention on Biological Diversitys thematic programme on inland water ecosystems and the 1971 Convention on Wetlands.  In addition, ensure that multilateral environmental agreements supersede all trade and investment agreements, within or outside WTO.
 
  • Recognize the finiteness of water and its special place in sustaining life, and for these reasons ensure that water is not privatized and that it is kept in the public realm.
 
  • Promote adoption of the Bonn recommendations, with the clear acknowledgement that the majority of the worlds water poor (for whom ecosystem functionality is crucial) live in rural areas, and a commitment to address their problems first and foremost. To reiterate, private sector participation should not be imposed on developing countries as a conditionality for funding; nor should it be encouraged.

X. STRENGTHENING GOVERNANCE FOR SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT AT THE NATIONAL, REGIONAL AND INTERANTIOANL LEVELS 

    A. International organisation 

    1. ILO 

The ILO suggests that a distinction is made between  

    -the governance of the integration of economic, social and environmental policies, which is, on the one hand, a process in which political actors are responsible  

    -and the governance of the transformation of the economy from unsustainable to more sustainable patterns of production and consumption, as well as the preservation of natural resources, which are, on the other hand, a process in which economic and social actors are the main decision makers 

The ILO suggests two changes in the discussion paper; first in the general considerations, and then, in an additional section V with three paragraphs: 

I. General considerations 

1.    In  order to . 

2.   The overriding objective of governance for sustainable development is to bring about an integration of economic, social and environmental strategies and policies.  This is to facilitate a transformation of the economy from unsustainable to more sustainable patterns of production and consumption. A strengthening of governance for sustainable development must be based on the principles of responsibility in open market economies. The role of the political institutions on an international level is to provide national governments with visions, knowledge, policy tools, and agreed standards for a successful integration of the three policy areas. The role of national governments is to set objectives and targets, create the necessary incentives and the legal frameworks and provision of services for a transformation of the economy. The role of economic and social actors, employers and workers, in business and SMEs, farmers and entrepreneurs, is to make the necessary decisions regarding investment and technology, with the goal of creating new and sustainable patterns of production and preservation of natural resources.  Success in achieving the objectives of the strategy for sustainable development requires strengthening of all the various aspects of good governance.
