AIR



The main air pollutants include sulphur dioxide, nitrogen oxides and dust. Most of these atmosphere-contaminating emissions are originated at fuel combustion processes. The largest share of sulphur dioxide is emitted by power generating utilities, nitrogen dioxide – by mobile sources, and dust – by power utilities and industrial processes.

Sources and emissions of basic contaminants in Poland in 1996




Emissions of the acidifying substances and emissions of dust have decreased since 1988.



The almost 50% fall of the sulphur dioxide emission over the last decade is a proof of efficiency of the legal and economic instruments implemented in the early nineties and of the technologies applied. A chance for further reduction of the SO2 emission is provided by way of gasification and change in household heating systems, which, in addition to the reduction of the country’s emission total volume, shall improve air quality in cities.



At the same time nitrogen oxides global emission reduction was recorded. Installing of low emissions burners in many plants further reduces the NOx emission from stationary sources. However the predominant share in the air contamination with nitrogen oxides is attributed to mobile sources, which are increasing in numbers.

 


The currently binding national regulations, as well as approval of the UN European Economic Commission’s all rules in respect with pollutant emission from motor vehicles, together with introduction of the requirement of international compliance certificates, should bring about some concrete effect of reduction of not only the nitrogen oxides, but also carbon monoxide, hydrocarbons and lead.

Dust produced at fuel combustion and some industrial processes typically includes some compounds of heavy metals such as cadmium, lead, mercury and chromium.

The air quality in Poland is also affected by contamination originating in the neighboring countries. 

The main air pollutant concentrations (SO2, NO2, dust) at the vast majority of Poland’s territory are relatively low. Excesses of the SO2, and dust over allowable concentrations are most often recorded in the industrialized Southern Poland and the cities, were individual heating systems prevail. Excesses over standardized concentration of nitrogen dioxide are the rarest. Ozone concentration is growing, most often beyond the allowable levels.

Despite those decremental trends, still on some areas exposed to emissions from nearby industrial complexes excesses over the standard concentrations of specific contaminants, i.e. phenol, formaldehyde, and heavy metals are recorded. Especially worthwhile is benzopyrene, excessive yearly average concentrations of which were recorded at all measuring stations. This carcinogenic emission is originated mainly at low temperature and inadequate oxygen content combustion processes.

Poland is striving for further reduction of hazardous pollutant emissions and as a party to many conventions and international agreements has assumed numerous obligations pursuant to them.
