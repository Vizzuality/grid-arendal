Air Management

Japan faced severe air pollution problems, especially in urban centers, in the 1960s and 1970s. Considerable action was taken to address these problems, and tremendous progress has been made. Despite it's high energy efficiency, absolute CO2 emissions are still high and demand special action.

Compared to other countries, Japan's level of air pollution is low. Its sulfur oxide (SOx) and nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions, both per capita and per unit of GDP, are the lowest in the OECD. Its CO2 emissions are below the OECD average. Japan does receive transboundary air pollution, especially SOx from Korea and China. Wide-scale acid deposition damage has not yet occurred, though.

Urban conditions have improved, especially due to high fuel prices, technological advancements, and strict standards. Progress on lead emissions, for example, has been dramatic. Distribution of unleaded gasoline began in 1975. By 1987, leaded gas had been completely eliminated from Japan.

Regulatory measures began as a reaction to the heavy air pollution of the late 1960s. In 1967, the Basic Law for Environmental Pollution Control set standards for five air pollutants: sulfur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), carbon monoxide (CO), particulate matter (PM), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). The Air Pollution Control Law of 1968 set emission standards for designated sources. These standards have been constantly revised and are now some of the strictest in the world. Establishment of effective standards was aided by Japan's excellent monitoring system. This system was put in place by the Air Pollution Control Law of 1974, which established an Area-Wide Total Pollutant Load Control System.

Climate Change

Role in Global Warming

Japan is extremely reliant on foreign energy sources. Its energy supply is considered one of the most vulnerable among OECD countries, and imports are needed to meet 84% of energy demand. The oil crisis of the early 1970s greatly reduced the rate of growth in energy use and prompted new policy initiatives prompting conservation and greater national self-sufficiency.

Japan is very energy efficient. Among OECD countries, it has one of the lowest ratios of energy consumed per unit of gross domestic product (GDP), as well as one of the lowest energy intensity rates per capita (though this has grown rapidly since 1984). Japan has shown that economic growth is not dependent on greater energy use. Between 1979 and 1987, for example, the ratio of energy supply to GDP fell by 25%.

Yet,while consumption rates have slowed, overall emissions still grow. This fact, coupled with the size of the Japanese economy, make it a significant contributor to global greenhouse gases . In 1992, for example, Japan produced 5.09% of the world's total CO2 emissions.

Goals

Japan ratified the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change in May of 1993. Yet its national policy on global warming predates UNCED by two years, having been set in the 1990 Action Program to Arrest Global Warming. Some goals of the program are:

  • Stabilizing CO2 emissions at 1990 levels by the year 2000
  • Stabilizing methane emissions at 1990 levels
  • Enhancing CO2 sinks by promoting increased forest cover domestically and internationally

Specific Policies

1990 Action Program to Arrest Global Warming: An important component in Japan's climate policy was its recognition of the need for greater integration in sectorial policies. To accomplish this, the government established the Council of Ministers for Global Environment Conservation. This body approved the Action Program to Arrest Global Warming in 1990. In addition to setting emission targets, the program also instituted mechanisms for information-gathering and cooperation. These include:

  • Research and monitoring of global warming, with special emphasis on modeling possible changes in the Asia-Pacific region
  • Development and sharing of technology for energy conservation, renewable energy and carbon fixation
  • International cooperation, especially the targeting of aid to developing countries

The council tracks the progress of the program and its emission goals and publishes its findings in annual reports.

"New Earth 21" Action Program for the Twenty-First Century was developed by MITI. It is a century-long development plan to stop global warming which maps out targets and technological goals through the next century. The report sets specific targets for energy reduction in various economic sectors. Its "Fourteen Proposals for a New Earth" include:

  • Revisions to the Law on Energy Conservation to promote efficiency through tax incentives and energy savings plans for industry (Japan does not have a carbon tax)
  • Promotion of nuclear and alternative energy sources, as well as a greater role for natural gas
  • International cooperation, including aid for conservation and clean coal technology as well as safety improvements for the nuclear facilities of the former Soviet Union

This century-long plan is divided into two fifty-year components:

  • The first fifty years is a transition period dedicated to research and developing new technologies. This includes further studies of global warming and carbon sinks, increases in energy efficiency, development of alternative energy sources, land reclamation and reforestation.
  • The second fifty years involves implementation of the new technologies. The plan actually projects a levelling off and eventual decrease in greenhouse gas concentrations in this period.

Current Progress

Japan's previous success in reducing energy demand has been considerable, yet there are signs that further progress is hampered by both technological and economic forces. Given the tremendous gains already achieved, it is questionable how much more can be accomplished through technology fixes. However, there is still room for some improvements in appliance standards and vehicle and building efficiency.

Low oil prices and a strong yen have made efficiency less important from an economic perspective. They have also increased the cost of labor relative to automation, which encourages increased use of machinery and, therefore, greater energy demand. Unlike many other countries, Japan does not use taxes to offset fluctuations in world energy prices, and thus its industries are more inclined to forgo efficiency measures when prices drop.

Water Management

Japan has high annual precipitation rates and abundant freshwater resources. Many of its fast-moving mountain rivers have been harnessed for hydroelectric power, and its numerous lakes are the primary source of drinking water. The most pressing water concern in Japan is improving its wastewater treatment systems. While considerable progress has been made in some areas of pollution, performance in other areas is still poor.

Two environmental quality standards for water were set in 1970. The first, for the protection of human health, concerns heavy metals and toxic substances. The second, for conservation of the "living environment," concerns organic pollution. Japan's performance on water pollution could be summarized as considerable progress on the first standard but little progress on the second.

Pollution from heavy metals and toxics was extreme in the 1950s and 1960s, and water pollution became one of the first important concerns for the Japanese environmental administration. Standards were set for the most dangerous substances, such as mercury and PCBs, and these have largely been met. In 1991, all but 0.02% of water samples met standards for heavy metals and toxics.

Considerable improvement is still needed for organic pollution. Eutrophication is damaging Tokyo Bay, Ise Bay and the Seto Inland Sea, and untreated sewage is released into surface waters, including drinking water sources like Lake Kasumigaura and Lake Biwa. Japan's most pressing need is to expand sewage treatment services. In 1992, only 35% of the population was connected to sewage systems, a very low level compared to Japan's OECD counterparts. In the same period, the United States had services for 74% of its population, and Great Britain serviced 87%. Most of the sewage treatment capacity is primitive as well. Only about 2.3 million of Japan's over 124 million inhabitants are served by advanced sewage treatment systems capable of removing nitrogen and phosphorus compounds.

Japan is taking action to improve waste water treatment. Its target is to increase treatment service to 70% of the population by the year 2000. A 1994 review conducted by the OECD was skeptical of this goal, however. It projected that additional funding mechanisms, such as pollution charges, would probably be needed to adequately fund this expansion.

Waste Management

As a relatively small, densly-populated country, Japan's primary concern on waste management is limitting the volume to be disposed in landfills. This was stated cleary in public policy under the Guidelines for Solid Waste Management, promulgated by the Ministry of Health and Welfare in 1979. The guidelines state that:" Given the difficulty of providing land or seaside areas for refuse disposal sites, it will be necessary to limit the volume of collected refuse designated for treatment or disposal."

The guidelines provide three mechanisms for waste disposal:

  • "Recycling disgarded refuse into useable resources"
  • "Reusing such resources as much as possible"
  • "Treating or disposing of the unusable portion in an environmentally sound manner"

Japan has been very successful in reducing waste volume, especially for municipal solid waste (MSW). In 1992, recycling rates for waste paper, for example, were at 48% and those for glass were 49%. The 1991 Law Concerning the Promotion of the Use of Recyclable Reources, furthermore, sets even higher targets for recycling of paper, glass and other materials.

The next step in reducing waste volume is incinceration. About 75% of MSW is incinerated, reducing its volume by 95%. While incineration is a solution for waste management, its effect on air quality may be troublesome. Incinerators must meet strict standards for SOx and NOx emissions, but there are no regulations covering dioxin, furons, PCBs and heavy metals. Guidelines based on best available technology have been developed for these pollutants, but they are not legally enforceable. Over 70% of Japan's incinerators are modern, continuous combustion facilities to which the strictest gudelines apply.

Japan's progress in reducing waste volume is somewhat offset by rapid increases in the amount of waste generated. From 1986 to 1989, the volume of municipal waste increased by 4% annually, a growth rate four-fold that of the pevious ten years. One reason for a steady increase in waste generated may be that Japan offers little economic incentives for households to reduce waste. User charges only cover about 10% of waste handling costs.

The situation of hazardous waste (termed "specially controlled waste" in Japan) is hard to analyze. In comparison with other OECD countries, Japan classifies a smaller ratio of its waste as hazardous. The shipment and ultimate fate of hazardous wastes is also unclear. A 1992 law on specially controlled waste did set up a manifest system to track waste through all stages of shipment. This system is relatively new, however, and no national accounting and analysis system for manifests is currently in place.

Nature Conservation

Japan has collected excellent data on conservation issues. The best resource is its "Green Census," a national inventory conducted by thousands of experts and citizen volunteers. The first census was begun in 1973, and subsequent ones were completed in 1979, 1987, and 1992. The surveys show rapid changes in the Japanese environment over the period covered.

Land-use and vegetation cover has changed. Agricultural land has decreased and urban areas have increased. Forest composition has changed. Fourteen percent of natural forests have been lost, most of them being replaced by plantation forests. Japan remains one of the most heavily forested countries in the OECD, and the extraction industry has declined in recent years. The cause is not so much decreased consumption of wood, but increased imports of softwood and tropical hardwood (Japan is the world's largest wood importer).

Freshwater resources and coastlines have been severely altered. Pollution of surface waters and coastal areas has often been severe. Natural shorelines have been lost to development. Thirty percent of lake shores have been developed; only 46% of the natural marine coastline remains.

Japan has considerable biodiversity. It is especially rich in avian species; acccording to the "Red Book of Japan," over half the vertebrates in the country are birds. Conservation policy is handled by several government bodies. The Environment Agency, though its Nature Conservation Bureau, has ultimate responsibility for conservation policy. It also administers national parks, wilderness areas and nature conservation areas. the Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries administers forest reserves, and forests important for soil and water conservation and for scenic beauty. The Agency for Cultural Affairs can also provide protection by designating certain plants, animals, or landscapes as "natural monuments." The main legislation on conservation is the Nature Conservation Law of 1972.
