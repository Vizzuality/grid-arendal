  PURPOSE

One of the principal goals for the USAID BOLFOR Project during its final years is to assure that the many worthwhile activities in which it is engaged continue after the project terminates. The contributions of the UF-FMT Partnership towards reaching this goal will be focused on continuation of research and training programs started under the auspices of BOLFOR. To sustain research requires well-trained and motivated researchers, funding, suitable study sites, and institutional support. To a large extent, if there are enough researchers with the appropriate training and interests, funding and the other pre-requisites for sustained research are likely to be secured. Unfortunately, although BOLFOR provided training to hundreds of Bolivians during the first years of the project, including several graduate fellowships for study abroad, the list of Bolivian forest scientists is still extremely short. For example, at present there may be no Bolivians with Ph.D.s in forestry or related disciplines. This is just one of many possible indicators of the lack of individuals sufficiently advanced in their training to secure continued funding and to carry out the research needed to support sustainable forest management in Bolivian. Likewise, institutional weakness in the forest research sector continues to be a problem despite years of effort on the part of BOLFOR.

  BACKGROUND

The BOLFOR project began in 1994. The project's goal is to reduce degradation of the natural resource base and protect biodiversity in the lowlands of Bolivia, especially in areas outside of the national park and protected area system.

The main strategy to accomplish this goal is the promotion of sustainable forest management. During the seven years that BOLFOR has been operating, research has been a fundamental activity that has cross cut the three major components: institutional strengthening and policy, natural forest management, and product development and marketing. Key to the successful promotion of sustainable forest management are research results that have oriented policy decisions, the development of technical standards and the implementation of improved forest management practices. These efforts have resulted in more than 1 million hectares of forest becoming certified under the guidelines of the Forest Stewardship Council.

  ACTIVITIES IN 2001

We are using funds provided through the US Forest Service to sustain applied research on issues related to sustainable forest management. Overall, this research is broad in its perspective, but a central focus is on a set of large-scale and long-term silvicultural research plots replicated in different forest zones in lowland Bolivia. The existence of permanent research sites with substantial background data (e.g., stand maps, site histories, soils data, harvesting and silvicultural treatment records) and at least modest facilities is expected to serve as a cornerstone for attracting researchers and research funding to Bolivia. Every effort is being made to assure that the basic studies are designed in a statistically rigorous fashion, that the treatments applied are silviculturally appropriate, and that the development of the research network is open for participation of both Bolivians and scientists from elsewhere in the region and world. Research we plan to support during the early phases of what is planned as a 40+-year study includes baseline silvicultural studies as well as investigations of impacts of silvicultural treatments on biodiversity and ecosystem functions. The plots are also anticipated to serve as focal areas for training of technical staff as well as graduate students. Finally, given that the challenges to sustainable forest management in Bolivia are not solely biophysical in origin, the UFFMT Partnership will use some of its funding to support research on social, political, and economic issues.
