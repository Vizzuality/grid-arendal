Acres of Late-Successional and Old-Growth Forests

The Wealth of Humboldt and the Klamath-Siskiyou Region

Frances Harmon

Source: Forest Ecosystem Management: An Ecological, Economic, & Social Assessment, A Report of the Forest Ecosystem Management Assessment Team (1993)

How are we doing?

Although our region has 1,793,400 acres (57.8% of 3,103,900 total acres in California and Oregon) of old-growth and 1,170,700 acres (42% of 2,793,500 total acres) of late-successional forests, the trend has been toward an overall decrease in acreage and fragmentation of remaining stands. For example, in the Siskiyou National Forest (Oregon), there was a 24% acreage decrease between 1981 and 1988, from 185,000 acres to 141,000 acres. Of these remaining acres, 66,000 were in fragmented landscapes, in which old-growth stands were less than 400 acres in size and surrounded by clearcuts, plantations, and non-forested areas.

Why is this important?

Acreage of old-growth forest is a positive indicator of natural capital. Loss of the relative abundance, diversity, and distribution of old-growth communities leading to fragmentation of remaining stands, as well as the negative externalities of logging--such as road failures and increased runoff and sedimentation--can have a potentially devastating effect on natural capital—both of the natural resource stock (i.e., depletion of fisheries, loss of viable old-growth dependent species) and environmental services (such as water quality). Human capital could also be negatively impacted, as ancient forests are a rich resource for furthering our knowledge of the interrelationships of ecological systems, and their loss would diminish that ability. Ecotourism prospects and related opportunities for created capital could be impaired. Social and cultural capital would likely be affected as well, since ancient forests have proved to be a mechanism for spurring volunteerism and defining the culture of surrounding communities.
