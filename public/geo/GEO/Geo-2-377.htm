blazing a trail
the tools | the people | ecological benefits | blazing a trail | diary | links

Carla Turner
CBC NEWS ONLINE

Fire managers are now coming to grips with the paradox of fire: that it both kills and gives life. Eliminating all fire leads to a build-up of debris on the forest floor, which ultimately changes the ecosystem. Moreover, the accumulation of forest debris can lead to more intense fires that spread more quickly.

Increasingly, the trend across North America is to introduce programs of prescribed fires. These are lit deliberately to restore fire-dependent ecosystems or to reduce forest debris. Prescribed fires may also be caused by lightning and then be allowed to burn. The forestry industry has been using such fires for decades to clear away logging debris and prepare logged sites for planting. But it is only recently that prescribed burns have been used as a method of conserving ecosystems. Twenty years ago, in a major policy change, Parks Canada decided that it wouldn't interfere in natural processes such as fire, insects and disease unless it had to -- that is, unless people or neighbouring lands were threatened.

By studying the fire cycles of forests, parks ecologists can determine when a particular area is overdue for fire. A controlled blaze can then be lit, usually in the spring or early summer. Trained specialists decide when and where, and for how long, to permit such fires to burn. They consider weather, vegetation type, fire behaviour and terrain in order for the fires to burn safely.

Prescribed fires burned 9,350 hectares of Canadas national parks in the year 2000. Such fires are also permitted in provincial parks in Ontario and B.C. The same policy has taken hold in the United States, though it has also run into controversy there. In 1988, the U.S. Forest Service was heavily criticized when parts of Yellowstone National Park were allowed to burn after being struck by lightning. The fires spread quickly and, in the end, men and resources were moved in to douse the flames. It was the costliest fire-fighting effort in U.S. history, at $120 million U.S. Still, both the Canadian and U.S. federal governments believe in the role of fire.

The provinces, meanwhile, conduct prescribed burns on a very limited basis. Timber is a valuable resource and key to many provincial economies. It is hard to convince anyone that burning down trees, even on a small scale, makes sense. Few want to risk their land and homes for a prescribed burn that could pick up in high winds and turn into an uncontrolled conflagration. Prescribed burns have been strongly opposed by landowners and outdoors-related businesses.

Forest fires are still generally viewed as bad -- and they do cause millions of dollars in damage. But efforts are being made to change the public's perception. Information campaigns in Canada and the United States are trying to combat the "Smokey Bear" message of the 1950s. It is increasingly accepted that fire does have its place -- in a controlled setting, where lives and property are not at stake.
