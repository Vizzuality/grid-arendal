FISHERIES AND OCEANS ANNOUNCES REBUILDING EFFORTS RESULT IN ASTOUNDING 
RECOVERY OF UPPER ADAMS AND NADINA SOCKEYE RUNS

Vancouver – Sockeye stocks from the Upper Adams River near Chase and the Nadina River south of Burns Lake experienced record escapement levels, the highest escapement in decades, Fisheries and Oceans Canada announced today.

This year, escapement surveys indicate that nearly 70,000 sockeye returned to the Upper Adams River to spawn. This is a miraculous recovery as the Upper Adams River early summer sockeye run, historically among the largest in the Fraser River, was driven to extinction in the early part of the 20th century. The combined effects of the Hell's Gate slide and local logging practices, such as a splash dam operated at a local mill on Adams Lake, devastated the stocks from the period 1913 until 1953.

By the 1980s, significant improvements to these salmon stocks began to show in the area. In 1992, 2,990 sockeye returned to spawn, and in 1996, approximately 25,000 sockeye returned. The improvements are the result of ongoing rebuilding efforts that began in 1949 with the transplanting and hatchery augmentation work begun by the decommissioned International Pacific Salmon Fisheries Commission. These efforts were continued and improved upon by Fisheries and Oceans Canada's Salmonid Enhancement Program (SEP) in conjunction with local First Nations.

In the Nadina River, more than 194,000 sockeye returned to spawn this year. Like the Upper Adams, the Nadina run suffered from the combined effects of the Hell’s Gate slide and deleterious logging practices such as log drives in the lower river. Poor escapements in the 1940s through to the 1960s prompted Fisheries and Oceans to construct a spawning channel in 1970, intended to augment the natural production of this stock. Escapement increased to several thousand in the 1980s, and in 1996 to nearly 40,000 sockeye.

In both of these areas, stock rebuilding success has been the result of many decades of dedicated enhancement, combined with a precautionary management approach that sets harvest rates at a level that permits the spawners to return to their natal areas.

Fisheries and Oceans Canada has placed a high priority on restoring these stocks and with the joint efforts of First Nations and other stakeholders, restoration efforts appear to be successful.
