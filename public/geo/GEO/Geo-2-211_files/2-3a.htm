Population and urbanisation in the  Mediterranean Region

 

 

The total population of the Mediterranean countries in 2000 reached 427 million people. It was 285 million in 1970, which means it grew by 142 million in thirty years. The latest projected demographic figures estimated[1] for the Blue Plan puts the population at 523.5 million by 2025, which shows a certain slowing down in growth with a little under 97 million additional inhabitants in 25 years.

Presently at 64.3 per cent, the average rate of urbanisation will reach 72.4 per cent by 2025. This rise is mostly due to the urban growth in the southern and eastern countries. In absolute terms the urban population of all Mediterranean countries—274.5 million in 2000—will reach 379 million by 2025. By that date, there would be 104.5 million additional urban dwellers, of which more than 98 million in the South and East.

The Blue Plan has always seen population and its physical distribution as a capital element in the Mediterranean system. Indeed, the Blue Plan's experience for the past twenty years in the Mediterranean region shows that the population dynamic is the main factor of change, for it calls for the most basic needs, determining the consumption of all sorts of resources and provoking the most direct environmental pressure. This of course does not mean that the "ecological footprint", i.e. the amount of resources concerned and of waste per person is the same from one country to the other.

Nonetheless, whatever the economic or social sector under consideration (energy materials, transportation infrastructures, the construction of schools and hospitals, the relationship between job offer and demand…), the number and age of the people concerned are the primary basic data on which actions to undertake may be based or forecasted in any given country. Likewise, the population's spatial distribution and migratory movements are the key to land development planning, which is marked by a strong tendency to urbanisation and the upheaval of former land-use schemes.

 The Plan Blue's work in this fundamental field first consisted of building up five scenarios (in 1985-86, publication in 1989) presenting diverse possible demographic futures in the Mediterranean for 2000 and 2025. Then in 1992 a partial update concerning the populations of six southern and eastern countries with forecasts for 2025 was made. Presently (1999-2000), a complete update of demographic data with forecasts to 2025, dealing with each of the Mediterranean countries and their coastal regions has been carried out.

These latter results, which came out in 2000, enable us to note the following (summarising the basic points):

i.      A rapid drop in fertility and considerable slowing down of demographic growth, yet still fairly strong in the South and East, with a stabilisation in the North where certain countries will even experience a slight drop in population;

ii.     A general phenomenon of ageing, affecting all countries with the growth of this older population becoming stronger in the South than in the North;

iii.    A deficit job-market by 2010 in the North but still largely in excess in the South where however a clear-cut drop of net entries onto the job market will be felt beginning in 2015;

iv.     Stabilisation of the urbanisation rate in the North with only some 250,000 additional urban-dwellers per annum, while in the South this rate will see a very high average annual growth rate (more than 2 per cent), which corresponds to some 4 million more urban dwellers every year;

v.     In the Mediterranean coastal regions, the population growth will be three times less rapid between 2000 and 2025 than in the three preceding decades. The coastal urban population will remain pretty much stationary in the North but will increase in the South by about 30 million people in 25 years.

The Blue Plan's up-coming activities concerning population and urbanisation will on the one hand be to use the newly available results in all its work on the development/environment relationships and, on the other, to regularly update the basic demographic data in the Mediterranean as well as a closer analysis of the situation in the Mediterranean coastal regions.

The work on new demographic projections for 2025 is just published by Economica in the "Fascicule du Plan Bleu" Series.
