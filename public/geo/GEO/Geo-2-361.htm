Annual 1997 Temperature and Precipitation in Historical Perspective

National Overview

1997… warm in the west and north but cool in the east. The national average temperature for 1997 (January through December), based on preliminary data, was warmer than normal overall, at 0.7 degree above the long-term average. Spatially-averaged annual temperatures were warmer than normal, in a pattern very similar to that of the autumn just past, providing further evidence of the impacts of the continuing El Nino drama currently playing itself out in the equatorial Pacific. On average, over 90% of the Canadian land area experienced above-normal temperatures in 1997. Annually-averaged temperatures were about a degree warmer than normal in western Canada, from the Pacific Coast to the Prairie Provinces, and about two degrees warmer than normal in the north from northern British Columbia/Yukon Territory to the Mackenzie District of Northwest Territories. Across the Arctic Tundra to Baffin Island in the east, it was about 0.7 degree warmer than normal for the year. In the south and east, from Ontario to the Atlantic Coast annually-averaged temperatures ranged from slightly below normal to about a half a degree below in Atlantic Canada. Nationally, 1997 ranks as the 9th warmest year (42nd coldest) in the 50 years since comparable temperature records began in 1948. A positive national temperature trend of 0.5 degree continues over the 50-year period of analysis. Nationally, temperatures have been above-normal in 7 of the last 10 years (1989, 1990 and 1992 were below), and similarly above in 13 of the last 20 since 1978. The warmest year nationally since 1948 continues to be 1981 (+2.0 degrees) and the coldest continues to be 1972 (-1.8 degrees). The consecutive seasons graph shows that, nationally, so far in the 1990s, temperatures have been warmer than normal in over 60% of all seasons with springs and summers tending to be warm and winters and autumns tending to be cool. Exceptions abound of course, such as for example, the mild autumn of 1994 followed by the warm winter of 1994/95, and the "dreadful" summer of 1992 which will be remembered for its lack of warmth across most of the country.

1997… wet in British Columbia and the north but dry on the prairies and in the east. The national average precipitation for 1997, based on preliminary data, was slightly wetter than normal overall, at about 3% above the long-term average. Spatially averaged annual precipitation was above normal throughout much of Canada (in a little over 50% of the Canadian land area) including the high Arctic Islands, the Yukon Territory, all of British Columbia, and from the Mackenzie Valley to the Ungava Peninsula. The heavy precipitation amounts received along coastal British Columbia in December are consistent with the late autumn-early winter precipitation impacts of the El Nino event. Elsewhere it was drier than normal from the prairies to Ontario and Quebec and to the east coast. Nationally, 1997 ranks as the 14th wettest (37th driest), since comparable precipitation records began in 1948. It should be kept in mind, however, that "normal" precipitation in northern Canada is generally much less than it is in southern Canada, and hence a percentage departure in the north represents much less actual precipitation than the same percentage departure in the south. The national precipitation rankings are therefore often skewed by the northern departures, and do not represent the rankings for the volume of water falling on the country. For the nation as a whole, 8 of the last 10 years have been wetter than normal (1992 and 1995 were drier), and in the past 20 years since 1978, 18 years have all been wetter than normal (exceptions again were 1992 and 1995). On a national basis since 1948, the wettest year continues to be 1996 (+11%) and the driest continues to be 1956 (-7%). Nationally, 1997 continues a period of wetter than normal seasons with nearly 70% of all seasons in the 1990s being wetter than normal as shown in the consecutive seasons graph. During the 1990s Canadian winters have tended to be on the dry side overall, especially in 1993, 1994 and 1995.
