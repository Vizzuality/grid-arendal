The BC Certification Forum
Seeking Peace in the Woods

The forest industry, environmentalists, government, and a variety of other British Columbians are going places in the woods they have never gone before. And the vehicle which they hope to travel in together is a process called "certification."

The intensive 2 and 1/2-day "BC  Forest Certification Forum" wrapped up Saturday, April 1, at Victoria's Laurel Point Inn. It was attended by more than 250 people from throughout BC and elsewhere who often have conflicting interests in the woods. The topic that brought them together was the certification of forest practices and products, providing the opportunity for improved resource stewardship, over and above the BC Forest Practices Code.

The Forum provided an opportunity for constructive dialogue between all participants in the forest sector from consultants, environmentalists, First Nations, forest industry, and government.

Delegates heard that certification is about improving the quality of forest management. Certification is a conservation-based, voluntary, market-driven third party verification process of financially rewarding companies for exemplary forest stewardship. Increasingly, export markets require that BC forest products be certified. In brief, forest companies which log in a way and  at a rate that does not exceed the carrying capacity of the forest to  sustain all forest values, can be rewarded with an eco-certification label on their products.

"With certification, the consumer  becomes a partner in forest management.  The informed buyer can choose to buy a forest product identified by a certified "eco-label" confident that it came from a sustainably managed forest. While a direct connection between the consumer and the producer is not new in other economic sectors such as retail sales and tourism it is new for forestry," said Ray Travers, a  registered professional forester  who is chair of the Victoria-based Ecoforestry Institute, which organized the forum.

Certification, which began as an idea following the 1992 Rio Earth Summit, has now emerged as three more-or-less parallel systems in Canada. These are the ISO (International Organization for Standardization), CSA (Canadian Standards Association) and the FSC (Forest Stewardship Council). The role of these systems is to accredit certifiers such as KPMG, Silva Forest Foundation, Smartwood and Qualifor-SGS - all currently active in BC.  The certifiers are then hired by forest companies who want to be  assessed and if qualified to become "certified." They conduct a detailed review of a company's operations in the woods to see whether its wood products and/or its management system can be certified.

The ISO and CSA tend to be favoured by the Forest Industry and the FSC by the environmental organizations - although some companies support and are testing all three systems. While the certification process does incur some  cost, full cost-recovery over time is possible.

The provincial government (the owner of nearly all BC forest land) which was well represented at the Forum has chosen to primarily take an enabling role.

Forest certification is still very much in the developmental stage, so the Forum was timely. It significantly increased our understanding of what certification is and how it works.
