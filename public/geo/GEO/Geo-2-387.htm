Flood Disaster Averted Again in Siberian City

Written by Stephanie Kriner, Staff Writer, RedCross.org

May 23, 2001 —  Floodwaters that once threatened to destroy a large Siberian city were no match for reinforced dikes Wednesday (May 23) afternoon. For the second day in a row, bombs dropped from helicopters were successful in breaking ice floes that were clogging the Lena River and forcing it to flood Yakutsk.


The Red Cross is supplying food and clothes to flood victims.

With disaster averted, Yakutsk residents who had evacuated or camped out on their roofs for days re-emerged on the streets to enjoy the spring sunshine and enjoy celebratory glasses of vodka. However, because some huge ice blocks continue to dam the river despite all efforts to break them apart, officials warned that villages further downstream still could be flooded.

"There are still very many jams," Tatyana Tarasova, a spokesperson for the regional government told the Associated Press. "It's going to keep flooding and flooding."

Earlier this week as the floods threatened Yakutsk, a city of 200,000 in the eastern Siberian republic of Yakutia, emergency workers used explosives to try to destroy ice jams clogging the Lena River. Meanwhile on land, workers dumped loads of sand to reinforce dikes around the regional capital as waters rose to a record 30 feet and closed in on the city center.

Thousands evacuated the city, threatened this week by two waves of floodwaters. Officials even asked hospital patients who were able to go without care to leave. Others were moved to the hospital's upper floors.

Parts of Yakutsk and outlying districts remain flooded, making thousands homeless. A federal commission is assessing the damage to determine the amount of aid the region will need. Some 4,000 houses will need to be rebuilt.

On Wednesday, the death toll increased to six, with all fatalities occurring in Lensk, about 500 miles southwest of Yakutsk, as people tried to escape the rising waters that have submerged almost the entire city.

Every spring, as Siberia gradually begins to warm up from months of subzero temperatures, frozen rivers thaw. At the same time, snow that blankets the ground throughout most of the year begins to melt, feeding the region's rivers. The result of these two events is annual flooding.

But this winter temperatures hit record lows, plunging to -50 degrees Celsius (-58 degrees Fahrenheit) in a cold spell that lingered for weeks. Some rivers froze solid, resulting in huge blocks of ice that now clog the natural flow. In addition, Siberia is experiencing an unusually warm spring, with snowmelt and seasonal rains pouring into the rivers faster than they can drain.

This year, the snowfall was particularly severe, exacerbating the current problem. With nowhere to go but up, the rivers are rising over their banks and inundating villages, causing Siberia's worst floods in a century.

Since May 13, when the disaster began, the floods have left almost 5,000 homes underwater in 32 towns and villages from the Volga River in European Russia to the Far East. Officials estimate that more than 80,000 people are affected.

On Monday (May 21), Russian President Vladimir Putin appeared on national television to urge people to remain calm. "The consequences are serious. More than 1,000 homes were affected; they were practically destroyed," he said. "We will have to put measures into effect to help people and rebuild their houses."

Russian media reported that most buildings in Lensk, which has a population of 27,000, were submerged. While some 12,000 people have been evacuated by air or boat, many remain stranded on rooftops, waiting for helicopters to arrive and fly them to safety. ITAR-TASS quoted the head of the local government, Vasily Vlasov, as saying that the town would have to be rebuilt elsewhere.

In addition to Yakutia republic, the Irkutsk oblast, where nearly 2,000 homes are flooded, has also been severely affected by the floods. Two cities in Irkutsk have been almost completely evacuated — Kirensk and Ust-Kut. Kirensk was completely underwater and Ust-Kut was 80 percent submerged.

Much of the flooding has occurred in rural areas of the sparsely populated region, making delivery of medical supplies, food and drinking water difficult. In one case, a plane delivering relief supplies to Kirensk had to fly 200 miles to another town. From there, officials had to deliver the goods by truck.

The Russian Red Cross says additional relief supplies are needed for flood victims. The organization is holding clothing and food drives, but reported that more food, water purification filters, medicines, blankets and warm clothes and footwear are needed.

With springtime temperatures still plunging below freezing at night, warm clothes and blankets are particularly important, said Ramsey Rayyis, an American Red Cross delegate in Russia. "It is still extremely cold here," Rayyis said. "The Russian Red Cross is trying to get to the shelters to give the people clothes and blankets so they can keep warm."

The American Red Cross has sent 15 tons of food to the affected area and is donating funds to allow the Russian Red Cross to purchase warm clothes, footwear, blankets and bed sheets for flood victims.

The last major flooding of the Lena River in Siberia occurred in 1998, forcing more than 40,000 people to evacuate.
