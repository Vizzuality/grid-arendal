STATUS of FOREST REGENERATION in CANADA

 

REGEN: A program for reporting on regeneration results

The REGEN component of the National Forestry Database Program was designed to use the best available information. In British Columbia, for example, every harvested area is surveyed after a prescribed number of years to confirm its regeneration status. In many jurisdictions, however, the only available information is the results of sample surveys. From these samples, the forest management agencies can model expected results based on the areas harvested and how they were subsequently treated.

In REGEN, regeneration status is defined in terms of nine classes which relate to the level of stocking of commercial tree species, their distribution and the degree to which they are affected by competing vegetation. The classes are usually aggregated for presentation to non-specialist audiences. The two classes used in the following graphs are defined as:

  • Understocked Disturbed forest land that will require silvicultural treatment to meet stocking standards. This class includes harvested areas that do not contain enough young trees to meet a province's or territory's stocking standards. Natural regeneration could eventually meet stocking standards, but this process would take a long time.
  • Successfully Regenerated Disturbed areas that have regenerated naturally or through planting and seeding. This class includes some recently disturbed areas that are expected to regenerate within an acceptable time without further silvicultural treatment.

Note: The following graphs provide a picture of the status of the regenerating land base resulting from the harvesting and silvicultural activities described earlier in this section.

Regenerating Land Base

This part of Section 6 describes the status of forest regeneration on Crown lands that have been harvested under even-aged management systems between 1975 and 1999. Most Canadian forests are comprised of even-aged stands, and the trees growing in these stands reach maturity at approximately the same time. These tree species are adapted to regenerate after major disturbances, such as fires. Forest management of even-aged forest attempts to mimic this life cycle.

Selection harvesting is commonly used in uneven-aged forest stands. Under these conditions, relatively small openings are created in the forest canopy to maintain an uneven-aged structure with trees of all ages and sizes. Regeneration is continuous in uneven-aged stands - the stands are not removed and replaced like even-aged stands - and so areas affected by selection harvesting are not part of the land base described here.

Figure 28 illustrates the regenerating land base described above. The areas shown on this graph are cumulative areas. For instance, the area shown for 1975 represents the area harvested in 1975. The area for 1976 represents the area harvested up to and including that year (1975 plus 1976), and so on. The regenerating land base amounted to 16.3 million hectares in 1999.

The regenerating land base does not include non-production areas, most of which consist of roads for access to forest resources, that have no timber production objective.

Annual Harvest

The silvicultural systems used in managing even-aged forests for timber production are clearcut, seed-tree and shelterwood harvesting. Clearcutting is the most widely used silvicultural system in Canada (see Figure 18). Selection harvesting (uneven-aged management) accounted for about 9% of the area harvested in Canada in 1999, about double the percentage in 1975.  Commercial thinning accounted for about 2% of the area harvested in 1999.

While clearcutting will likely remain predominant in Canada for the foreseeable future, harvesting techniques are changing. In Ontario, for example, increasing use is made of "careful logging around advanced growth." In Quebec, "cutting with protection of regeneration and soils" was mandated by the Forest Act. In 1987, this technique accounted for 22% of the area harvested under even-aged management on Crown land when the new act came into force, and 81% in 1999.


Regeneration Treatments

Since the 1980s, most jurisdictions have passed legislation or signed agreements that require logging companies to ensure regeneration on sites they harvest. The foresters' options include selection cutting in forest types that are suited to uneven-aged management, clearcutting and scarifying to promote natural regeneration, or adopting a modified harvesting method that will protect advance regeneration. Clearcuts may also be regenerated by planting or seeding.

Provincial planting and seeding programs were stepped up substantially in the 1980s under the Federal/Provincial Forest Resource Development Agreements (or FRDAs) and through new legislation which enforced better regeneration practices. By the early 1990s, planting programs had largely eliminated the backlog of treatable understocked sites, and several provinces began scaling back their planting programs (see Figure 18).  British Columbia accounted for 53% of the planting on Crown land in 1994, but scaled back to 42% in 1999.  Ontario accounted for 22% of the planting on Crown land in 1999.

Today, forest managers rely on advance regeneration and appropriate regeneration techniques to ensure that the majority of harvested areas will regenerate naturally. The remaining sites are regenerated by planting or seeding. Additional seedlings can be planted on sites where existing regeneration fails to meet stocking standards.


Status of Forest Regeneration

Figure 29 illustrates the status of the regenerating land base as it evolved between 1975 and 1998. Again, this figure shows the cumulative areas of successfully regenerated and understocked forest lands. The graph presents the status each year of the cumulative area harvested since 1975. Although the annual increase in understocked area is small, the total that has accumulated since 1975 is substantial. By 1992, it amounted to almost 2.7 million hectares. The most recent data indicate that the cumulative understocked area has been shrinking since 1993.

A significant proportion of recently harvested areas will always be reported as understocked because of the time lag between harvesting and observable results of subsequent treatments, such as planting, or natural forest stand development. The lag is evident in Figure 30, which illustrates the regeneration status in 1999 of the area harvested each year since 1975. The area that is reported as understocked for status year 1999 is equivalent to about 80% of the three years' harvest from 1997 to 1999.
